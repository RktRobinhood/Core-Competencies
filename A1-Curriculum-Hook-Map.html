    Appendix A1 — Curriculum Hook Map: TOK and Competency Development

    ◉ Overview

    This appendix provides a practical curriculum map showing how the competencies developed throughout this course directly support TOK assessment objectives and knowledge questions. The map is designed to help TOK teachers and curriculum leaders see that competency development is not external to TOK; it is central to achieving TOK aims. The map also provides ready-to-use knowledge question stems that anchor TOK discussions in competency development.

    Why This Map Matters

    TOK is assessed through two components: an exhibition (internal, 33% of grade) and a 1,600-word essay (external, 67% of grade). Both require students to demonstrate critical reflection on the nature of knowledge. But what is critical reflection? It requires the competencies developed throughout this course: critical thinking, metacognition, perspective-taking, and communication. By explicitly mapping competencies to TOK assessment, teachers can show students that competency work is not preparation for TOK; it is TOK.

    Direct Mapping: TOK Assessment Objectives and Competencies

    TOK Assessment Objective Definition Core Competencies Required How Competencies Support This Objective
    Critical examination of knowledge questions Students respond to a TOK knowledge question (KQ) that asks about the nature, justification, or limits of knowledge Critical thinking, Metacognition To examine a KQ critically, students must think about their own assumptions (metacognition) and evaluate claims with evidence and alternative perspectives (critical thinking).
    Links between KQs and the world around us Students connect abstract knowledge questions to concrete, real-world situations and examples Critical thinking, Information literacy, Perspective-taking Students must identify real-world examples, understand the context, evaluate different viewpoints, and see connections between abstract theory and concrete instances—all requiring these competencies.
    Links between KQs and Areas of Knowledge (AOKs) Students show how the same KQ manifests differently across AOKs (e.g., how standards of justification differ in Natural Sciences vs History) Comparative thinking, Perspective-taking, Systems thinking Understanding AOK differences requires comparing methods and standards systematically (comparative thinking), appreciating why different methods make sense in different contexts (perspective-taking), and seeing how AOKs are part of a larger knowledge landscape (systems thinking).
    Relevant and coherent arguments Students construct clear claims, provide counterclaims, and evaluate both with nuance Critical thinking, Metacognition, Communication Building an argument requires thinking clearly about your position, anticipating and fairly representing opposing views, and explaining your reasoning—directly requiring these competencies.
    Effective examples and evidence Students distinguish anecdote from expert claim from empirical evidence, and use examples that genuinely support the argument Information literacy, Critical thinking, Metacognition Students must evaluate sources (information literacy), assess the strength of evidence (critical thinking), and understand what kinds of evidence are appropriate for what claims (metacognition about knowledge methods).
    Different points of view Students acknowledge and represent viewpoints different from their own Perspective-taking, Communication This objective requires genuine engagement with perspectives that challenge your own—a competency that develops through deliberate practice, not passive exposure.
    Implications of arguments Students explain what follows from their argument: what becomes possible, what becomes problematic, what questions remain Critical thinking, Metacognition, Perspective-taking Thinking about implications requires imagining consequences, understanding stakeholder perspectives, and assessing uncertainty—all demanding competencies.

    Optional TOK Themes and Competency Connections

    TOK Theme Core Questions Competencies Most Directly Involved
    Knowledge and the knower How does my identity, perspective, and values shape what and how I know? To what extent can I transcend my perspective? Metacognition, Perspective-taking, Self-regulation, Critical thinking
    Knowledge and technology How do tools (AI, Google, Wikipedia, calculators) change what it means to know something? When does technology enhance knowledge and when does it replace it? Information literacy, Metacognition, Critical thinking, AI literacy
    Knowledge and language How do words constrain and enable knowledge? Can something be known that cannot be expressed in language? Communication, Metacognition, Perspective-taking
    Knowledge and indigenous societies What knowledge is visible in mainstream academic systems and what is invisible or marginalized? Whose epistemology counts? Perspective-taking, Critical thinking, Cultural awareness
    Knowledge and politics How do power and interest shape knowledge production and what counts as knowledge? Critical thinking, Perspective-taking, Systems thinking

    Knowledge Question Stems: Ready to Use

    The following knowledge question stems can be used directly in TOK lessons. They are framed around competency development and can anchor discussions, exhibitions, and essays.

    KQs About Critical Thinking and Knowledge

    1. To what extent does using AI tools change what it means to "know" something?
    2. This KQ invites reflection on whether knowledge is justified true belief, or whether outsourcing to tools changes the nature of knowledge.

    3. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, information literacy

    4. When does outsourcing cognition to technology improve knowledge, and when does it undermine it?

    5. This KQ asks students to think comparatively about what should and should not be outsourced, with what consequences.

    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, perspective-taking

    7. How should we judge knowledge claims when fluent language is cheap to produce?

    8. This KQ addresses the challenge of evaluating AI-generated content and distinguishing language quality from knowledge quality.
    9. Competency focus: Information literacy, critical thinking, metacognition

    KQs About Knowledge Responsibility and Ethics

    1. Are there ethical limits on what knowledge should be produced or applied through AI systems?
    2. This KQ invites discussion of dual-use knowledge, environmental costs, consent, and responsibility.

    3. Competency focus: Ethical reasoning, critical thinking, perspective-taking

    4. Whose knowledge is amplified or marginalized when AI systems mediate inquiry?

    5. This KQ addresses whose perspectives are represented in training data and algorithmic design, connecting to power and justice.
    6. Competency focus: Perspective-taking, critical thinking, cultural awareness

    KQs About Metacognition and Knowledge

    1. How does awareness of my own thinking change what and how I learn?
    2. This KQ asks students to reflect on the relationship between metacognition and knowledge.

    3. Competency focus: Metacognition, critical thinking, self-regulation

    4. What standards of justification do different Areas of Knowledge use, and why?

    5. This KQ invites systematic comparison of how Natural Sciences, History, Mathematics, and Arts justify claims differently.
    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, comparative thinking, systems thinking

    KQs About Culture, Identity, and Knowledge

    1. To what extent is an individual a representative of their culture, and how does this shape what they know?
    2. This KQ invites students to explore the relationship between personal identity and cultural knowledge — whether culture exists only in the individuals who carry it, and what happens to cultural knowledge when individuals change. It connects to the dannelse concept of cultural formation (see Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation) and to CAS projects where students act as representatives of their communities.

    3. Competency focus: Perspective-taking, metacognition, cultural awareness, critical thinking

    4. If no single person embodies their entire culture, how can we claim to "know" a culture at all?

    5. This KQ addresses the epistemological challenge of cultural knowledge: culture is distributed across individuals who each carry only fragments. It raises questions about generalisation, stereotyping, and the limits of ethnographic knowledge.
    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, perspective-taking, ethical reasoning

    KQs About Attention, Technology, and Knowledge

    1. To what extent does our inability to sit with our own thoughts represent a knowledge problem?
    2. This KQ draws on the Wilson et al. (2014) self-shocking study (see Ch-03-Skills-at-Risk-in-a-Digital-Age) and the boiling frog parable. If people prefer electric shocks to solitary reflection, what does this imply about the kind of knowledge we can produce? Deep thinking requires sustained attention; if that capacity is eroding, certain forms of knowledge may become inaccessible. Connects to metacognition (Ch-15) and productive struggle (Ch-17).

    3. Competency focus: Metacognition, critical thinking, self-regulation

    4. How do gradual changes to our cognitive habits affect what we can know?

    5. This KQ applies the boiling frog principle to epistemology. If attention spans, reading depth, and tolerance for ambiguity are declining incrementally, the changes may be invisible to those experiencing them — yet they reshape the kinds of knowledge that individuals and societies can produce. It invites students to consider whether there are forms of knowledge that require cognitive capacities we are losing.
    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, perspective-taking, systems thinking

    KQs About Agency, Self-Belief, and Knowledge

    1. To what extent does believing you can learn something change whether you actually learn it?
    2. This KQ explores the relationship between self-efficacy and knowledge acquisition — whether belief is merely motivational or whether it constitutes a genuine epistemic condition. It connects to Bandura's self-efficacy research (see Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties), Dweck's growth mindset framework (see Ch-16-Growth-Mindset), and the self-rescue principle.

    3. Competency focus: Metacognition, self-regulation, critical thinking

    4. If "no one is coming to save you" in your learning, what role do teachers, peers, and institutions actually play in knowledge production?

    5. This KQ asks students to examine the tension between radical self-responsibility and the social nature of knowledge. It invites reflection on whether knowledge can be produced in isolation or whether it always requires community, even when the learner takes full ownership of the process.
    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, collaboration, perspective-taking, ethical reasoning

    KQs About Collaboration and Knowledge

    1. How does collaboration change the nature of knowledge produced?
    2. This KQ asks whether knowledge produced collaboratively is different from individual knowledge.

    3. Competency focus: Collaboration, perspective-taking, communication

    4. When does consensus indicate truth, and when does it indicate power?

    5. This KQ addresses how groups reach agreement and what that agreement means.
    6. Competency focus: Critical thinking, perspective-taking, communication

    KQs About Feedback Cycles, Visibility, and Self-Knowledge

    1. To what extent does making knowledge public change its reliability?
    2. This KQ explores how visibility and accountability affect the quality of self-knowledge and commitment. When students journal privately versus publicly, does the knowledge they generate about themselves differ? Does the act of declaring a goal to others change the knower's relationship to that knowledge?
    3. Competency focus: Metacognition, self-regulation, critical thinking

    4. Connected chapters: Ch-19 (virtuous and vicious feedback cycles, cognitive dissonance), Ch-20 (documented transformation, Stoic journaling), Ch-28 (reflective practice)

    5. Can a person know they are in a vicious cycle while being unable to escape it? What does this imply about the relationship between knowledge and action?

    6. This KQ addresses the gap between knowing and doing — a central tension in epistemology. Tim Urban's procrastination framework suggests that awareness alone is insufficient without structural support. What kinds of knowledge are necessary but not sufficient for behavioural change?
    7. Competency focus: Metacognition, self-regulation, critical thinking
    8. Connected chapters: Ch-19 (procrastination spiral, persuasion toolkit), Ch-15 (metacognition), Ch-17 (productive struggle)

    KQs About Attention, Failure, and Emotional Knowledge

    1. If attention is a finite cognitive resource, to what extent can a person be said to "know" something they were not attending to when they encountered it?
    2. This KQ examines the relationship between attention and knowledge acquisition. If a student reads a chapter while intermittently checking their phone, does the knowledge they form differ in kind from knowledge formed under sustained attention? What does this imply about the conditions necessary for genuine understanding?
    3. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, self-regulation

    4. Connected chapters: Ch-14 (science of attention, multitasking myth), Ch-12 (deep processing), Ch-15 (metacognitive monitoring)

    5. Is failure a source of knowledge, an obstacle to knowledge, or both? Under what conditions does experiencing failure produce understanding that success cannot?

    6. This KQ asks students to examine the epistemological status of failure. Scientists often learn more from failed experiments than from successful ones. Does the same apply to personal knowledge? If so, what distinguishes "productive" failure from failure that teaches nothing?
    7. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, resilience

    8. Connected chapters: Ch-17 (value of failure, productive failure), Ch-20 (resilience), Ch-16 (growth mindset)

    9. Can emotional states constitute a form of knowledge, or are they merely influences on knowledge? When a person changes their emotional state "on purpose," are they changing what they know?

    10. This KQ explores the relationship between emotion and knowing, drawing on the strategic recipe concept. If a cold shower or a piece of music can shift a person from despair to determination, has the world changed or has the knower changed? What does deliberate emotional regulation reveal about the nature of subjective knowledge?
    11. Competency focus: Metacognition, critical thinking, self-regulation
    12. Connected chapters: Ch-20 (strategic recipes, stress mindset, breathing exercises), Ch-15 (metacognitive awareness)

    KQs About Formation, Culture, and Collective Practice

    1. To what extent can character be formed through collective practice, and at what point does formation become coercion?
    2. This KQ asks students to examine the boundary between education and indoctrination. China's synchronised morning exercises and military training programmes deliberately shape character through embodied collective ritual — but so do school assemblies, sports teams, and national anthems. What distinguishes formation from manipulation, and who decides?
    3. Competency focus: Critical thinking, perspective-taking, ethical reasoning

    4. Connected chapters: Ch-29 (dannelse and character formation, China comparative case study), Ch-19 (autonomy and motivation)

    5. If culture exists only in the individuals who carry it, can a state legitimately use education to define what that culture should be?

    6. This KQ extends the cultural formation concept from Ch-29 into a political epistemology question. China's suzhi jiaoyu and Denmark's dannelse both claim to develop the "whole person" — but they define wholeness differently and enforce it through different mechanisms. What gives any institution the authority to define what a well-formed person looks like?
    7. Competency focus: Critical thinking, perspective-taking, ethical reasoning, cultural awareness

    8. Connected chapters: Ch-29 (dannelse, suzhi jiaoyu, Confucian formation), Ch-08 (global competence)

    9. How do we know whether a competency-based education programme is working — and whose definition of "working" counts?

    10. This KQ connects evaluation methodology to epistemology. Measuring critical thinking or collaboration requires definitions that are themselves value-laden. When an implementation team tracks "fidelity," they are measuring adherence to a chosen model — but what if the model is wrong? This raises questions about the relationship between measurement, knowledge, and power.
    11. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, systems thinking

    12. Connected chapters: Ch-33 (evaluation and metrics), Ch-25 (assessment literacy)

    13. Does understanding compound interest change what you know, or only what you can calculate? What is the difference between mathematical knowledge and mathematical understanding?

    14. This KQ draws on the finding that financial literacy education explains only 0.1% of the variance in financial behaviour. If knowing the mathematics of compound interest fails to change behaviour, is it really knowledge? Or is genuine mathematical understanding something deeper — an intuitive grasp that shapes perception and decision-making, not merely a computational ability?
    15. Competency focus: Critical thinking, metacognition, self-regulation
    16. Connected chapters: Ch-03 (compound interest and the compounding gap), Ch-12 (memory and levels of processing), Ch-15 (metacognition)

    Pedagogical Section: Using This Map in TOK Teaching

    For lesson planning: Before designing a lesson on a specific KQ, consult this map. Which competencies does that KQ require students to develop? Which competencies are already strong in your class, and which need more attention? Use your lesson to both answer the KQ and develop the competencies.

    For student assessment: When assessing TOK work (exhibition or essay), use the competency lens alongside the TOK rubric. A student who develops a strong competency (e.g., genuine perspective-taking, sophisticated critical thinking) will likely demonstrate it in their TOK work. Conversely, competency gaps will show up as weaknesses in TOK performance.

    For curriculum integration: If your school is implementing the competency development framework throughout the curriculum, TOK becomes a natural venue for students to apply and reflect on the competencies they are developing in other subjects. A student developing critical thinking in Science, Collaboration in Group Projects, and Communication in Language classes will naturally bring those developing competencies to TOK.

    For student scaffolding: Use the knowledge question stems provided above to launch TOK discussions. They are framed around competency development, so they naturally engage students in the competency work while anchoring them in TOK content and assessment objectives.

    Connection to TOK Assessment Rubric

    The IB's official TOK assessment rubrics evaluate seven criteria. Here is how competency development supports each:

    TOK Assessment Criterion How Competencies Support It
    Knowledge question focus Critical thinking (evaluating whether the KQ is knowledge-related), metacognition (understanding what makes a good KQ)
    Contextual awareness Perspective-taking (understanding diverse contexts), information literacy (researching contexts thoroughly)
    Knowledge analysis Critical thinking (analyzing how knowledge claims are justified), metacognition (understanding methods and standards)
    Perspectives Perspective-taking (understanding why different viewpoints exist), communication (representing them fairly)
    Use of examples Information literacy (finding good examples), critical thinking (evaluating whether examples support the claim)
    Argument structure and coherence Critical thinking (developing clear, coherent reasoning), communication (expressing it clearly)
    Engaging with complexity and ambiguity Metacognition (recognizing limits of knowledge), critical thinking (holding multiple perspectives), perspective-taking (understanding different positions)
