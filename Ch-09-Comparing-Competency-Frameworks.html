    Chapter 9 — Comparing Competency Frameworks: Mapping an Integrated Approach

    ◉ Overview

    A wealth of competency frameworks exist, each offering a map of essential skills and capacities for contemporary life. The Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21), the International Baccalaureate's Approaches to Learning (ATL), the OECD Learning Compass 2030, and the Danish tradition of dannelse each define competencies through different lenses. Understanding their overlaps, distinctions, and potential integration is essential for schools designing coherent approaches to competency development. This final chapter in Part II maps these frameworks against each other and considers how to synthesize them into a unified vision.

    Overview of Major Frameworks

    Before comparing frameworks, it is useful to understand each one's origins, scope, and philosophy.

    The Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21)

    The P21 framework (Battelle for Kids, 2019), developed by Battelle for Kids and widely adopted in the United States and internationally, organizes competencies into four main categories. The Learning and Innovation skills are the "4Cs": Critical Thinking, Communication, Collaboration, and Creativity. The Information, Media, and Technology skills address information literacy, media literacy, and ICT literacy. The Life and Career Skills include flexibility, initiative, self-direction, social and cross-cultural skills, productivity, and accountability. The framework is comprehensive, widely recognized, and has influenced policy in many countries. Its strength is coherence and accessibility; its limitation is that it was developed largely with economic productivity in view, and it is vague about how schools should implement these competencies.

    Approaches to Learning (ATL) in the IB

    The International Baccalaureate's ATL framework (IBO, 2015), introduced across all IB programmes (PYP, MYP, DP, CP), organizes skills into five categories. Communication Skills encompass reading, writing, language use, and negotiation of ideas. Social (Collaboration) Skills address working cooperatively, building consensus, managing conflict, and giving/receiving feedback. Self-Management Skills include organization, affective skills (mindfulness, resilience, growth mindset), and reflection on learning. Research Skills encompass information literacy and media literacy. Thinking Skills address critical and creative thinking as well as transfer (applying learning across contexts).

    The ATL framework is embedded within IB curricula and designed for explicit teaching and assessment within subjects. Its strength is that it provides practical language and rubrics; its limitation is that it is specific to IB schools and is less well-researched than some alternatives.

    OECD Learning Compass 2030

    The OECD framework (OECD, 2019), developed through extensive consultation and research, organizes learning differently. Rather than listing discrete skills, the OECD identifies transformative competencies that help individuals address complex global challenges: Creating new value (combining disciplinary knowledge with creativity, entrepreneurship, and problem-solving); Reconciling tensions (balancing individual and collective interests, short-term and long-term needs); and Taking responsibility (participating in and shaping inclusive communities). Underlying these transformative competencies is student agency—the capacity to set goals, reflect, and act responsibly.

    The OECD framework is research-driven and forward-looking, emphasizing how competencies enable students to shape their futures responsibly. Its limitation is that it is somewhat abstract and may be harder for teachers to operationalise than more concrete frameworks.

    The Danish Dannelse Tradition

    The Danish tradition, rooted in educational philosophy dating to the 19th century, understands education as human formation (dannelse) rather than mere skill acquisition. The 2014 Folkeskole reform (Danish Ministry of Education, 2014) identified core areas including kritisk tænkning (critical thinking), kreativitet (creativity), kommunikation (communication), samarbejde (collaboration), ansvar for egen læring (responsibility for one's own learning), informationskompetence (information competence), and demokratisk dannelse (democratic character formation).

    Crucially, the Danish approach embeds competencies within a philosophical framework emphasizing human flourishing, democratic citizenship, and personal responsibility. Competencies are not merely functional skills to be acquired but practices and virtues to be developed as expressions of human dignity. This philosophical grounding is distinctive and influential; its limitation is that it is culturally rooted and not easily transferred to other contexts.

    Mapping Frameworks Against Core Competencies

    The five competencies addressed in this Part—critical thinking, collaboration, information and digital literacy, self-directed learning, and leadership with global competence—appear across all frameworks but with different emphases and terminology. Constructing a simple mapping reveals both convergence and divergence.

    Areas of Remarkable Convergence

    What strikes one on examining these frameworks is how much agreement exists despite their different origins and philosophies.

    All frameworks prioritize critical and creative thinking. Every framework recognizes that students must think carefully and imaginatively. Whether called "critical thinking," "thinking skills," or "creating new value," this capacity is non-negotiable across frameworks.

    All frameworks emphasize communication and collaboration. Students must work together and communicate effectively. The terminology varies—P21's "4Cs," ATL's "Communication and Social Skills," OECD's embedding in transformative competencies, Danish samarbejde—but the recognition is universal.

    All frameworks include learning-to-learn capacities. Under various names—"Self-Management," "Responsibility for One's Own Learning," "Student Agency"—all frameworks recognize that students must develop capacity to direct and regulate their own learning.

    All frameworks address information and knowledge work. In an age of information abundance, all frameworks recognize that information literacy—finding, evaluating, and using information—is essential.

    All frameworks extend beyond academic skills to include personal and social dimensions. P21's "Life and Career Skills," ATL's affective dimensions of self-management, OECD's emphasis on agency and responsibility, and Danish dannelse's focus on character and citizenship all recognize that competencies include how students manage themselves, relate to others, and participate in communities.

    This convergence is reassuring. Despite different origins, frameworks developed in different countries for different systems agree on what matters. This suggests these competencies are robust, reflecting genuine requirements of contemporary life.

    Areas of Meaningful Divergence

    Yet frameworks also diverge in important ways.

    Philosophical grounding differs markedly. The P21 and ATL frameworks are largely instrumental—they ask "What skills do students need to succeed?" (in college, careers, contemporary contexts). The OECD framework adds a moral dimension, asking "What competencies enable students to address global challenges and build better futures?" The Danish dannelse tradition goes further, asking "What does human flourishing look like? What kind of people should education help develop?" These are not merely semantic differences; they lead to different priorities and different implementations.

    Character and well-being are explicitly included in Danish and OECD frameworks but largely absent from P21 and ATL. While P21 includes "Life and Career Skills," it focuses more on career readiness than character formation. The Danish and OECD frameworks more explicitly address human flourishing, well-being, and the virtues that sustain ethical life. This difference matters: it shapes whether schools see competencies as mainly about college and career preparation or about developing flourishing human beings.

    Cultural competence and global citizenship receive different emphasis. The IB framework, designed for international contexts, emphasizes intercultural understanding more explicitly. The OECD emphasizes "reconciling tensions" and global challenges. The Danish framework emphasizes democratic participation and global awareness. P21, while including "cross-cultural skills," is less explicit about global citizenship. These differences reflect different design contexts: IB serves international student populations; the OECD addresses global challenges; Denmark emphasizes democratic values.

    Implementation specificity varies considerably. P21 is a framework without detailed implementation guidance—schools must determine how to teach these competencies. ATL, embedded in IB curriculum documentation, provides greater implementation specificity through subject guides and assessment rubrics. The OECD provides research but not detailed curriculum guidance. The Danish approach, embedded in the Folkeskole tradition and current education reform, has centuries of practice and institutional structures supporting implementation.

    ATL as a Comprehensive Competency Framework

    A closer look at the IB's Approaches to Learning reveals it as a particularly comprehensive framework that explicitly integrates all the competencies addressed in this Part.

    Thinking Skills directly address critical thinking and creativity, including the capacity to transfer learning across contexts. Research Skills encompass information and digital literacy, including the evaluation of sources and media literacy. Self-Management Skills address self-directed learning through organization, affective skills (resilience, growth mindset), and reflection. Communication Skills address both communication within collaboration and the advanced communication dimensions discussed in Chapter 8. Social Skills directly address collaboration and include elements of leadership through distributed leadership practices.

    The ATL framework is particularly useful because it is designed to be embedded within subject teaching rather than taught separately. Teachers in mathematics, history, literature, science, and other subjects all develop ATL skills as they teach content. This embedding is important: research on critical thinking and metacognition consistently shows that skills taught in isolation transfer poorly, while skills taught and practiced within disciplinary contexts transfer more readily.

    However, ATL implementation faces challenges that research has identified. Many teachers assume that ATL skills will develop through exposure to rigorous curriculum without explicit instruction. Research on critical thinking, metacognition, and collaborative learning consistently shows this assumption is unfounded; skills must be explicitly taught, modelled, and scaffolded. Without whole-school approaches to ATL integration, without vertical articulation of skill development across grades, and without professional development in how to teach learning skills, ATL can remain a bureaucratic requirement rather than a genuine learning driver.

    Integrating Frameworks: Toward a Coherent Vision

    Rather than choosing one framework, the most effective approach integrates the strengths of multiple frameworks.

    One can organize around P21's practical skill categories (Critical Thinking, Communication, Collaboration, Creativity, and extending to information literacy and self-direction), which are clear and widely understood. One can incorporate ATL's five-category structure (Thinking, Communication, Social, Self-Management, Research), which is more comprehensive and better operationalized for embedded teaching. One can integrate OECD's emphasis on agency and transformative competencies, ensuring that skill development is oriented toward authentic, meaningful purposes. And one can ground the entire approach in Danish dannelse philosophy, ensuring that competencies are developed not merely as functional skills but as expressions of human dignity, democratic citizenship, and personal flourishing.

    Such an integrated approach would ensure that:

    • Critical thinking is explicitly taught and embedded in every subject, not assumed to develop naturally
    • Communication is developed in diverse forms (written, oral, visual, digital) and within authentic contexts
    • Collaboration is structured with positive interdependence, individual accountability, and explicit teaching of social skills
    • Information and digital literacy are developed through authentic research tasks and media analysis
    • Self-directed learning is supported through explicit instruction in metacognitive strategies and gradual release of responsibility
    • Leadership and global competence are developed through opportunities for distributed leadership and engagement with diverse perspectives
    • Reflection is built into regular routines, helping students develop metacognitive awareness of their own learning and growth

    This requires more than adopting a framework; it requires coherent curriculum design, explicit instruction, assessment aligned with competency development, and professional development for teachers. Schools that achieve this integration report stronger student outcomes not only in competencies but in academic achievement as well.

    ℹ Key Concept — Framework Integration as a School Design Problem

    Successfully integrating competency frameworks is ultimately a school design problem, not just a curriculum problem. It requires alignment between learning goals, instruction, assessment, and school culture. It requires that all teachers see competency development as part of their role, not an add-on. It requires time for professional collaboration, shared language, and continuous improvement. Schools that approach framework integration as a whole-school initiative sustained over years see better results than schools that add frameworks on top of existing structures without rethinking them.

    Assessment of Competencies

    One final important consideration is assessment. How schools assess competencies shapes what gets taught and how seriously competencies are taken. Yet assessing competencies is genuinely difficult.

    Content assessment is relatively straightforward: Does the student understand this concept? Can they solve this problem? Competencies are harder to assess because they are multidimensional and context-dependent. A student might demonstrate critical thinking in evaluating a historical source but less capability in evaluating a scientific claim. A student might collaborate beautifully in one group context but struggle in another.

    Effective competency assessment typically involves multiple methods:

    • Rubrics that describe developing, proficient, and advanced performance on competencies, used for both teacher and student evaluation
    • Portfolios of evidence where students collect examples of their competency development over time
    • Formative assessment embedded in daily teaching—classroom observation, exit tickets, peer feedback—rather than relying on summative tests
    • Self-assessment where students evaluate their own growth on competencies
    • Triangulation where competency is judged from multiple sources rather than a single test

    The OECD and ATL frameworks both provide rubric examples. Danish schools often use portfolios and student self-assessment aligned with dannelse values. P21 has developed some assessment resources but less systematically than other frameworks.

    » In Practice

    A school might assess critical thinking through rubrics used in subject classrooms (a history essay on source evaluation, a science report on interpreting data), through observation of student reasoning in classroom discussion, through student self-assessment against criteria ("Can I analyze an argument? Can I identify assumptions? Can I consider multiple perspectives?"), and through portfolio evidence collected across the year. Rather than a single critical thinking test, multiple forms of evidence paint a picture of the student's developing competence.

    The IB's Competencies of the Future Research Initiative

    In 2024, the IB launched a significant research initiative titled Competencies of the Future, commissioning the Center for Assessment (NCIEA) to conduct a series of literature reviews examining competencies that students will need to flourish in a rapidly changing world. The initiative is explicitly framed around developing uniquely human qualities unlikely to be replaced by technology — a framing that resonates powerfully with the argument developed throughout this textbook and explored in depth in Ch-35-Being-More-Human-in-an-AI-Age.

    The initiative identifies six competency domains for investigation: analytical thinking, creative thinking, critical thinking, ethical thinking, intercultural understanding, and student agency. For each domain, the Center for Assessment has produced research reviews that synthesise what is currently known about the competency, how it develops, and how it can be assessed. Several of these reviews were published in 2024, with the programme expanding to six thematic concepts: Change, Connections, Identities, Perspectives, Systems, and Well-being (IBO, 2024).

    ℹ Key Concept — IB Competencies of the Future

    The IB's Competencies of the Future research programme identifies six domains — analytical thinking, creative thinking, critical thinking, ethical thinking, intercultural understanding, and student agency — as essential for students to flourish in a world shaped by technological disruption. Each domain is grounded in a literature review that synthesises research on what the competency is, how it develops, and how it can be measured (IBO, 2024; NCIEA, 2024).

    Several features of this initiative are worth noting for our purposes. First, the emphasis on intercultural understanding as a standalone competency reflects the IB's international character. The 2024 literature review synthesises over 30 research-based definitions to arrive at a working definition centred on the knowledge and appreciation of cultural similarities and differences, and the skill of reflecting on one's own culture in relation to others. This connects directly to the global competence dimension explored in Ch-08-Leadership-Communication-and-Global-Competence and to the Danish concept of demokratisk dannelse examined in Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation.

    Second, the inclusion of ethical thinking as a distinct competency goes beyond what most frameworks address. While P21 and ATL embed ethical considerations within other categories, the IB's explicit naming of ethical thinking as a competency in its own right signals an alignment with the dannelse tradition's insistence that education is about character formation, not merely skill acquisition.

    Third, the initiative's emphasis on student agency — the capacity to set goals, reflect on progress, and take responsible action — echoes the OECD Learning Compass 2030 framework's central concept of agency and connects to this textbook's philosophy of the self-directed learner (Ch-07-Self-Directed-Learning) and the principle that every student is a teacher (Ch-18-Learning-to-Learn).

    The research also addresses design thinking and computational thinking as cross-programme competencies, with a 2020 audit examining how these are fostered across PYP, MYP, and DP subjects. A parallel study on metacognitive skills investigated how metacognition is addressed within and across IB programmes — a finding directly relevant to the argument developed in Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation that metacognition must be explicitly taught rather than assumed.

    » In Practice — Connecting IB Competencies to TOK

    The six competency domains map naturally onto TOK assessment objectives and classroom activities. Analytical thinking and critical thinking underpin the evaluation of knowledge claims. Creative thinking enables students to generate diverse perspectives on prescribed titles. Ethical thinking grounds knowledge questions in real-world stakes. Intercultural understanding enriches the exploration of shared and personal knowledge across cultural contexts. And student agency drives the self-directed inquiry that the expanded 150-hour programme is designed to support.

    For schools implementing the expanded TOK curriculum, this initiative provides an evidence-based bridge between the IB's institutional framework and the broader competency research examined throughout this Part. The Competencies of the Future research validates the direction this textbook takes: competencies are not supplementary to academic education but are its essential purpose.

    Implications for the IB TOK Expansion

    As a Danish school implements an IB TOK programme expanded from 100 to 150 hours, the opportunity arises to integrate these competency frameworks coherently. The expansion creates space not only for deeper engagement with epistemological questions (traditional TOK content) but also for systematic development of the core competencies that this Part has addressed.

    An expanded TOK curriculum can:

    • Integrate critical thinking explicitly by examining how different disciplines approach evidence, claim-making, and argumentation
    • Develop collaboration skills through structured debates, group inquiry projects, and peer learning
    • Build information and digital literacy by examining sources of knowledge, evaluating claims in context, and understanding media and technology
    • Support self-directed learning by helping students pursue knowledge questions that matter to them, with metacognitive reflection
    • Develop leadership and communication through student-led discussions, presentations, and the kind of dialogue that characterizes TOK classrooms
    • Ground everything in the dannelse tradition by connecting epistemological questions to questions of human flourishing and responsible citizenship

    The IB and Danish frameworks are complementary. The IB's emphasis on questioning knowledge, understanding multiple perspectives, and developing international-mindedness aligns with Danish values of critical thinking and global awareness. The Danish emphasis on character formation and democratic participation can deepen and ground the IB's framework. An expanded TOK course that explicitly integrates these frameworks can develop not only students' thinking about knowledge but their capacity to think, collaborate, learn, lead, and engage responsibly with an interconnected world.
