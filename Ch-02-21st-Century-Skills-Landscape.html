    Chapter 2 — Mapping the 21st-Century Skills Landscape: What the Research Converges On

    ◉ Overview

    Multiple international frameworks have attempted to define the competencies essential for success in the 21st century. Despite different origins and emphases, they converge on four core competency areas—critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity—along with domains of information and digital literacy, and life/career skills. This chapter maps these frameworks, reveals their convergence, addresses their limitations, and explains how they connect to Danish educational values.

    Why Frameworks Matter

    In the absence of a shared language and framework for competencies, each school is left to invent its own understanding of what "critical thinking" or "collaboration" means. Teachers in different departments may have entirely different conceptions of these skills. Students may develop them inconsistently across subjects. The result is often well-intentioned but fragmented efforts that lack coherence or depth.

    The major international frameworks—developed by researchers, educators, and organizations across different countries and contexts—provide a common vocabulary and a synthesis of research. They answer three crucial questions: What competencies matter? How are they defined? What distinguishes good performance in these areas from poor performance?

    Four frameworks deserve detailed attention: the Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21), the Assessment and Teaching of 21st Century Skills (ATC21S), the OECD Learning Compass 2030, and the IB Approaches to Learning (ATL). A Danish school implementing a competency curriculum should understand what these frameworks agree on, where they differ, and how to integrate their insights.

    The Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21)

    The P21 framework, developed by Battelle for Kids with input from educators and business leaders, organises student outcomes into three interconnected domains (Battelle for Kids, 2019). This structure has influenced curricula and competency initiatives worldwide.

    The first domain is Learning and Innovation Skills—the 4Cs:

    • Critical thinking and problem solving involves using various reasoning types (inductive, deductive, systems thinking), analysing evidence and claims, solving non-routine problems, and reflecting critically on one's learning processes.
    • Communication means articulating thoughts effectively across oral, written, and nonverbal forms; listening actively; communicating for multiple purposes (informing, instructing, motivating, persuading); and operating effectively in multilingual and diverse environments.
    • Collaboration requires working respectfully in diverse teams, exercising flexibility and making compromises toward shared goals, assuming shared responsibility, and valuing individual contributions.
    • Creativity and innovation involves using idea-generation techniques, creating new and worthwhile ideas, remaining open to diverse perspectives and feedback, and viewing failure as a learning opportunity.

    The second domain is Information, Media, and Technology Skills: information literacy, media literacy, and ICT literacy. In an age of information abundance and digital manipulation, the ability to find, evaluate, and use information ethically is foundational.

    The third domain is Life and Career Skills: flexibility and adaptability, initiative and self-direction, social and cross-cultural skills, productivity and accountability, and leadership and responsibility.

    Importantly, P21 emphasises that these skills are not developed in isolation. They are supported by systems including standards, assessments, curriculum, professional development, and learning environments. The framework recognizes that skills develop in context and require systemic support.

    ℹ Key Concept — The 4Cs

    Critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity are the core competencies that P21 identifies as essential. They appear, in various forms, in all major competency frameworks. They are not brand new to the 21st century—they have been educational aspirations for centuries—but the research on how to teach them explicitly and how to assess them has advanced significantly.

    ATC21S: Integrating Cognitive Science

    The ATC21S framework, developed by the University of Melbourne with sponsorship from major technology companies, takes a slightly different organisational approach, rooted in cognitive science research (Griffin, McGaw, & Care, 2012). It identifies four broad categories that together constitute 21st-century competencies.

    Ways of thinking encompass creativity, critical thinking, problem-solving, decision-making, and learning to learn. This category prioritises metacognitive and higher-order thinking skills. "Learning to learn" is particularly important—it refers to metacognitive awareness of one's own learning processes and the ability to adapt learning strategies to different contexts and challenges.

    Ways of working focus on communication and collaboration. Like P21, this framework recognizes that knowledge work in the modern world happens in teams and requires the ability to articulate ideas, listen carefully, and coordinate with others toward shared goals.

    Tools for working include information literacy and ICT literacy. The category recognizes that in the digital age, tools mediate knowledge work. But literacy with these tools is not about technical proficiency alone; it is about understanding how to access, evaluate, and use information effectively and ethically.

    Living in the world encompasses citizenship, life and career skills, and personal and social responsibility. This domain explicitly links individual competency development to participation in society—a recognition that education serves not just individual achievement but community and civic engagement.

    The ATC21S framework emphasizes that competencies are interconnected and situational (Griffin, McGaw, & Care, 2012). A person might demonstrate excellent critical thinking in one domain but struggle in another. Competencies develop through practice in authentic contexts, not through decontextualised skill lessons.

    The OECD Learning Compass 2030

    The OECD's framework takes a distinctive approach by foregrounding student agency and transformative competencies (OECD, 2019). Rather than listing skills to be acquired, the OECD asks: What competencies enable students to shape their own futures and contribute to collective well-being?

    The framework identifies three transformative competencies:

    Creating new value involves the capacity to generate ideas, products, or solutions that are novel and worthwhile—not just reproducing existing knowledge. This extends beyond innovation in technology to innovation in thinking, communication, and social engagement.

    Reconciling tensions and dilemmas recognizes that real-world problems are rarely clear-cut. This competency involves navigating competing values, multiple perspectives, and uncertainty. It includes ethical reasoning and the ability to live with complexity.

    Taking responsibility is perhaps the most philosophically grounded competency. It involves understanding one's role in larger systems, recognising how one's choices affect others, and acting with awareness of consequences.

    What makes the OECD framework distinctive is its emphasis on knowledge, skills, attitudes, and values as interconnected. A student might possess the skill of critical analysis but lack the attitude of intellectual humility necessary to consider alternative perspectives. A student might understand collaboration but lack the value of reciprocity necessary to collaborate authentically. The framework insists that competency development involves the whole person.

    » In Practice

    A student researching climate change might demonstrate strong critical thinking skills—finding sources, evaluating evidence, constructing arguments. But if she lacks the attitude of epistemic humility (recognizing the limits of her own knowledge), she might dismiss legitimate scientific perspectives. If she lacks the value of shared responsibility, she might focus solely on individual consumer choices rather than systemic change. OECD competencies would be fully developed only when skills, attitudes, and values align.

    The IB Approaches to Learning (ATL)

    The IB's ATL framework, developed specifically for the International Baccalaureate programmes, organises competencies around five broad categories of learning skills (IBO, 2015). ATL skills are meant to be taught across subjects and to develop progressively from primary through secondary levels.

    The five categories are:

    1. Thinking skills (analysing, evaluating, creating) develop the higher-order cognitive processes at the heart of inquiry and critical thinking.
    2. Communication skills (reading, writing, listening, speaking) emphasise that ideas must be expressed in multiple forms for multiple audiences.
    3. Social skills (collaborating, demonstrating empathy, working with diversity) recognise that learning happens in community and requires interpersonal capability.
    4. Self-management skills (organisation, time management, persistence, self-regulation) acknowledge that learners must develop agency and autonomy.
    5. Research skills (information literacy, finding and evaluating sources, synthesis) are essential for independent inquiry and lifelong learning.

    The ATL framework is explicitly integrated with IB's transdisciplinary curriculum philosophy. The idea is not that ATL skills are separate from subject content, but that they are developed through engaging with disciplinary content. A student learning history develops research skills (finding primary and secondary sources), thinking skills (analysing causation and perspective), and communication skills (writing persuasive historical arguments)—simultaneously.

    For Danish schools, the ATL framework is particularly relevant because many implement IB programmes, and the framework's emphasis on holistic learning aligns well with dannelse.

    The Convergence: Four Core Competency Areas

    Despite the differences in organisation and emphasis, these frameworks converge remarkably on four core competency areas. Call them the 4Cs or organise them differently, but the substance is consistent:

    Critical thinking appears in every framework under various names: "analysing," "evaluating," "decision-making," "ways of thinking." Its core components are consistent: using evidence to support claims, considering multiple perspectives, evaluating credibility and bias, recognising assumptions, reflecting on one's own reasoning.

    Communication is similarly universal. It encompasses not just speaking and writing but listening, reading for comprehension, and adapting message to audience. It includes both linguistic and nonverbal forms.

    Collaboration refers to working effectively with others toward shared goals. Every framework recognizes that knowledge work happens in teams and that the ability to coordinate, compromise, and leverage diverse perspectives is essential.

    Creativity and innovation (sometimes framed as "creating new value") appears in every framework. Whether called "creativity," "problem-solving," or "creating new value," it refers to the capacity to generate novel ideas and solutions rather than simply reproducing existing knowledge.

    Beyond these four core areas, frameworks emphasize domains that are foundational but more specific:

    • Information and digital literacy are consistently identified as essential in an age of information abundance and digital technology.
    • Metacognition and self-regulation appear in various forms: "learning to learn," "self-management," "taking responsibility," "critical reflection."
    • Ethical reasoning and responsibility are increasingly explicit in frameworks, reflecting recognition that competent people must act with awareness of values and consequences.

    This convergence is significant. It suggests that despite different starting points and different stakeholder interests, independent frameworks have identified something real about what matters for human success and flourishing in complex, knowledge-based societies.

    Criticisms: Valid Concerns About 21st-Century Skills Rhetoric

    The frameworks have important critics, and their concerns deserve serious engagement.

    First, the accusation of vagueness. Rotherham and Willingham (2010) argue that frameworks like P21 are too broad to guide curriculum design meaningfully. What does "creativity" actually look like in a 9th-grade biology class? How would a teacher assess it? Without more specificity, the frameworks become aspirational rhetoric rather than actionable guidance. This criticism is fair. A school adopting a competency curriculum must move beyond the frameworks to the level of specific learning intentions and success criteria.

    Second, the claim of false novelty. Silva (2009) and others argue that these competencies are not new. Critical thinking, communication, and collaboration have been educational goals for centuries. The label "21st-century" implies they are new when they are not. This criticism, while historically accurate, misses the point. The novelty lies not in the competencies themselves but in three things: the convergent evidence that these specific competencies matter across multiple frameworks and contexts; the research on how to teach them explicitly; and the urgency of addressing them systematically, given the demands of contemporary work and society.

    Third, the assessment challenge. Care, Kim, and Vista (2018) identify a genuine problem: measuring complex skills like creativity and collaboration reliably remains difficult. Traditional testing approaches (multiple-choice assessments) cannot capture these competencies. New approaches (portfolios, performance tasks, rubrics) are more promising but require more teacher training and time. This is a real implementation challenge that has no simple solution.

    Fourth, the economic bias critique. Kereluik and colleagues (2013) argue that frameworks like P21 are driven by corporate interests in workforce readiness rather than by educational philosophy or human flourishing. The language of "21st-century skills" serves employer interests in flexible, adaptable workers. This criticism contains truth but deserves nuance. Some frameworks (particularly OECD's) deliberately move beyond workforce readiness to questions of agency and social contribution. And even if frameworks began as responses to economic change, the competencies identified are valuable for reasons beyond employment.

    ? For Reflection

    Consider a competency you teach or want to develop—let's say critical thinking. Can you articulate specifically what critical thinking means in your discipline? How would a student demonstrate it? How would you assess it? Where does the framework help you, and where do you need to do more detailed work?

    The Danish Addition: Dannelse as Philosophical Foundation

    While the international frameworks are valuable, they operate at a level of educational policy and psychology. The Danish educational tradition adds something they often lack: a philosophical foundation that reaches beyond skill acquisition.

    The concept of dannelse—often translated as "formation," "Bildung," or "character formation"—frames education as the development of the whole person as a thinking, ethical, culturally engaged individual. Associated with thinkers like N.F.S. Grundtvig, the founder of the folk high school tradition (Korsgaard, 2004), dannelse has deep roots in Danish educational philosophy. It is embedded in contemporary Danish law — the Folkeskoleloven (primary school law) refers to almen dannelse, general formation, as a core purpose of schooling (Danish Ministry of Education, 2019) — and in the Folkeskole curriculum.

    Dannelse does not reject the competencies identified in international frameworks. Rather, it provides a deeper why. Critical thinking is important not merely because employers value it, but because it is essential to an informed, reflective citizen. Collaboration matters not just for team productivity but because humans are social beings who develop through community (fællesskab). Communication is valued not only for professional effectiveness but because it is fundamental to human dignity and connection.

    Moreover, dannelse extends competency development beyond the cognitive and social to include aesthetic, ethical, and existential dimensions. A person formed through dannelse is not just a skilled thinker but a person of character, capable of judgment, responsible in their choices, engaged in culture and community.

    For a Danish school designing a competency curriculum, this means grounding international frameworks in Danish educational values. The 4Cs and the domains identified by OECD and ATC21S become not merely skills to be acquired but facets of human formation. This deeper philosophical grounding often provides greater motivation and coherence than framework alone.

    Practical Implications for Curriculum Design

    What do these frameworks suggest for the practical work of curriculum design?

    First, clarity on what matters. The frameworks converge on core competencies. A school implementing competency education should choose one framework (or synthesise across frameworks) as the backbone of the curriculum. This provides teachers with a shared language and prevents the fragmentation that occurs when different departments use different definitions.

    Second, integration over isolation. All frameworks emphasise that competencies develop through engagement with content, not through standalone "skills lessons." A student learns critical thinking by thinking critically about history, science, literature—not by attending a generic "critical thinking" unit. This has major implications for professional development and curriculum design. Teachers in content areas must become skilled at making competencies visible, teaching competency processes explicitly, and providing opportunities for practice and feedback within their disciplines.

    Third, clarity on learning progressions. Critical thinking in primary school looks different from critical thinking in secondary school. A framework should specify how competencies develop over time, from simpler to more complex forms. The IB's ATL framework attempts this across primary and secondary levels. A school developing curriculum should do the same: specify what critical thinking looks like at each level, with increasing cognitive complexity.

    Fourth, assessment that matches competencies. If competencies like collaboration and creativity cannot be reliably measured by multiple-choice tests, then assessment must change. Performance tasks, portfolios, presentations, and observation-based rubrics become necessary. This is a significant professional development and implementation challenge.

    Fifth, systemic support. P21's insistence on systems—curriculum, assessment, professional development, learning environment—is correct. Individual teachers can teach critical thinking well, but for competency development to be consistent and deep across a school, it requires alignment of standards, materials, assessment, and teacher learning. This is why Building Thinking Classrooms (Liljedahl, 2020), with its focus on structural classroom practices, is so valuable: it creates an environment where competencies develop not through exhortation but through the design of tasks and interactions.
