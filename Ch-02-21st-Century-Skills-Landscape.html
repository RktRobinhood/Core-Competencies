    Chapter 2 — Framework Reference: The 21st-Century Skills Landscape

    ◉ Purpose

    Chapter 1 argues that independent competency frameworks converge on what matters and that this convergence is significant. This chapter provides the detailed framework-by-framework reference that supports that argument. It is designed as a reference tool, not as sequential reading. Consult the specific framework section you need, or use the convergence table to compare them.

    How to Use This Chapter

    This chapter serves three purposes. First, it provides the detailed descriptions of each framework's categories and definitions for curriculum designers who need to align their work to a specific framework. Second, it maps the convergence across frameworks in a single table, making the case from Chapter 1 visually concrete. Third, it presents the criticisms and limitations of framework approaches so that implementation is honest about what frameworks can and cannot do.

    For the argumentative case — why frameworks matter and what their convergence means for schools — see Chapter 1.

    The Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21)

    The P21 framework, developed by Battelle for Kids with input from educators and business leaders, organises student outcomes into three interconnected domains (Battelle for Kids, 2019).

    Learning and Innovation Skills — the 4Cs. Critical thinking and problem solving involves using various reasoning types (inductive, deductive, systems thinking), analysing evidence and claims, solving non-routine problems, and reflecting critically on one's learning processes. Communication means articulating thoughts effectively across oral, written, and nonverbal forms, listening actively, and communicating for multiple purposes. Collaboration requires working respectfully in diverse teams, exercising flexibility and making compromises toward shared goals, and assuming shared responsibility. Creativity and innovation involves using idea-generation techniques, creating new and worthwhile ideas, remaining open to diverse perspectives, and viewing failure as a learning opportunity.

    Information, Media, and Technology Skills. Information literacy, media literacy, and ICT literacy. In an age of information abundance, the ability to find, evaluate, and use information ethically is foundational.

    Life and Career Skills. Flexibility and adaptability, initiative and self-direction, social and cross-cultural skills, productivity and accountability, and leadership and responsibility.

    P21 emphasises that these skills are not developed in isolation but are supported by systems including standards, assessments, curriculum, professional development, and learning environments.

    ATC21S: Integrating Cognitive Science

    The ATC21S framework, developed by the University of Melbourne, identifies four broad categories rooted in cognitive science research (Griffin, McGaw, & Care, 2012).

    Ways of thinking encompass creativity, critical thinking, problem-solving, decision-making, and learning to learn. The inclusion of "learning to learn" is significant — it refers to metacognitive awareness and the ability to adapt learning strategies to different contexts.

    Ways of working focus on communication and collaboration.

    Tools for working include information literacy and ICT literacy — not just technical proficiency but the ability to access, evaluate, and use information effectively and ethically.

    Living in the world encompasses citizenship, life and career skills, and personal and social responsibility.

    ATC21S emphasizes that competencies are interconnected, situational, and develop through practice in authentic contexts rather than decontextualised skill lessons.

    The OECD Learning Compass 2030

    The OECD foregrounds student agency and three transformative competencies (OECD, 2019).

    Creating new value involves generating ideas, products, or solutions that are novel and worthwhile — extending beyond innovation in technology to innovation in thinking, communication, and social engagement.

    Reconciling tensions and dilemmas recognizes that real-world problems are rarely clear-cut. This competency involves navigating competing values, multiple perspectives, and uncertainty — including ethical reasoning and the ability to live with complexity.

    Taking responsibility involves understanding one's role in larger systems, recognising how one's choices affect others, and acting with awareness of consequences.

    The OECD framework is distinctive in insisting that knowledge, skills, attitudes, and values are interconnected. A student might possess the skill of critical analysis but lack the attitude of intellectual humility necessary to consider alternative perspectives. Competency development involves the whole person.

    The IB Approaches to Learning (ATL)

    The IB's ATL framework organises competencies around five categories of learning skills (IBO, 2015), designed to be taught across subjects and to develop progressively from primary through secondary levels.

    Thinking skills develop higher-order cognitive processes at the heart of inquiry and critical thinking. Communication skills emphasise expressing ideas in multiple forms for multiple audiences. Social skills recognise that learning happens in community and requires interpersonal capability. Self-management skills acknowledge that learners must develop agency and autonomy. Research skills are essential for independent inquiry and lifelong learning.

    The ATL framework is explicitly integrated with the IB's transdisciplinary philosophy: competencies develop through engaging with disciplinary content, not as standalone skill lessons.

    Convergence Table

    Competency Area P21 ATC21S OECD 2030 IB ATL
    Critical thinking 4Cs: Critical thinking & problem solving Ways of thinking: Critical thinking, decision-making Reconciling tensions & dilemmas Thinking skills
    Communication 4Cs: Communication Ways of working: Communication (embedded across competencies) Communication skills
    Collaboration 4Cs: Collaboration Ways of working: Collaboration Taking responsibility; co-agency Social skills
    Creativity 4Cs: Creativity & innovation Ways of thinking: Creativity Creating new value Thinking skills
    Metacognition (implicit in critical thinking) Ways of thinking: Learning to learn Student agency; anticipation-action-reflection cycle Self-management skills
    Information literacy Information, media & technology skills Tools for working: Information literacy (embedded in creating new value) Research skills
    Ethical reasoning Life & career: Leadership & responsibility Living in the world: Citizenship Taking responsibility Social skills: Ethical practice

    The convergence on critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity is robust across all four frameworks. Metacognition and self-regulation appear in every framework under varying names. Ethical reasoning and social responsibility are increasingly explicit.

    Criticisms and Limitations

    The frameworks have important critics, engaged honestly in Chapter 1. In summary: the accusation of vagueness is fair — frameworks must be operationalised at the level of specific learning intentions and success criteria (Rotherham & Willingham, 2010). The claim of false novelty is historically accurate but misses the point — the novelty lies in the convergent evidence and the research on explicit instruction, not in the competencies themselves (Silva, 2009). The assessment challenge is real and unresolved — measuring competencies like collaboration reliably requires new assessment approaches (Care, Kim, & Vista, 2018). The economic bias critique contains truth — some frameworks prioritise workforce readiness over human flourishing (Kereluik et al., 2013) — but the Danish concept of dannelse provides a deeper philosophical grounding that addresses this concern directly.
