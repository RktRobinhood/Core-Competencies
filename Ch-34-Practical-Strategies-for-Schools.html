Chapter 34 — Practical Strategies for Schools: From Theory to Daily Practice

◉ Overview

This chapter synthesizes research-based strategies for embedding core competencies, cognitive science insights, and thinking classroom practices into daily teaching. It serves as the practical heart of this course: not just what competencies matter and why, but how to make them visible and valued in classrooms every day. Strategies are organized by context and include specific classroom routines that teachers can implement immediately.

Strategy 1: Begin Lessons with Thinking Tasks

The research from Peter Liljedahl on building thinking classrooms identifies a powerful finding: when lessons begin with a non-routine problem that students tackle in random groups, with the task given verbally before they sit down, significantly more thinking occurs. This is not a minor effect. It fundamentally changes classroom culture.

How to do it: Before students are seated or settled, give them a task verbally while they stand. The task should have multiple entry points (different students can begin at different levels of complexity), should require thinking (not following a procedure), and should be interesting to students. Spend 15-20 minutes on the task before transitioning to direct instruction or content learning.

Examples: A problem like "A farmer has 120 meters of fencing. What dimensions of rectangular field would maximize growing space?" invites multiple approaches: some students try specific rectangles, some use algebra, some reason about geometry. Another example: "Two trains leave cities 200 kilometers apart, one at 80 km/h, one at 60 km/h. When do they meet?" allows students to draw, build a model, calculate, or reason conceptually.

Why it works: The task produces cognitive engagement. Students must think, not follow instructions. The public, verbal presentation prevents some students from opting out. Random groups expose students to different approaches. The non-routine nature means there is not a single right procedure; students must reason.

Connection to competencies: This strategy directly develops critical thinking, metacognition (students must monitor their approach), collaboration, and resilience (staying with a hard problem).

» In Practice

A Danish class on written communication begins with this task: "You have one sentence to explain a complex idea (your choice). What matters more: precise vocabulary or clear structure? How would you decide?" Students discuss in their groups, disagree productively, and generate examples. Then the teacher reveals that they will be studying argument and clarity throughout the unit—and students now understand why both precision and structure matter. The thinking task primes the learning that follows.

Strategy 2: Use Retrieval Practice Every Day

Research consistently shows that retrieval practice—recalling information from memory—is among the highest-impact learning techniques available. Yet many classrooms rarely use it. The evidence from Dunlosky et al. (2013) is clear: daily retrieval of previous learning, spaced across weeks and units (not just yesterday's lesson), dramatically improves long-term retention and transfer.

How to do it: Open or close every lesson with a 5-minute retrieval activity. Include questions from previous weeks and units, not just the current lesson. Mix question types and topics (interleaving). Keep stakes low; the goal is learning, not grading.

Practical tools: - Brain dumps: "Write everything you remember about photosynthesis" (5 minutes, then review) - Mini-quizzes: 3-5 questions, self-marked or peer-marked, low stakes - Paired recall: "Tell your partner three things about the French Revolution" - Flashcard routines with expanding intervals (review often at first, then less frequently over time) - Quick writes: "What's one question you still have about..." then share with partner

Why it works: Retrieval strengthens memory. Spacing intervals (not massing practice) produces better long-term retention. Interleaving (mixing topics) improves transfer. Low stakes removes anxiety and improves willingness to try.

Connection to competencies: This strategy develops knowledge retention and the foundation for critical thinking (you cannot think critically about material you do not remember). It also builds self-regulation: students learn to own their learning by tracking what they remember and don't remember.

ℹ Key Concept — Spacing and Interleaving

Spaced practice means reviewing material at expanding intervals: day 1, day 3, day 7, day 14. This produces better long-term learning than massed practice (reviewing the same material five times on day 1). Interleaving means mixing topics during retrieval: asking about photosynthesis, then respiration, then cellular transport, rather than five questions about photosynthesis in a row. Interleaving improves transfer to novel problems but feels harder during learning, which is why students sometimes resist it.

Strategy 3: Teach Metacognition Explicitly Using a Structured Model

The Education Endowment Foundation (2025) identifies metacognition as high-impact and cost-effective. But metacognition doesn't develop automatically. It must be taught explicitly.

How to do it: Use the EEF seven-step model: activate → instruct → model → memorise → guide → practise → reflect.

  • Activate: What do students already think about how they learn?
  • Instruct: Teach the metacognitive strategy directly. "Here's how good readers monitor their understanding: they pause and ask 'Does this make sense? Do I agree? What's the evidence?'"
  • Model: Use think-alouds where you verbalize your own thinking process. "I'm going to solve this problem aloud so you hear my thinking, not just my answer."
  • Memorise: Help students remember the strategy. Create posters, acronyms, or routines that make it stick.
  • Guide: Use scaffolded practice: "I do it, we do it together, you do it independently."
  • Practise: Repeated application in different contexts until the strategy becomes automatic.
  • Reflect: Regular reflection: "What strategy did you use? How did it help? When else might it be useful?"

Practical classroom routines: - Planning before solving: "What do you know? What are you trying to find? What approach will you try?" - Monitoring during work: "Stop and ask: Does this make sense? Am I on track? Do I need to change my approach?" - Reflection after: "What went well? What would you do differently? What did you learn about how you learn?"

Connection to competencies: Metacognition is self-regulation. It is the competency that allows learners to take ownership of their own development.

Strategy 4: Embed Competency Language Into Subject Teaching

Core competencies should not be taught separately; they should be embedded into subject learning. When teaching critical thinking, creativity, collaboration, or communication, name the competency explicitly and connect it to subject content.

How to do it: - List competencies in learning objectives: "Today you will solve optimization problems (content) while developing your problem-solving and communication competencies (competency)." - Provide progression rubrics showing what developing, proficient, and advanced looks like for that competency in that context - Use structured reflection where students assess their own competency development - Plan cross-departmental competency alignment so students see the same competencies across subjects

Example progression rubric for collaboration:

Developing Proficient Advanced
Completes assigned role; follows group decisions Contributes ideas; listens to others; helps solve disagreements Builds on others' ideas; ensures all voices are heard; reflects on group effectiveness

Connection to competencies: When competencies are named and valued explicitly, students attend to them and develop them deliberately. When they are hidden, students don't notice them and don't grow in them.

Strategy 5: Protect Deep Reading Time

Deep reading—sustained engagement with complex texts—develops critical thinking, vocabulary, background knowledge, and attention. Yet it is eroding. Schools should deliberately protect it.

How to do it: - Schedule sustained silent reading (SSR) in print texts - Teach close reading and annotation strategies: mark questions you have, underline key ideas, write reactions in margins - Use print for complex texts; reserve digital for search and reference - Model deep reading as a teacher—read alongside students, share your thinking about the text - Assign reading thoughtfully: not excessive quantity, but texts worth reading deeply

AI-era addition: When AI can summarize any text, the value of deep reading becomes more important, not less. Schools should explicitly protect it.

Connection to competencies: Deep reading develops critical thinking, information literacy, and the ability to engage with complex ideas over time.

Strategy 6: Maintain Handwriting as a Cognitive Tool

Research shows that handwritten notes produce better memory and transfer than typed notes, even when note content is identical. Yet handwriting is declining. Schools should intentionally preserve it for high-value cognitive tasks.

How to do it: - Use handwriting for note-taking during lessons, not typing - Teach students the cognitive science behind why handwriting aids memory (the motor encoding, the slower pace that forces selection) - Use handwriting with Vertical Non-Permanent Surfaces (VNPS—whiteboards, chart paper) for collaborative problem-solving - Reserve typing for production tasks (essays, reports, presentations)

Connection to competencies: Handwriting supports memory and deep learning. It slows thinking down enough to allow reflection and connection-making.

Strategy 7: Build Collaboration Skills Deliberately Using Structures

Collaboration will not develop without deliberate teaching and structure. Random grouping alone is not enough; students need explicit instruction in how to collaborate well.

How to do it: - Use visibly random groups that change frequently (not self-selected groups or fixed groups) - Assign structured roles within groups: facilitator, recorder, timekeeper, reporter - Teach skills explicitly: active listening, asking for ideas, respectful disagreement, checking understanding - Use structured protocols: think-pair-share (think alone, discuss with partner, share with class), jigsaw (each group becomes expert on one topic then teaches others), gallery walk (groups create, post, view others' work), Socratic seminar (discussion with norms for listening and building on ideas) - Reflect on group process: "How did we work together? What went well? What would we do differently next time?"

Connection to competencies: Collaboration is both a competency students develop and a condition for developing other competencies. Well-structured collaboration supports thinking, creativity, and communication.

Strategy 8: Connect Competencies to Dannelse

Competencies should not be taught as technical skills alone. They should be framed within the larger question of who students are becoming and what they are responsible for.

How to do it: - Frame skills with purpose: "Why does this competency matter? How does it relate to who you are and who you want to become?" - Include reflection on values and identity: "What do you value? How does this learning connect to your values?" - Use Danish language for core concepts: dannelse, selvbestemmelse, fællesskab, medbestemmelse - Draw on VIA character strengths: Help students identify their strengths and understand how to develop them - Make connections to citizenship, responsibility, and contribution

Example: Rather than "Today we practice giving feedback," frame it as: "Thoughtful feedback is a way of showing respect for someone and helping them grow. Today we practice giving feedback in a way that is honest and kind. How does being able to give good feedback make you a better collaborator, friend, and future professional?"

Connection to competencies: When competencies are connected to purpose and identity, they become more meaningful and more likely to transfer beyond school contexts.

Strategy 9: Design Assessment That Matches Values

If collaboration is a core competency, assess it. If metacognition is valued, collect evidence of metacognitive thinking. Assessment communicates what the school values.

How to do it: - Use formative assessment to inform both teaching and learning: Where is this student in their competency development? What's the next step? - Give students clarity about where they are and where they are going: Show progression rubrics, give descriptive feedback - Move toward outcomes-based grading (competencies demonstrated) rather than events-based grading (test score) - Include process evidence: drafts, thinking routines, reflections—not just final products - Assess in authentic contexts when possible: students collaborating on a real problem, thinking routines visible in their work

Connection to competencies: What and how you assess shapes what students attend to and develop.

Strategy 10: Manage Technology Intentionally

Technology can support or undermine competency development. Schools should use it deliberately.

How to do it: - Establish tech-free zones and times for focused work: no devices during thinking tasks, deep reading, or face-to-face collaboration - Remove devices when they are not serving the learning task - Teach students about persuasive technology design: How do apps try to capture your attention? What is your responsibility when using them? - Build digital literacy into curriculum: not as a separate unit, but integrated with information literacy and critical thinking - Model intentional technology use as a teacher

Connection to competencies: This strategy protects the cognitive and social space needed for deep learning, collaboration, and critical thinking.

Implementation Sequence for Schools Beginning This Work

Schools beginning competency work should not try all ten strategies at once. A staged sequence is more effective:

Weeks 1–3: Introduce thinking tasks and visibly random groups. These produce visible, immediate changes in classroom dynamics and culture. Students begin to see themselves as capable thinkers.

Weeks 4–6: Add daily retrieval practice across departments. Combine with interleaving by including previous topics. Students begin to develop stronger long-term memory and see connections across units.

Weeks 7–10: Begin explicit metacognitive instruction. Use think-alouds and structured reflection. Students develop awareness of their own thinking and begin to take more ownership of their learning.

Weeks 11+: Layer in remaining strategies. Build ATL language and competency naming, protect reading time, maintain handwriting, develop collaboration structures. Develop assessment practices aligned with valued competencies.

Term 2+: Develop portfolio evidence of competency development. Reflect on progress and plan next steps.

The implementation sequence here is grounded in the research on thinking, learning, and school change described throughout this course. It prioritizes high-impact, visible changes early, builds on them with deeper work later, and allows teachers to develop their own competency as they support students' growth.

Troubleshooting Common Challenges

"I don't have time for all this." Start with one strategy. Do it well. Move to the next. Quality matters more than quantity. One well-implemented thinking task beats five poorly implemented initiatives.

"My students are used to traditional teaching and resist new structures." Resistance is normal and often temporary. Persist with clarity about why the new structures matter. Student voice and choice help. Most importantly, make sure the new structures are genuinely better for learning than what came before; students will feel that difference.

"How do I assess competencies?" Start with observation and descriptive feedback. Use progression rubrics showing what development looks like. Collect evidence from multiple sources: student work, collaboration observations, reflections, projects. Over time, develop more formal assessment as the competencies become clearer.

"Different teachers teach these competencies differently." Good. Different contexts call for different approaches. The key is that the competencies are named, valued, and intentionally developed—not that they all look identical. Use professional learning communities to share different approaches and learn from each other.
