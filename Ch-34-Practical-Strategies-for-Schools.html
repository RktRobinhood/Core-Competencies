    Chapter 34 — Practical Strategies for Schools: From Theory to Daily Practice

    ◉ Overview

    This chapter is a practical hub synthesizing research-based strategies for embedding core competencies, cognitive science insights, and thinking classroom practices into daily teaching. It serves as the practical heart of this course: not just what competencies matter and why, but how to make them visible and valued in classrooms every day.

    The strategies are organized across two sub-chapters. The first contains ten foundational classroom strategies, organized by context and including specific routines teachers can implement immediately. The second covers AI-augmented teaching practices, implementation sequences for schools, troubleshooting, and complete references.

    The Structure of This Chapter

    This hub is organized as follows:

    Chapter 34a: Classroom Strategies and Routines (Strategies 1–10)

    Core teaching practices organized by classroom context:

    1. Begin Lessons with Thinking Tasks — Using non-routine problems in random groups to build thinking culture from the start of a lesson
    2. Use Retrieval Practice Every Day — Spaced, interleaved review of previous material to strengthen long-term memory and transfer
    3. Teach Metacognition Explicitly — Using a structured seven-step model to develop learner self-awareness and self-regulation
    4. Embed Competency Language Into Subject Teaching — Making competencies visible and deliberate within subject content, not as separate skills
    5. Protect Deep Reading Time — Sustaining engagement with complex texts in an AI-era classroom
    6. Maintain Handwriting as a Cognitive Tool — Preserving handwriting for high-value tasks despite digital pressure
    7. Build Collaboration Skills Deliberately — Using structured protocols and random grouping to teach collaboration explicitly
    8. Connect Competencies to Dannelse — Framing competencies within purpose, identity, and values
    9. Design Assessment That Matches Values — Using formative and competency-based assessment to communicate what the school values
    10. Manage Technology Intentionally — Using technology deliberately to support, not undermine, deep learning and collaboration

    These ten strategies are grounded in cognitive science research and are designed to be implemented immediately in any classroom.

    Chapter 34b: AI Agents and Implementation (Strategy 11, Implementation Guidance, Troubleshooting, References)

    Advanced practices enabled by AI agents and implementation guidance for schools:

    1. Leverage AI Agents for High-Value Practices — Using AI to scale practices that research supports but that teachers cannot manage alone, including:
      • Note checks and learning audits (assessing thinking in student notes)
      • Spaced retrieval prompts (personalised, interleaved review at scale)
      • Accountability and habit tracking (making student commitments visible and supported)
      • Formative writing feedback at scale (rapid feedback on student writing with human review of revised work)

    This sub-chapter also includes: - Implementation Sequence for Schools — A staged rollout plan (Weeks 1–3 through Term 2+) for schools beginning this work - Troubleshooting Common Challenges — Responses to time pressure, student resistance, assessment questions, and cross-departmental coordination - Complete References — All citations for both sub-chapters

    Why Two Sub-Chapters?

    The original chapter (356 lines) combined foundational classroom strategies with advanced AI-supported practices. Splitting into two sub-chapters:

    • Reduces cognitive load — Each file focuses on a coherent idea (core practices vs. AI-augmented practices)
    • Enables different use cases — Teachers seeking core strategies can start with Ch-34a; those exploring AI integration can go directly to Ch-34b
    • Preserves all content — No material was deleted; all strategies, examples, research notes, and troubleshooting are intact
    • Maintains the hub structure — The main file (this page) links both sub-chapters and provides overview, while each sub-chapter links back to the hub and cross-references

    Key Principles Across All Strategies

    Whether implementing classroom routines or AI-supported practices, several principles run throughout:

    1. Competencies are embedded, not separate — Strategies 1–10 show that competencies develop best when named and valued within subject teaching, not taught in isolation.

    2. High-value practices are often unsustainable alone — Strategy 11 (AI agents) addresses a real constraint: many evidence-based practices are abandoned not because teachers don't believe in them, but because the logistics overwhelm a single teacher.

    3. AI augments, doesn't replace, human judgment — AI handles the monitoring and initial feedback ("the arithmetic"); the teacher provides relationship, judgment, and deeper pedagogical decisions ("the humanity").

    4. Assessment communicates values — What and how schools assess shapes what students attend to and develop. Strategies 4 and 9 emphasize designing assessment systems aligned with competency goals.

    5. Implementation is staged, not all-at-once — Research on school change recommends introducing high-impact, visible changes early and layering in more complex practices later. See Ch-34b for the implementation sequence.

    The Research Behind These Strategies

    These strategies are not new ideas. They are well-established, evidence-based approaches that research has repeatedly shown to be high-impact. The challenge is not whether they work, but how to implement them sustainably.

    • Thinking tasks and classroom culture are grounded in research by Peter Liljedahl (2020) on building thinking classrooms.
    • Retrieval practice and spaced learning are among the most robust findings in cognitive psychology, synthesized by Dunlosky et al. (2013).
    • Metacognition is identified by the Education Endowment Foundation (2025) as high-impact and cost-effective.
    • Handwriting and note-taking are supported by research from Mueller & Oppenheimer (2014) on motor encoding and memory.
    • AI augmentation of teaching is grounded in evidence from Gallup (2025) on teacher productivity, scaled assessment, and student learning.

    See Ch-34b for complete references.

    Getting Started

    Schools beginning this work should not try to implement all strategies at once. See Ch-34b for an implementation sequence that starts with high-impact, visible changes (Strategies 1 and 7 in Weeks 1–3) and layers in deeper practices (metacognition, competency naming, assessment, technology management) over weeks 7–10 and beyond.
