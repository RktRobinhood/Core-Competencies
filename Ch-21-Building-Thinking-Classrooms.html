    Chapter 21 — Building Thinking Classrooms

    ◉ Overview

    Peter Liljedahl's Building Thinking Classrooms framework, developed through 15 years of classroom research, identifies fourteen interconnected teaching practices that shift students from passive task completion to continuous engaged thinking. The framework moves beyond content delivery to redesign the fundamental social and physical structures of the classroom, producing observable changes in student behaviour: students expect to think, take intellectual risks, collaborate across social boundaries, and sustain focus for extended periods. This chapter integrates three core structural practices—visibly random groups, vertical non-permanent surfaces, and thinking tasks—along with supporting practices for sustaining and consolidating thinking.

    The Central Research Question

    Liljedahl's work began with a deceptively simple observation: in typical classrooms, students spend most of their time engaged in behaviour that does not involve thinking. They copy notes, follow procedures, wait for the teacher to provide answers, and disengage when given tasks that require sustained cognitive effort. This led Liljedahl to ask a fundamental question that shaped fifteen years of research: "How can we get more students to think, and to think for longer periods of time?"

    The answer was not a single strategy but a tightly integrated system of 14 practices organized into three phases: practices for starting thinking, practices for sustaining thinking, and practices for consolidating thinking. What emerged from Liljedahl's iterative design-based research was a framework that transformed passive learning environments into spaces where thinking became the default expectation.

    ℹ Key Concept — The Thinking Culture

    A thinking culture is not created by motivational posters or exhortations to "think harder." It emerges when the structural, social, and cognitive conditions in the classroom align to make thinking the natural, unavoidable activity. When students are placed in situations where they must think to move forward, where collaboration with different peers is the norm, and where risk-taking is psychologically safe, the culture shifts: students begin to expect to think in every lesson.

    The Three Starting Practices

    The first three practices establish the preconditions for thinking to occur. Each addresses a specific barrier to engagement that Liljedahl observed across hundreds of classrooms.

    Thinking Tasks (Practice 1) begins with the recognition that the quality of thinking depends on the quality of the task. Liljedahl found that engaging non-curricular problems (puzzle-like tasks that require sense-making but no prerequisite knowledge) are effective entry points for establishing a thinking culture, especially with students who have experienced years of procedural mathematics. However, the ultimate goal is not entertainment but curriculum-based thinking tasks—problems that require students to apply, combine, and reason with content rather than merely reproduce procedures. The task must demand thinking; the curriculum must be worth thinking about.

    Visibly Random Groups (Practice 2) represents one of the most powerful findings from Liljedahl's research. When students were assigned to groups using strategic selection by the teacher, 80 percent entered the group with the mindset that their role was not to think—they expected the teacher or their stronger peers to think for them. When students self-selected their groups, they replicated pre-existing social hierarchies and roles. However, when groups were formed using a visibly random method—drawing cards, pulling names from a hat, or using a randomiser that the whole class could see—something remarkable happened. After six weeks of consistent use, 100 percent of students entered their groups expecting to think and contribute.

    The word "visibly" is critical. The randomness must be transparent. If students suspect the teacher is manipulating the outcome ("pretending to be random"), the benefits disappear. Groups of three prove optimal: pairs lack the cognitive diversity needed for rich thinking; groups of four allow disengagement. VRG needs to be applied every lesson or at minimum every task. It is not a novelty but a structural norm.

    The mechanisms by which VRG works are multiple and reinforcing. First, it breaks down fixed social hierarchies by ensuring that every student works with every other student over time, preventing calcified roles. Second, it increases "knowledge mobility"—ideas, strategies, and understanding flow through the classroom rather than remaining siloed within friendship groups. Third, because group placement is random, no student feels personally responsible for being "stuck" with particular peers; the social risk is lower, making intellectual risk-taking easier. Fourth, the norm of random assignment creates a universal expectation of participation; no one can opt out or hide. Finally, students report reduced anxiety when VRG is used consistently, because the predictability of the process (groups will be random every time) reduces anxiety about the outcome (who will I work with?).

    Vertical Non-Permanent Surfaces (Practice 3) operationalizes the insight that workspace configuration affects thinking. Liljedahl systematically tested two dimensions: permanence (whether work can be easily erased) and verticality (whether students sit or stand). The results were striking: standing at vertical erasable surfaces (whiteboards, blackboards, windows) produced more thinking and more sustained engagement than all other configurations.

    Non-permanence reduces psychological risk. On paper, every mark feels final and binding; a mistake is permanent evidence of failure. On a whiteboard, work is provisional. Students can erase, revise, and try again without the weight of a committed error. This shifts the mindset from "I must get this right" to "Let me try this and see." It invites productive struggle and normalizes revision.

    Verticality produces physical engagement and equality. When students stand, they cannot mentally withdraw while appearing to work—the body is activated. When work is at eye level and visible, no one can hide. Students in sitting groups often allow one or two members to do the thinking while others disengage; standing groups become more genuinely collaborative because physical access to the board is shared. Visibility also enables natural peer learning: students glance at neighbouring groups' work, see alternative strategies, and calibrate their own progress against peers without the social awkwardness of explicit comparison.

    When VNPS is combined with VRG, Liljedahl's research showed that students could sustain focused thinking for up to 60 minutes continuously—a striking finding in an era of perceived short attention spans. The combination works because randomness removes social anxiety, verticality ensures physical engagement, and non-permanence invites risk-taking.

    » In Practice

    A Year 9 mathematics teacher begins the lesson by drawing cards to assign students to groups of three. She gives the task verbally, standing in the room while students stand: "Use your whiteboard to show me a number that is 40 percent of 50. You can check your work before you show me." Groups approach the VNPS, discuss, try strategies, erase, revise. After eight minutes, all groups have produced correct answers. The non-permanent surface meant students could try, fail, try again without shame. The random grouping meant the quieter student had equal standing to contribute. The vertical arrangement meant everyone could see, point, and write.

    The Six Sustaining Practices

    Once thinking is initiated, the challenge becomes sustaining it. Liljedahl identified six practices that keep students engaged in thinking rather than reverting to procedural automation.

    The Defronted Classroom (Practice 4) is an architectural principle: arrange furniture so there is no "front" of the room. When the teacher's desk or central point is fixed, students orient toward it, adopting a passive reception posture. Defronting—creating a room where the teacher moves and groups work from multiple orientations—promotes active thinking over passive listening.

    Answering Only Keep-Thinking Questions (Practice 5) is a counterintuitive practice rooted in careful observation. Liljedahl found that teachers answer 200–400 questions daily, but 90 percent are not genuine questions. Many are "proximity questions" (asked simply because the teacher is physically near) or "stop-thinking questions" (asked to reduce effort, like "Is this right?"). These latter questions invite the teacher to do the thinking. When a student asks "Is this right?" the pedagogically powerful response is not to confirm or correct but to say, "How could you check that yourself?" or direct the student to compare with a peer's work. The teacher should answer only "keep-thinking questions"—those that move thinking forward, that students cannot reasonably solve alone, and that the answer genuinely addresses what the student needs.

    Giving Tasks Verbally, Early, Standing (Practice 6) has a precise implementation: tasks should be presented orally (not written on the board where students copy before thinking), delivered in the first five minutes of the lesson (not after a lengthy introduction), and given while students are standing in a loose formation (not sitting at desks). This timing and modality ensure maximum processing and engagement. Students hear the task while mentally alert, before passivity sets in, and standing creates a sense of immediacy and action.

    Meaningful Homework (Practice 7) reframes the conversation around out-of-class learning. Rather than "homework" (work done for the teacher), the practice rebrands assigned tasks as "check-your-understanding questions." The framing shift changes the student mindset from "I am doing this for the teacher, to avoid punishment" to "I am doing this to discover whether I understand." This positioning connects directly to self-regulated learning: students check their understanding because it serves them.

    Student Autonomy (Practice 8) encourages independence by reducing teacher dependency. When students ask the teacher a question, one response is: "Have you checked what your peers have done?" This simple redirection teaches students to use peer work as a resource, to learn from nearby groups, and to solve problems collectively rather than deferring to authority. Over time, this builds classroom knowledge as a shared resource that students draw upon rather than a privilege dispensed by the teacher.

    Hints and Extensions (Flow) (Practice 9) addresses the reality that different groups progress at different speeds. Rather than try to synchronize the whole class (an impossible task), Liljedahl recommends asynchronous scaffolding: have prepared hints available for groups that are stuck, and prepared extensions for groups that have finished. Hints might be visible on the board ("Try a simpler version first") or provided by the teacher as a brief prompt. Extensions might be deepening questions ("Can you prove that this always works?") or applications to new contexts. This practice replaces the myth of synchronous instruction with a system that keeps all groups in a state of flow—neither frustrated nor bored—through self-paced progression.

    The Five Consolidation Practices

    The final five practices capture and solidify thinking, transforming it from ephemeral class activity into durable understanding.

    Consolidating from the Bottom (Practice 10) inverts the typical lesson closure. Rather than the teacher announcing the correct method and students internalizing it, Liljedahl recommends building consolidation upward from student work. The teacher examines the solutions visible on VNPS, selects a piece of incomplete or partially correct student thinking, and uses it as the anchor for the lesson's synthesis. This ensures that consolidation is grounded in the actual thinking students have demonstrated, not in what the teacher assumes they should think. It also models for students how mathematical ideas are refined through building on imperfect attempts.

    Meaningful Notes (Practice 11) emerged from a surprising finding: when Liljedahl surveyed students about whether they looked back at notes, 80 percent of students never consulted their traditional notes again. However, students who created their own "notes to my future self"—deciding for themselves what future learning would require them to remember—consulted those notes at much higher rates. The metacognitive act of deciding what to record, how to phrase it, and what to highlight is more powerful than passive transcription. This aligns with encoding research: students who actively determine note content process at a deeper level than those who copy.

    Evaluating What You Value (Practice 12) addresses alignment between stated and assessed competencies. If the classroom culture emphasizes collaboration, risk-taking, and perseverance, then these must be assessed. Liljedahl found that when schools added rubric criteria for these competencies—when collaboration appeared on the grading rubric alongside content mastery—both student behaviour and learning improved. The assessment signal clarifies what the school actually values, not merely what it says it values.

    Formative Assessment for Learning (Practice 13) operationalizes the principle that assessment should inform students about their learning trajectory. When students know clearly where they are (what they can and cannot do) and where they are going (the learning goal), assessment becomes a tool for self-regulation rather than mere judgment. Research shows that when this clarity is high, test performance can improve 10–15 percent.

    Outcomes-Based Grading (Practice 14) shifts from recording isolated events ("Student scored 73 percent on the test on March 15") to aggregating evidence of progress toward specific outcomes. Rather than averaging all scores equally, outcomes-based grading asks: "What is the most recent, highest-quality evidence that the student can do X?" This supports growth mindset by making it clear that performance can change and that current evidence is most relevant. It also distributes formative feedback throughout the year rather than concentrating judgement at test time.

    Evidence and Alignment with Cognitive Science

    Liljedahl's research was conducted across hundreds of classrooms over 15 years using iterative design-based methodology: practices were tested, refined, and retested in diverse settings. The measured outcomes include increased time spent thinking, increased willingness to engage, improved collaboration, greater risk-taking, reduced anxiety, and higher achievement on both procedural and conceptual assessments.

    The framework also aligns strongly with findings from cognitive science. VNPS combined with VRG promote deep processing by requiring active engagement with material. Thinking tasks demand retrieval and application rather than passive reception, aligning with retrieval practice research. Meaningful notes engage metacognitive regulation—students must plan what to record and evaluate its usefulness. Formative assessment supports self-regulated learning by providing the feedback loop that learners need to adjust strategy.

    ? For Reflection

    In your current classroom, how much time do students spend in behaviour that involves thinking versus procedural copying or waiting? If you implemented VRG and VNPS tomorrow, what barriers or concerns would emerge in the first week? How might you address them?

    Connection to Danish Educational Values

    Building Thinking Classrooms aligns deeply with Danish educational principles. The emphasis on fællesskab (community) is operationalized through VRG: every student works with every other student, building bonds across social lines and demonstrating that everyone belongs. Demokratisk dannelse (democratic formation) is embedded in the practice structure: random grouping treats all students as equal participants; defronting distributes power throughout the room; and student autonomy develops the judgment and voice central to democratic citizenship. Ligeværd (equal worth) is communicated through the structural expectation that every student is a valued thinking partner, not a passive recipient or a second-class participant.
