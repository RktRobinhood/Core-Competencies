    Chapter 8 — Leadership, Communication, and Global Competence

    ◉ Thesis

    Schools that reserve leadership for students who hold formal positions, treat communication as self-expression rather than argumentation, and approach global competence as cultural awareness rather than developmental transformation are underdeveloping three competencies that students will need every day of their adult lives. Leadership is a set of learnable practices available to every student, not a trait possessed by a few. Communication at its most consequential involves navigating disagreement, not merely articulating ideas. And global competence requires a developmental journey from ethnocentrism to genuine perspective-taking — a journey that does not happen through exposure alone. Each requires the same explicit, structured instruction that every competency in this curriculum demands. And none can be taught credibly by adults who do not practise it themselves.

    Leadership Is a Practice, Not a Position

    The popular image of leadership — the charismatic individual at the front of the room — is not merely incomplete; it actively misleads. It suggests that leadership is a fixed trait: you either have it or you don't. Contemporary research tells a different story. Leadership comprises a set of practices that can be identified, taught, and developed in every student.

    Kouzes and Posner (2018), in their adaptation for student contexts, identify five practices that constitute leadership. Model the Way means demonstrating through action the values you ask of others — a student leader who works hard, acts with integrity, and shows respect is leading regardless of whether she holds a title. Inspire a Shared Vision means helping others understand a compelling goal and their role in achieving it. Challenge the Process means questioning the status quo — asking "Why do we do this?" and proposing alternatives. Enable Others to Act means creating conditions where others can succeed: sharing responsibility, providing support, removing obstacles. Encourage the Heart means recognising effort and celebrating progress.

    The critical insight is that these are not the practices of a president or captain. Every student can model values, challenge processes, enable others' action, and encourage effort. The shift from "leadership roles" to "leadership practices" democratises leadership — and in a classroom context, this means every student can be developing leadership every day, not only the few who win elections or receive appointments.

    Spillane's (2006) concept of distributed leadership reinforces this. In effective teams, leadership emerges where it is needed: one person leads planning, another leads problem-solving, another ensures everyone is heard. No single person leads everything, and the quality of the group's work depends on leadership being shared rather than concentrated. Schools that develop distributed leadership see stronger outcomes than those where leadership is a position occupied by one.

    ℹ Key Concept — Distributed Leadership

    Distributed leadership describes leadership as shared across many people rather than concentrated in a formal leader (Spillane, 2006). In well-functioning teams, different members lead at different moments depending on the task and their strengths. This model is not merely more democratic; it is more effective — because no individual possesses all the competencies every situation demands.

    The Ownership Principle

    The OECD Learning Compass 2030 (OECD, 2019) positions "student agency" as foundational — the capacity to set goals, reflect, and act responsibly. Agency and leadership are inseparable: a student with agency does not wait for direction; she identifies what matters, sets goals, and works toward them.

    Jocko Willink's Extreme Ownership framework (Willink & Babin, 2015) sharpens this into a behavioural practice: the most effective leaders take full responsibility for everything in their domain, including team failures. Rather than assigning blame, the ownership-oriented leader asks what she could have done differently in communication, preparation, or support. For students, this translates to a powerful shift — from "the group didn't work well together" to "what could I have done to help the group work better?" The practice develops the internal locus of control and reflective habit that make distributed leadership effective in reality, not just in theory.

    The principle applies equally to teachers. A teacher who blames students for poor performance without examining her own instruction is not modelling ownership. A teacher who asks "What could I have done differently to help them succeed?" is — and students notice the difference. The full treatment of Willink's discipline and ownership frameworks, including their connection to resilience and productive struggle, appears in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit.

    Teachers as Leaders: The Authenticity Test

    Leadership development cannot be taught by someone who does not embody it. Simon Sinek captures the essential distinction: "Managers work to see numbers grow. Leaders work to see people grow" (Sinek, 2014). Applied to education, the distinction is uncomfortable. Too many schools run like businesses — obsessed with metrics (grades, pass rates, university placements) rather than the development of the humans inside the system. Sinek argues that teachers become teachers because they care about people, and that the system's fixation on measurement erodes exactly the relational work that makes teaching effective.

    The implication cuts both ways. If we expect students to develop leadership, they must be surrounded by adults who are visibly developing too. This means teachers who model vulnerability, seek feedback on their own practice, take ownership when things go wrong, and demonstrate that growth is a lifelong project, not something that ends with a degree. Kouzes and Posner's first practice — Model the Way — applies to adults as much as to students.

    Sinek's infinite game framework (Sinek, 2019) deepens this. Education is inherently an infinite game — there is no winning, only the ongoing development of people. An infinite-minded school leader does not ask "Did we hit our targets?" but "Are our people — teachers and students alike — developing in ways that will matter in ten years?" When a principal takes care of teachers, teachers take care of students. When teachers model the competencies they are trying to develop — critical thinking, ethical reasoning, genuine collaboration — students see that these are lived practices, not academic exercises.

    ⚠ The Authenticity Test

    Students are experts at detecting hypocrisy. A teacher who demands self-regulation but loses her temper, who promotes growth mindset but punishes failure, who preaches collaboration but dominates meetings, teaches a hidden curriculum that contradicts the explicit one. The most powerful leadership development tool available to any school is the visible, honest growth of the adults within it.

    Communication Is Not Self-Expression

    The Partnership for 21st Century Learning defines communication as "articulating thoughts effectively across oral, written, and nonverbal forms." The definition is accurate as far as it goes, but it understates the complexity of the competency that students actually need. The communication challenges of adult life are not primarily about expressing oneself clearly. They are about arguing well, listening carefully, and navigating disagreement — capacities that require their own instruction.

    Academic argumentation involves building evidence-based claims with supporting evidence and acknowledgment of counterarguments. This is fundamentally different from expressing an opinion. A student who can say "I think X" has communicated. A student who can say "X is supported by evidence A and B, while the counterargument Y has merit but is weakened by C" has argued — and the difference matters for every professional and civic context she will enter.

    Listening as an active, empathic process is the neglected half of communication. Most instruction focuses on output — speaking and writing — while treating listening as passive reception. Active listening involves understanding not merely what someone says but what they mean, what they feel, and what they are trying to accomplish. It is a skill that develops through practice and feedback, not through the instruction to "listen carefully."

    Visual and digital communication — using data visualisation, infographics, and platform-appropriate formats — has become increasingly important as information becomes more visual and media more varied. A professional email differs from a social media post, which differs from a formal presentation. Each has conventions, and students who have never been taught those conventions learn them through trial and error at professional cost.

    Importantly, communication is disciplinary. How a mathematician communicates differs from how a historian or artist communicates. A mathematical proof has a different structure than a historical argument than a literary interpretation. This is why communication taught in isolation — generic "presentation skills" sessions — transfers less effectively than communication taught within the disciplines where it matters (see Ch-04-Critical-Thinking for the parallel domain-specificity argument).

    The Skill of Difficult Conversations

    One of the most consequential communication competencies — for both teachers and students — is the ability to navigate uncomfortable conversations. This is not a peripheral social skill; it is a core leadership competency. Leaders who avoid difficult conversations erode trust, allow problems to compound, and model avoidance to those they lead. Students who never learn to raise concerns, give honest feedback, or disagree constructively enter adult life without one of the most important capacities that adult life demands.

    Sinek introduces the FBI method as a practical framework: Feelings, Behaviour, Impact (Sinek, 2023). Communicate how a specific action affected your emotional state — feelings cannot be invalidated because they belong to you. Identify the specific, observable behaviour that prompted those feelings, avoiding generalisations and character judgments. Explain the impact — what consequences you fear if the behaviour continues, and what it means for the relationship or shared work.

    The Harvard Negotiation Project provides a complementary and more deeply researched framework. Stone, Patton, and Heen (1999) found that every difficult conversation is actually three simultaneous conversations: the "What happened?" conversation (disagreement about facts and interpretation), the Feelings conversation (emotions triggered but often unexpressed), and the Identity conversation (the threat to each person's sense of self — am I competent? am I a good person?). When we focus only on "what happened," we miss the emotional and identity dimensions that actually drive the conflict. The goal is to shift from delivering a message to understanding a situation — from a "stance of certainty" to a "stance of curiosity."

    Patterson and colleagues (2012) add a structural insight from research with over 10,000 professionals: the conversations that matter most are those where stakes are high, opinions differ, and emotions run strong. Individuals who handle these crucial conversations well earn more, are promoted more often, and maintain stronger relationships. Their framework emphasises making it safe — establishing mutual purpose and mutual respect before addressing content.

    » In Practice — The Difficult Conversation Protocol

    For staff development: Introduce the FBI method in a professional development session. Have teachers practise in low-stakes triads: one delivers feedback, one receives, one observes and debriefs. Start with workplace scenarios before moving to higher-stakes situations.

    For students: Teach the three-conversation model (Stone, Patton, & Heen) explicitly. Have students identify the "What happened?", "Feelings," and "Identity" layers in a case study before attempting their own conversations. Role-play with gradually increasing stakes. The skill transfers directly to TOK, where students must hold multiple perspectives, tolerate disagreement, and argue without attacking.

    The deeper implication connects to the teachers-as-leaders principle. A school that wants students to handle disagreement constructively must be a place where adults model it. If staff meetings are dominated by avoidance or surface agreement, the unspoken message is that difficult conversations are too dangerous. A staff culture where disagreement is welcomed, feedback is specific and kind, and vulnerability is treated as strength creates conditions in which students learn the skill by immersion — not just by instruction.

    Global Competence: Beyond Cultural Tourism

    Students who study other cultures in a textbook, taste international foods at a school event, and learn a few phrases in another language have been exposed to cultural difference. They have not necessarily developed intercultural competence. The distinction matters because exposure without structured reflection produces familiarity, not understanding.

    Deardorff (2006) proposes a process model that begins with attitudes — openness, respect, and curiosity — and develops through knowledge and skills (understanding one's own culture and others', cross-cultural communication) to produce internal outcomes (empathy, adaptability) and external outcomes (effective communication across cultures, relationship-building). The model is cyclical: increased competence increases willingness to engage, which develops further competence. But the cycle does not start spontaneously; it requires deliberate activation.

    Bennett's (1993) Developmental Model of Intercultural Sensitivity maps the progression from ethnocentrism to ethnorelativism — from seeing one's own culture as the natural centre of things to genuinely valuing and operating within multiple cultural frames. The ethnocentric stages — denial (unaware of difference), defence (aware but defensive), minimisation (acknowledging difference but treating it as superficial) — describe where most students begin. The ethnorelative stages — acceptance (recognising and valuing genuine difference), adaptation (adjusting behaviour to interact effectively), integration (comfortably functioning across cultural contexts) — describe where intentional development can take them.

    The progression is not automatic. It requires structured exposure, guided reflection, and the kind of cognitive conflict that the negotiated spaces of Ch-05-Collaboration-and-Teamwork describe. International collaboration projects, diverse source materials, explicit discussion of cultural assumptions, and language learning all contribute — but only when accompanied by reflection that moves students beyond "that's interesting" to "that challenges how I see things."

    » In Practice

    A Danish student studying Chinese approaches to education might begin with curiosity about a different way of organising learning. Through study, interaction with Chinese peers, reading Chinese authors' own perspectives, and structured reflection, she develops understanding of Chinese values and reasoning patterns. Over time, she can shift perspectives — understanding not just what Chinese people do but why these approaches make sense from within a Chinese worldview. This is not abandoning Danish identity; it is holding multiple perspectives simultaneously — the intellectual flexibility that genuine global competence requires.

    The IB's emphasis on "international-mindedness" (IBO, 2015) and the Folkeskole's commitment to giving students "an understanding of other countries and cultures" (Danish Ministry of Education, 2014) both aim at this capacity. The question is whether schools develop it through structured, developmental instruction or leave it to the hope that exposure will suffice. The evidence from every competency in this curriculum points in the same direction: hope is not a strategy.

    Danish Communication Traditions

    Danish education builds on communication traditions that are philosophically deeper than "classroom discussion." The concept of samtalekultur (conversation culture) frames dialogue not as a pedagogical technique but as an expression of values about how humans should relate — values rooted in the dannelse tradition (see Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation).

    The Folkeskole tradition emphasises student voice, classroom discussion, and democratic dialogue. These are understood as expressions of fællesskab (community) — the conviction that learning happens through genuine exchange, that different perspectives are necessary rather than merely tolerated, and that the quality of a community is measured by the quality of its conversations. This provides a cultural foundation for communication instruction that goes beyond skills to virtues: respect, intellectual humility, and the willingness to be changed by what one hears.

    Connections

    Leadership, communication, and global competence build on the foundation of critical thinking, collaboration, and self-directed learning, extending these competencies into contemporary global contexts. The teachers-as-leaders principle connects to Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit (Willink's extreme ownership), Ch-24-Effective-Feedback (feedback as difficult conversation), Ch-28-Reflective-Practice (adult growth as modelling), and Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation (formation requires authentic exemplars). The difficult conversations framework connects to TOK assessment objectives on multiple perspectives and argumentation (see A1-Curriculum-Hook-Map). These competencies are central to the frameworks examined in Chapter 9 and reflect the interconnected world students are entering.
