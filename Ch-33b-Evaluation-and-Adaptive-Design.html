    Chapter 33b — Evaluation and Adaptive Design: Implementation Without Measurement Is Hope, Not Strategy

    ◉ Thesis

    The most common defence for not evaluating competency education is that the outcomes are too complex to measure. This is half true and wholly dangerous. Critical thinking, metacognition, and collaboration are harder to measure than content recall — but the OECD's 2022 PISA creative thinking assessment demonstrated that complex competencies can be assessed at scale when sufficient investment is made, and validated instruments exist for nearly every competency in this textbook. The real danger is not imperfect measurement but no measurement at all: schools that do not track whether their implementation is working will not know it has failed until the lagging indicators — graduation rates, examination results, student disengagement — confirm what leading indicators would have revealed two years earlier. This sub-chapter argues for a measurement portfolio approach that is rigorous enough to detect change, adaptive enough to guide mid-course correction, and honest enough to acknowledge what it cannot capture.

    Danish School Culture Makes This Easier — But Not Automatic

    A staged implementation approach fits Danish school cultures that value professional collaboration, distributed leadership, and continuous improvement. Danish teachers are accustomed to working in teams and having autonomy over pedagogy. The NIRN and EEF frameworks respect professional expertise while providing structure to ensure that innovations become reliable practices, not one-off experiments.

    For a school expanding the IB course on core competencies, implementation science provides the bridge between policy aspiration (the 2014 Folkeskole reform (Danish Ministry of Education, 2014), the dannelse tradition (Klafki, 2001), the competency goal structure) and classroom reality (teachers using competency language, assessing competencies, helping students develop them reliably). Without this bridge, competency education remains rhetoric rather than reality.

    The Measurement Challenge: Difficult Does Not Mean Impossible

    Implementation without evaluation is hope, not strategy. Yet evaluation in competency-based education presents a distinctive challenge: the outcomes that matter most — growth in critical thinking, collaboration, metacognition, self-regulation — are precisely those that resist easy measurement. This section addresses the evaluation challenge directly, drawing on implementation science, psychometrics, and project management thinking to propose an approach that is rigorous, adaptive, and honest about its own limitations.

    The Honest Acknowledgement: What We Want to Develop Is Hard to Measure

    The first honest acknowledgement is that measuring competency development is genuinely difficult. Traditional achievement tests capture content knowledge reliably but tell us little about whether students are thinking more critically, collaborating more effectively, or regulating their own learning with greater sophistication. The gap between what we want to develop and what we can measure is the central tension in competency-based evaluation.

    The OECD has stated the principle clearly: we must measure what matters, not simply what is easy to measure (OECD, 2023). Their 2022 PISA creative thinking assessment — which tested over 140,000 teenagers across sixty countries on their ability to generate, evaluate, and refine ideas — represents a paradigm shift: the first large-scale international attempt to measure a complex competency alongside traditional academic domains. The assessment used scenario-based tasks across written expression, visual expression, social problem-solving, and scientific problem-solving, demonstrating that complex competencies can be assessed at scale when sufficient conceptual and technological investment is made (OECD, 2022).

    For schools, the practical challenge is smaller in scale but no less important. Validated instruments exist for specific competencies. Critical thinking can be assessed through performance-based rubrics using analytic scales with exemplars. Metacognition can be measured through behavioural traces — observable indicators such as underlining, note-taking patterns, self-questioning, and planning behaviours — which research shows have higher effect sizes than self-report questionnaires (Winne & Perry, 2000). Collaboration can be assessed through structured observation using instruments such as the Teamwork Assessment Scale (TAS), a 14-item observational tool for team functioning. Systems thinking can be assessed through scenario-based tasks with open-ended responses and rubric scoring — measuring students' ability to identify variables, map relationships, and recognise feedback loops.

    The critical point is that no single instrument captures competency development adequately. Schools need a measurement portfolio that combines multiple methods: formative classroom observations, performance tasks, student self-assessments, and periodic summative measures. This is more work than a standardised test, but it is the work that competency-based education requires.

    Leading Indicators Predict Success; Lagging Indicators Only Confirm Failure

    A distinction from quality management and project planning proves directly useful for schools: the difference between leading indicators and lagging indicators.

    Lagging indicators are easy to measure but difficult to change in real time. They tell you what has already happened: graduation rates, standardised test scores, final examination results, end-of-year competency ratings. By the time lagging indicators reveal a problem, the window for intervention has often closed.

    Leading indicators are harder to measure but predictive of future outcomes. They provide early signals that enable course correction: the quality of student discourse in problem-solving tasks, evidence of metacognitive strategy use in classwork, students' willingness to engage with productive struggle rather than seeking immediate answers, teacher-student relationship quality, and fidelity of implementation of targeted practices.

    For a school implementing competency-based education, leading indicators matter more than lagging ones during the first two years. If teachers are implementing thinking tasks with high fidelity, if students are demonstrating increased metacognitive self-monitoring in their classwork, if collaborative tasks are producing genuine interdependence rather than parallel individual work — these are the signals that predict eventual outcome improvements. Schools that track only lagging indicators will not know whether their implementation is working until it is too late to adjust.

    » In Practice — Leading Indicator Dashboard

    A school's implementation team creates a simple leading indicator dashboard updated fortnightly. It tracks three metrics: (1) percentage of observed lessons where targeted competency practices are implemented with high fidelity (target: 80%); (2) frequency of student metacognitive behaviours observed during classroom walkthroughs (using a brief checklist: self-questioning, planning before acting, revising after feedback); (3) student self-reported sense of academic challenge on a 1–5 scale (target: sustained increase over baseline). None of these metrics requires expensive assessment tools — they require consistent observation protocols and a willingness to use data formatively.

    Growth Requires Multiple Measurements — And Realistic Expectations

    When schools do collect quantitative data on competency development, the statistical approach matters. Two principles should guide the work.

    First, growth requires multiple time points. Pre-post designs (measuring once before and once after an intervention) provide limited information. They cannot distinguish genuine growth from measurement noise, and they reveal nothing about the trajectory of change — whether growth was linear, whether it accelerated, or whether it plateaued. Latent growth curve modelling, which estimates individual growth trajectories across three or more time points, provides substantially richer information about both average growth and individual variation (Morris & DeShon, 2002). Schools implementing competency assessment should plan for at least three measurement points: baseline, mid-implementation, and end-of-cycle.

    Second, effect sizes in education are smaller than people expect. Cohen's (1988) conventional thresholds — small (d = 0.2), medium (d = 0.5), large (d = 0.8) — were developed for laboratory experiments. In field-based educational interventions, effects of d = 0.2 to 0.3 represent meaningful practical improvements. Kraft (2020) argues that education researchers and practitioners should recalibrate their expectations: an effect size of 0.2 standard deviations sustained over a school year represents a significant achievement. Schools that expect dramatic, immediately visible gains from competency-based education will be disappointed; schools that track modest but sustained growth over multiple cycles will find genuine evidence of impact.

    Evaluation Should Drive Improvement, Not Retrospective Judgment

    The most important shift in evaluation thinking for competency-based education is from summative judgment to iterative learning. The explicit assumption should be that initial implementation will not be perfect — and the evaluation system should be designed to support continuous improvement rather than retrospective accountability.

    The Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle, already established in implementation science (see Ch-33a-Frameworks-and-Blueprint), provides the operational framework. Each improvement cycle includes a planning phase (identifying a specific practice to implement or refine), a delivery phase (implementing with fidelity monitoring), a study phase (collecting and analysing data on both fidelity and outcomes), and an action phase (making targeted adjustments for the next cycle). The study phase is where evaluation happens — but it is evaluation in the service of improvement, not judgment.

    This iterative approach borrows from agile project management methodology, which has been adapted for educational contexts with promising results. The core agile insight is that complex projects — and implementing competency-based education is undeniably complex — cannot be fully planned in advance. They require tight feedback loops, short implementation cycles (analogous to "sprints"), regular retrospectives where the implementation team reflects on what worked and what didn't, and explicit permission to adjust course based on evidence (Dingsøyr, Nerur, Balijepally & Moe, 2012).

    Fixsen et al. (2005) found that effective implementation typically takes two to four years to reach full fidelity — and can take longer to fully embed into everyday practice. This timeline should be communicated honestly to staff, parents, and leadership. Schools that expect transformation within a single academic year are setting themselves up for demoralisation. Schools that plan for iterative improvement across multiple years, with evaluation data driving each cycle, are far more likely to achieve lasting change.

    Measure Implementation First — Because Poor Fidelity Looks Like a Failed Practice

    Proctor et al. (2011) proposed a framework that distinguishes three types of outcomes — a framework that clarifies what schools should be measuring at each stage of implementation.

    Implementation outcomes assess whether the practice is being delivered as intended. These include acceptability (do teachers and students find the practice reasonable and satisfying?), adoption (what proportion of intended practitioners are using it?), appropriateness (does it fit the school context?), feasibility (can it realistically be sustained given available resources?), fidelity (are the active ingredients present?), and cost (what resources does it require?). Programs implemented with high fidelity show effect sizes two to three times larger than those implemented with lower fidelity — establishing a direct link between implementation quality and student outcomes (Proctor et al., 2011).

    Service outcomes assess the quality and timeliness of implementation processes — whether coaching is happening as planned, whether materials are available, whether the implementation team is meeting regularly.

    Student outcomes assess changes in student learning, wellbeing, and behaviour — the lagging indicators that ultimately justify the effort.

    The critical insight is sequencing: implementation outcomes should be tracked first, because they are prerequisites for student outcomes. A school that finds poor student outcomes but has not tracked implementation fidelity cannot distinguish between a practice that doesn't work and a practice that was never properly implemented. Fidelity measurement across five dimensions — adherence, dosage, quality of delivery, differentiation from existing practice, and participant responsiveness — provides the diagnostic information needed to identify and address implementation weaknesses before they undermine outcomes (Proctor et al., 2011).

    One Size Does Not Fit All: Adaptive Interventions for Individual Students

    The most sophisticated approach to evaluation in competency-based education is adaptive intervention design — an approach borrowed from clinical research that acknowledges individual variation in response to educational practices.

    Rather than assuming one fixed intervention will work for all students, adaptive interventions contain decision points (specific times to assess progress and make intervention decisions), intervention options (multiple treatment paths or dosages available), and feedback loops (data collected at each decision point to tailor the next phase). Intervention options — types, intensities, modalities — are sequentially tailored over time based on individual student response, contextual factors, and real-time performance data.

    For schools, this means building in structured decision points — perhaps at the end of each quarter — where the implementation team reviews student-level data and adjusts the approach for students who are not responding as expected. Some students may need more scaffolding in metacognitive instruction. Others may need different collaborative structures. The evaluation system should generate the data needed to make these decisions, not merely the data needed to report to external stakeholders.

    ℹ Key Concept — The RE-AIM Framework

    The RE-AIM framework (Glasgow, Vogt & Boles, 1999) provides a comprehensive structure for evaluating school improvement programmes. It assesses five dimensions: Reach (what proportion of the intended audience is participating?), Effectiveness (to what degree does the intervention change outcomes?), Adoption (what proportion of intended practitioners are implementing?), Implementation (is delivery faithful and high-quality?), and Maintenance (are effects sustained over time?). Reach, Adoption, and Implementation function as leading indicators; Effectiveness and Maintenance function as lagging indicators. Schools that evaluate across all five dimensions get a complete picture of whether their competency-based education is working — and why.

    What This Means for Schools Using This Textbook

    This course on core competencies has embedded in it a 150-hour implementation. Teachers and leaders will use the course materials to understand what the competencies are, why they matter, and how to teach them. But understanding is not implementation. Schools using this course should:

    1. Use implementation science deliberately. Do not assume that distributing course materials results in competency teaching. Plan for the explore-prepare-deliver-sustain sequence (see Ch-33a-Frameworks-and-Blueprint).
    2. Form implementation teams that own the work and provide ongoing support.
    3. Select high-leverage practices from the course (e.g., think-alouds for metacognition, visible random groups for collaboration) and implement them well in one context before expanding.
    4. Track fidelity, not only outcomes. Use the practices as they are designed; observe lessons to ensure active ingredients are present.
    5. Celebrate and share early wins as the work progresses.
