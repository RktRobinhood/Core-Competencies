Part I — The Case for Change

Schools have traditionally organised themselves around disciplines: history, literature, mathematics, science. Students progress through these subjects, accumulating content knowledge. But research and practice increasingly suggest that this is insufficient. The students who thrive in complex, knowledge-based societies are those who can think critically, communicate persuasively, collaborate effectively, and adapt to change. These competencies develop neither automatically nor through exposure alone. They require intentional, explicit teaching grounded in evidence.

Part I makes the case for why a Danish school should commit to a dedicated, systematic competency curriculum.

What This Part Covers

Ch-01-Introduction establishes why core competencies matter. It shows that the research is clear: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and other competencies must be explicitly taught. It introduces the role of cognitive science in understanding how learning works. It presents Building Thinking Classrooms as a transformative model for classroom practice. And it explains how the Danish concept of dannelse provides a philosophical foundation that extends beyond international frameworks.

Ch-02-21st-Century-Skills-Landscape maps four major international competency frameworks—P21, ATC21S, OECD Learning Compass, and IB ATL—showing where they converge and where they differ. The convergence across independent frameworks on core competencies (critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity) signals something fundamental about what matters for human flourishing. This chapter also engages with criticisms of competency frameworks and explains how Danish educational values deepen rather than replace international research.

Ch-03-Skills-at-Risk-in-a-Digital-Age examines what threatens competency development. While digital technology offers learning benefits, research identifies genuine risks to deep reading, sustained attention, handwriting, source evaluation, and independent thinking. This chapter distinguishes real concerns from overblown narratives, explains the mechanisms at work, and proposes strategies for protection through intentional design. It also addresses how generative AI accelerates some of these challenges and how the concept of digital dannelse provides a response.

The Argument in Brief

  1. Competencies do not develop through exposure alone. They require explicit instruction, modelling, and scaffolding. This is the most consistent research finding across domains.

  2. Multiple frameworks converge on core competencies: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity. This convergence suggests they identify something fundamental.

  3. Danish dannelse provides philosophical depth. It frames competency development not as workforce preparation but as formation of the whole person—intellectually, ethically, culturally, and socially.

  4. Digital technology presents real challenges that require protection of specific skills (deep reading, sustained attention, handwriting, source evaluation) through intentional classroom design.

  5. Intentional design is the answer—not technology rejection, but deliberate choices about how technology is integrated, when analogue approaches serve learning better, and how core competencies are protected and developed.

How This Part Connects to the Rest of the Curriculum

Part I establishes why competency education matters. Part II (Foundations of Learning and Cognition) provides the scientific evidence for how learning works. Part III (The Core Competencies) teaches each competency explicitly: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity, and metacognition. Part IV (Implementing Competency Education) addresses systemic design—how to align curriculum, assessment, and professional development to support competency development school-wide.

Each chapter in Part I connects to subsequent chapters, particularly to the detailed explorations in Part III. For example, Chapter 1's discussion of explicit instruction informs Ch-04-Critical-Thinking. Chapter 3's concern about deep reading connects to Ch-04-Critical-Thinking and Ch-03-Skills-at-Risk-in-a-Digital-Age. The framework overview in Chapter 2 provides the structure within which all subsequent competencies are situated.

Begin with Ch-01-Introduction to understand why this work matters.
