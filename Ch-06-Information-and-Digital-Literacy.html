    Chapter 6 — Information and Digital Literacy: Navigating a World of Abundance

    ◉ Thesis

    Today's students have access to more information than any generation in history, and they are worse at evaluating it than schools assume. The "digital native" myth — the belief that early exposure to technology produces critical digital competence — has been empirically dismantled, yet it continues to excuse schools from teaching what students cannot learn on their own. Research from Stanford shows that even university students rely on surface-level cues to judge credibility, while professional fact-checkers use fundamentally different strategies that can be taught. Information and digital literacy are not library skills or IT competencies; they are the survival competencies of a democratic society saturated with misinformation, algorithmic curation, and AI-generated content. Schools that do not teach them explicitly are sending students into an information environment they are not equipped to navigate.

    The Dangerous Confidence of the Uninstructed

    The most alarming finding in information literacy research is not that students struggle to evaluate sources — it is that they believe they are good at it. This confidence-competence gap makes the problem self-concealing: students who overestimate their ability see no reason to develop it further, and teachers who assume digital fluency equals digital literacy see no reason to teach it.

    Wineburg, McGrew, and colleagues (2016, 2019) at the Stanford History Education Group tested how students — including university students at elite institutions — evaluate online sources. The results were sobering. Students relied on surface-level cues: if a website looked professional, they assumed it was credible. If a claim appeared in multiple places, they assumed it was true. When asked to evaluate a photograph claiming to show an environmental disaster, most did not think to do a reverse image search to verify the image's origin and date. They read vertically — staying within the source, examining its internal features — rather than laterally, which is what professional fact-checkers do.

    The contrast with expert practice is instructive. Fact-checkers do not read deeply into a source while on that site. They leave immediately — opening new tabs, searching for information about the organisation behind the claim, checking what other credible sources say, looking for counterarguments. Wineburg and McGrew (2019) call this "lateral reading," and it proves far more effective at identifying misinformation than the vertical deep-reading strategies schools traditionally teach. The irony is significant: the approach that sounds more intellectually rigorous — close, careful reading of the source — is the less effective strategy for determining whether the source deserves careful reading in the first place.

    ℹ Key Concept — Lateral Reading

    Lateral reading is the source evaluation strategy used by professional fact-checkers (Wineburg & McGrew, 2019). Rather than reading a source deeply while on the source itself, lateral readers leave the website to verify claims through external searches and cross-references. They ask: Who is behind this organisation? What do other sources say about this claim? Are there counterarguments? What is the author's expertise? This practice, counterintuitive to traditional deep-reading advice, is remarkably effective — and teachable.

    The "Digital Native" Myth and Its Costs

    The assumption that young people are "digital natives" — that growing up with technology produces digital competence — has done measurable harm to information literacy education. Kirschner and De Bruyckere (2017) dismantle the myth empirically. Young people may use devices fluently, but fluency with devices does not translate to critical understanding of digital systems, responsible use, or evaluative skill. A student who can navigate Instagram, edit a video, and manage multiple group chats has demonstrated operational digital skill. She has not demonstrated the ability to evaluate whether a news source is reliable, to understand how algorithmic curation shapes her information diet, or to recognise when AI-generated content is passing as human-authored.

    The pedagogical cost is real. When schools assume digital competence is innate, they do not teach it. When they do not teach it, the students who develop strong information literacy are those whose home environments provide it — families where source evaluation, critical media consumption, and scepticism toward claims are modelled daily. Students without these advantages are left to navigate the information environment with surface-level heuristics that actively mislead them. The equity implications mirror the argument made about critical thinking in Ch-04-Critical-Thinking: implicit approaches reproduce existing inequalities, and explicit instruction is the corrective.

    McGrew and colleagues (2019) document the scale of the problem: students who can identify a URL or check when a website was last updated believe they are evaluating sources critically, when in fact these surface checks are insufficient. The gap between students' confidence and their actual competence is not small; it is large enough to constitute a civic vulnerability.

    What Information and Digital Literacy Actually Require

    The terms "information literacy" and "digital literacy" are related but distinct, and conflating them produces incomplete instruction.

    Information literacy is the ability to recognise an information need, locate information from diverse sources, critically evaluate that information, and use it effectively. It is primarily cognitive — the skills of evaluation, synthesis, and judgment that apply regardless of the medium. A student evaluating a printed historical document and a student evaluating an online news article are exercising the same core competency, even though the specific cues differ.

    Digital literacy extends this to the tools and environments through which information is increasingly accessed and created. It includes navigating digital platforms, protecting personal data, creating digital content responsibly, and — crucially — understanding how algorithms shape what we see. The European Commission's DigComp 2.2 framework (Vuorikari, Kluzer, & Punie, 2022) maps five competence areas: information and data literacy, communication and collaboration, digital content creation, safety, and problem-solving. This framework is widely adopted across Europe and provides useful shared language, though like all frameworks it requires translation into specific classroom practices to be actionable.

    The integration of these literacies matters. A student might possess strong information literacy — able to evaluate claims, identify bias, assess evidence — but lack the digital literacy to understand that what she sees online has been algorithmically selected to match her existing preferences. Conversely, a student might understand algorithmic curation in the abstract but lack the evaluative skills to assess the content those algorithms deliver. The competencies are complementary, and instruction that addresses one without the other leaves students partially equipped.

    All Media Is Constructed — And Students Need to See the Construction

    Information literacy extends beyond evaluating written sources. All media — visual, audio, video, interactive — is constructed by people with perspectives, purposes, and constraints. An advertisement, a news article, a documentary, and an Instagram post all involve choices about what to include and exclude, what to emphasise and downplay. Media literacy means making those choices visible.

    The P21 framework (Battelle for Kids, 2019) distinguishes between media literacy (analysing and creating media products) and ICT literacy (applying technology effectively). A student might know how to use social media platforms technically but not understand how algorithmic curation shapes what she sees — a gap between ICT literacy and media literacy that has consequences for how she understands the world.

    » In Practice

    In a Danish studies class, students research how digital platforms covered a recent environmental policy debate. Media literacy involves asking: How did different news sources frame the issue? Which voices were included and which excluded? What visual choices — photographs, graphics, video clips — were made, and how do they shape the emotional response? How did algorithms on social media determine what different students saw about the same event? Rather than simply gathering "all perspectives," students learn to recognise that media construction itself shapes public understanding — and that their own information diet is not neutral.

    The Emerging Frontier: AI-Generated Content and Algorithmic Curation

    The information landscape is not static, and two developments are reshaping what digital literacy must address.

    AI-generated content introduces unprecedented complexity to source evaluation. When a text, image, or video can be created entirely by artificial intelligence — without a human author responsible for truth claims — traditional evaluation strategies must adapt. "Who created this?" becomes harder to answer. "What is their expertise?" may be meaningless. "What are their motives?" requires understanding the training data and deployment context of an AI system, not the biography of an author. Students will need frameworks for evaluating content provenance and authenticity that go beyond anything current curricula teach. The implications are still unfolding, but the direction is clear: the evaluation challenge is becoming harder, not easier, and the need for explicit instruction is intensifying (see Ch-32-AI-in-Education for the broader context).

    Algorithmic curation and filter bubbles present a subtler threat. When algorithms determine what information to show based on past behaviour and engagement metrics, users see a curated version of reality that reinforces their existing views. Diverse perspectives are filtered out not by censorship but by optimisation — the algorithm shows what keeps users engaged, and agreement is more engaging than challenge. Students must develop awareness that what they see online is not the full picture of available information, and that their own preferences actively shape what algorithms deliver. This represents a new form of literacy: understanding algorithmic systems as mediators of knowledge, not neutral windows onto it.

    ⚠ The Stakes Are Democratic

    Information literacy has always mattered for education. In a digital age, it matters for democracy. A population that cannot evaluate sources, recognise algorithmic manipulation, or distinguish authentic content from AI-generated fabrication is a population vulnerable to manipulation on a scale previous generations did not face. The Danish tradition of dannelse — forming students as critical, independent citizens — provides the philosophical motivation, but the skills must be taught with the same explicitness and rigour as any other competency in this curriculum.

    The Danish Context: From IT Skills to Technological Understanding

    Danish education has moved further than many countries in integrating digital literacy into national policy. The Folkeskole curriculum introduced teknologiforståelse (technological understanding) as a cross-curricular focus area through a pilot programme beginning in 2018. The aim was not to teach students to use digital tools — that is operational skill — but to develop their ability to understand and critically evaluate digital technologies: how they work, who controls them, and what their societal implications are.

    This shift from IT competency to technology literacy reflects a deeper educational philosophy. In the dannelse tradition (see Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation), understanding technology is not a vocational skill but a dimension of informed citizenship. A student who can use a spreadsheet but cannot understand how an algorithm determines her news feed has operational competence without civic literacy. The Danish approach insists on both.

    What Explicit Instruction Looks Like

    The evidence on teaching information and digital literacy points to the same conclusion reached in every chapter of this curriculum: explicit, structured, repeated instruction embedded in authentic contexts works. Isolated one-off lessons do not.

    Lateral reading must be taught explicitly. Students should learn to leave a source before reading it deeply — to open new tabs, search for information about the organisation, check what other sources report, and read competing perspectives. Teachers should model this process aloud, making the decision-making visible.

    Source evaluation must be embedded in authentic research. Abstract lessons on "how to spot misinformation" are less effective than situations where students need evaluative skill to pursue genuine questions. In history, students evaluate competing historical interpretations. In science, they distinguish peer-reviewed research from advocacy. In literature, they compare critical perspectives. Source evaluation becomes necessary, not decorative — and the skills transfer because they are practised across contexts.

    Media construction must be made visible. Students should regularly compare how different news sources cover the same event, analyse advertisements critically, and examine how platforms present information differently to different users. These activities develop the analytical habits that media literacy requires.

    Algorithmic awareness requires direct experience. Students searching the same term on different devices or accounts and comparing results produces more understanding than any lecture on filter bubbles. The experience of seeing one's own information diet as constructed, not given, is the foundation of algorithmic literacy.

    Verification must become habitual. Triangulation (consulting multiple sources), checking for updates, assessing author expertise, and reverse image searching are skills that improve with practice and atrophy without it. Like the critical thinking sub-skills in Ch-04-Critical-Thinking, they must be taught, practised, reinforced, and assessed — not mentioned once and assumed.

    ℹ Key Concept — The Role of Libraries

    School librarians and information specialists, where they exist, are natural partners for information literacy instruction. The most effective approaches integrate library expertise into subject-specific units rather than teaching information literacy as a standalone skill. A librarian co-teaching a history unit — helping students evaluate primary versus secondary sources, assess competing interpretations, and navigate databases — embeds information literacy where it is needed. Where library positions have been cut, classroom teachers must take fuller responsibility, which makes cross-curricular coordination and shared professional development all the more important.

    Connections

    Information and digital literacy are foundational to every other competency in this curriculum. Critical thinking requires access to credible information to evaluate — and the evaluative skills themselves overlap substantially. Collaboration often involves shared research and synthesis. Self-directed learning requires students to locate and evaluate resources independently. Leadership and global competence depend on understanding information from diverse sources and cultures. The frameworks in Chapter 9 consistently recognise information literacy as essential. And the digital age risks explored in Chapter 3 make information literacy the frontline defence against the cognitive vulnerabilities that technology creates.
