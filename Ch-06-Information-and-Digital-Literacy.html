    Chapter 6 — Information and Digital Literacy: Navigating a World of Abundance

    ◉ Overview

    Today's students face a paradox: they have access to more information than any previous generation, yet the abundance itself creates challenges. Distinguishing credible sources from misinformation, understanding how media is constructed, and using digital tools responsibly are essential competencies. This chapter explores the conceptual landscape of information and digital literacy, examines what research reveals about students' actual capacities, and identifies strategies for developing these critical competencies.

    Understanding the Landscape: Definitions and Frameworks

    Information literacy and digital literacy are closely related but distinct concepts that together form the foundation for informed participation in contemporary society. Information literacy is the ability to recognize an information need, locate information from diverse sources, critically evaluate that information, and use it effectively to address a question or solve a problem. It is primarily about cognitive processes of evaluation and synthesis.

    Digital literacy extends this framework to address the tools and environments through which information is increasingly accessed and created. Digital literacy includes skills such as navigating digital platforms, protecting personal data, creating digital content responsibly, and understanding how algorithms shape what we see. Together, information and digital literacy form an integrated competency essential for 21st-century citizenship.

    The European Commission's DigComp 2.2 framework (Vuorikari, Kluzer, & Punie, 2022) provides a comprehensive mapping of digital competencies. It identifies five core competence areas: Information and data literacy (browsing, searching, filtering, evaluating, managing data); Communication and collaboration (interacting through digital platforms, sharing, managing online identity); Digital content creation (creating, integrating, and remixing digital content); Safety (protecting devices, personal data, health, and the environment); and Problem-solving (identifying when digital tools are needed and using them creatively to solve problems). This framework is widely adopted across Europe and provides a useful language for discussing digital competence systematically.

    ℹ Key Concept — Lateral Reading

    Lateral reading is a source evaluation strategy used by professional fact-checkers (Wineburg & McGrew, 2019). Rather than reading a source deeply while on the source itself, lateral readers leave the website to verify claims through external searches and cross-references. They check: Who is behind this organization? What do other sources say about this claim? Are there counterarguments? This practice, which seems counterintuitive to traditional deep-reading advice, has proven remarkably effective for identifying misinformation.

    The Source Evaluation Challenge

    One of the most important dimensions of information literacy is source evaluation—the ability to judge whether a source is credible, biased, or misleading. Research from the Stanford History Education Group paints a sobering picture of student capability in this area.

    Wineburg, McGrew, and colleagues (2016, 2019) conducted studies where even university students were asked to evaluate online sources. The results were striking. Many students relied on surface-level cues: if a website looked professional or if a claim appeared in multiple places, they assumed it was credible. They rarely checked the domain or investigated who was behind an organization. When asked to evaluate a photo claiming to show an environmental disaster, many students didn't think to do a reverse image search to verify the photo's origin and date.

    The researchers contrasted this with how professional fact-checkers approach source evaluation. Fact-checkers don't read deeply into a source while on that site; instead, they leave it immediately to check external sources. They ask: Who is making this claim? What is their expertise? What do other reliable sources say? Do counterarguments exist? This strategy, which Wineburg and McGrew (2019) call "lateral reading," seems to contradict conventional wisdom about close reading, yet it proves far more effective at identifying misinformation.

    The research also suggests that explicitly teaching source evaluation techniques improves students' performance—but the improvements can be short-lived. Without consistent reinforcement and application in authentic contexts, students revert to their default strategies. The implication is clear: source evaluation must be taught not once but repeatedly, across different subjects and contexts, and embedded in real research tasks where stakes matter.

    Beyond Surface Critique: Media Literacy

    Information literacy encompasses more than evaluating written sources. Students must also understand how media is constructed and consumed. Visual media, video, audio, and interactive media all present different literacy challenges.

    Media literacy involves understanding that all media is constructed—created by people with particular perspectives, purposes, and constraints. An advertisement, a news article, a documentary, and an Instagram post all involve choices about what to include and exclude, what to emphasize and downplay. Media literacy means asking: Who created this? What is their purpose? Who benefits if people believe this? What perspective or viewpoint might be missing?

    The P21 framework (Battelle for Kids, 2019) distinguishes between media literacy (analysing and creating media products) and ICT literacy (applying technology effectively). Together, these form the competencies necessary for responsible digital citizenship. A student might understand the technical aspects of how to use a digital tool (ICT literacy) but fail to understand the social and political implications of how that tool works (media literacy). For example, a student might know how to use social media platforms technically but not understand how algorithmic curation shapes what they see—a form of media literacy that affects what information they have access to.

    » In Practice

    In a Danish studies class, students are asked to research how digital platforms covered a recent environmental policy debate. Media literacy involves asking: How did different news sources frame the issue? Which voices were included and which excluded? What visual choices (photos, graphics) were made? How did algorithms on social media shape what different students saw? Rather than simply gathering "all perspectives," students learn to recognize how media construction itself shapes public understanding.

    The "Digital Native" Myth and Overconfidence

    A persistent misconception in education is that young people are "digital natives"—that their early exposure to technology means they naturally possess high digital literacy. Research suggests otherwise. Kirschner and De Bruyckere (2017) challenge the "digital native" myth empirically. Young people may use digital devices fluently, but fluency with devices does not translate to critical understanding of digital systems or responsible use. Research consistently finds that students overestimate their own ability to evaluate information (McGrew et al., 2019) — a confidence-competence gap that makes explicit instruction essential.

    Moreover, digital native assumptions can lead educators to underestimate the need for explicit instruction. If students are "naturally" digital literate, teachers assume, why teach digital skills? The evidence suggests this is misguided. Students need explicit instruction in information evaluation, data privacy, algorithmic awareness, and responsible digital citizenship. The fact that they use devices frequently does not mean they use them wisely.

    Digital Literacy in the Danish Curriculum

    Danish education has explicitly integrated digital literacy into national policy. The Folkeskole curriculum introduced teknologiforståelse (technological understanding) as a cross-curricular focus area, initially through a pilot programme in 2018. The aim was not simply to teach students to use digital tools but to develop their ability to understand and critically evaluate digital technologies. This connects directly to the dannelse tradition's emphasis on informed, thoughtful citizenship.

    The emphasis on technology comprehension rather than mere technology skills reflects a shift from IT competency (how to use applications) to technology literacy (understanding how digital systems work, who controls them, and what their societal implications are). This deeper understanding is essential as students navigate increasing automation, artificial intelligence, and algorithmic decision-making in their lives.

    Emerging Challenges: AI-Generated Content and Filter Bubbles

    The information landscape continues to evolve, creating new challenges for digital literacy instruction. Two deserve particular attention.

    AI-generated content introduces unprecedented complexity to source evaluation. If a text, image, or video can be created entirely by artificial intelligence without human authorship, traditional source evaluation strategies must adapt. How does one evaluate credibility of content without a clear human agent responsible for truth claims? The implications are still unfolding, but it is clear that students will need updated frameworks for understanding provenance and authenticity in an age of generative AI.

    Algorithmic curation and filter bubbles present a more subtle challenge. When algorithms decide what information to show users based on past behaviour and engagement, users see a curated version of information that reinforces their existing views. This can create echo chambers where diverse perspectives are filtered out. Students must develop awareness that what they see online is not the full picture of available information, and that their own preferences shape what algorithms show them. This represents a new form of literacy—understanding algorithmic systems and how they work.

    ⚠ The Confidence-Competence Gap

    Research reveals a consistent finding: students often overestimate their own ability to evaluate information. Students who can identify a URL or check when a website was last updated may believe they are evaluating sources critically, when in fact these surface-level checks are insufficient. Real source evaluation requires deeper investigation—tracking organizational funding, comparing multiple sources, understanding domain expertise. The gap between students' confidence and their actual competence is substantial and dangerous.

    Practical Strategies for Developing Information and Digital Literacy

    Developing information and digital literacy requires systematic, ongoing instruction rather than one-time lessons.

    First, teach lateral reading explicitly. Don't ask students to evaluate sources while on those sources. Instead, teach them to open new tabs, search for information about the organization, check what other sources say, and read multiple perspectives. Model this process aloud so students see the thinking.

    Second, embed source evaluation in authentic research tasks. Rather than abstract lessons on "How to spot misinformation," have students pursue genuine research questions in their subjects. In a history class, students research competing historical interpretations. In a science class, students must distinguish peer-reviewed research from non-peer-reviewed claims. In a literature class, students compare different critical interpretations. In each case, source evaluation becomes necessary, not decorative.

    Third, create opportunities to analyze media construction. Have students compare how different news sources cover the same story. Analyze advertisements critically. Examine how social media platforms present information. These activities develop the media literacy dimension of information literacy.

    Fourth, teach about data privacy and digital safety. Explicitly address how data is collected, sold, and used. Discuss privacy settings, password management, and responsible online behaviour. This dimension of digital literacy is often neglected but increasingly important.

    Fifth, develop awareness of algorithmic curation. Help students understand that what they see online is shaped by algorithms. Have them search the same term on different devices or accounts and observe the differences. Discuss how this affects their information diet.

    Sixth, teach verification strategies. Not all source evaluation is about detecting deliberate misinformation. Sometimes credible sources make errors. Teaching students to triangulate (consult multiple sources), check for updates (has new information emerged?), and consider author expertise all strengthen verification skills.

    The Role of Libraries and Information Specialists

    School librarians and information specialists play a crucial role in developing information literacy. In schools with strong library programmes, librarians work alongside subject teachers to embed source evaluation into units of study. Rather than teaching information literacy as a standalone skill, librarians integrate it into authentic research tasks. A librarian might co-teach a history unit, for example, helping students understand primary versus secondary sources and evaluate historical interpretations.

    Unfortunately, school library positions have declined in many countries, limiting this resource. Where libraries are under-resourced or librarians are unavailable, classroom teachers must take fuller responsibility for information literacy instruction. This is challenging without library expertise but is not impossible if teachers actively seek professional development and collaborate with peers.

    Connections

    Information and digital literacy are foundational to the other core competencies. Critical thinking requires access to credible information to evaluate. Collaboration often involves shared research and synthesis of information. Self-directed learning requires students to locate and evaluate resources independently. And leadership and global competence depend on understanding information from diverse sources and cultures. The frameworks in Chapter 9 consistently recognize information literacy as essential.
