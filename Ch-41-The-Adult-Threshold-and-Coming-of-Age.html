    Chapter 41 — The Adult Threshold and Coming of Age: Modern Education Removed the Crucible — And Extended Adolescence Is the Result

    ◉ Thesis

    Every known human culture developed structured rites of passage to mark the transition from childhood to adulthood — not because ancient peoples were sentimental about ceremony, but because they understood something that modern Western education has forgotten: becoming an adult requires a crucible, not just a birthday. The transition from dependent learner to autonomous thinker follows the same three-phase structure that anthropologist Arnold van Gennep (1909/1960) identified across cultures: separation from the familiar, liminality (a period of disorienting challenge where the old identity is suspended and the new one has not yet formed), and incorporation as a recognised adult. Modern schools removed these structures and replaced them with nothing — and psychologists have observed the predictable consequence: an extended adolescence in which the psychological transition to adult identity is delayed indefinitely (Arnett, 2000). This chapter argues that the rites of passage traditions — Aboriginal walkabout, the Spartan agoge, Nordic coming-of-age rituals, bar/bat mitzvah — alongside Campbell's Hero's Journey and the story of Telemachus, model what the crossing from preference-driven to value-driven action looks like, and that education must find structural equivalents for the crucible that modern life has quietly removed.

    The Three-Phase Structure: Why Every Culture Independently Invented the Same Process

    Arnold van Gennep's (1909/1960) foundational anthropological work identified a three-phase structure common to rites of passage across cultures: separation (the initiate leaves the familiar world), liminality (the initiate enters a transitional state of ambiguity and challenge), and incorporation (the initiate returns, transformed, and is recognised as an adult by the community). Victor Turner (1969) developed the concept of liminality further, showing that the transitional phase is where the deepest transformation occurs — precisely because the old identity has been suspended and the new identity has not yet formed.

    For education, this structure matters because the transition from dependent learner to autonomous thinker follows the same pattern. Students must leave the comfort of being told what to do (separation), struggle with the uncertainty of thinking for themselves (liminality), and emerge with a new identity as self-directed learners (incorporation). The absence of structured rites of passage in modern Western cultures may contribute to what psychologists have observed as an extended adolescence — a delay in the psychological transition to adult identity (Arnett, 2000).

    Aboriginal Walkabout

    In certain Aboriginal Australian cultures, the walkabout is a journey undertaken by young men as part of their transition to adulthood. The initiate leaves the community and travels alone through the landscape, surviving by the skills and knowledge transmitted through cultural instruction. The walkabout is simultaneously a test of practical competence and a spiritual journey of self-discovery (Chatwin, 1987).

    Educational Resonance

    The walkabout embodies the principle that competence must be demonstrated, not merely claimed. The young man does not become an adult because he has reached a certain age; he becomes an adult because he has demonstrated the capacity to sustain himself through challenge. This maps to the distinction between performance and competence that runs through the assessment literature (see Ch-25-Assessment-Literacy-Students-as-Assessment-Agents): authentic assessment requires demonstration of capability in context, not simply the ability to answer questions about capability in the abstract.

    ⚠ Contextual Honesty

    "Walkabout" is a term that has been oversimplified and romanticised in Western popular culture. Aboriginal Australian cultures are enormously diverse, and initiation practices vary significantly between language groups. The account here draws on generalised anthropological literature and should not be read as representing any specific community's practices. Teachers using this material should acknowledge these limitations (Stanner, 1979).

    The Spartan Agoge

    The Spartan agoge (ἀγωγή, "training") was the education and training system for male citizens of ancient Sparta, beginning at age seven and continuing until age thirty. The agoge combined physical training, endurance tests, communal living, and deliberate hardship — including periods of deliberate deprivation — to produce citizens capable of subordinating personal comfort to collective duty (Cartledge, 2003).

    What the Agoge Gets Right and Wrong

    The agoge illustrates a genuine educational principle: that competence under pressure requires practice under pressure. This connects to the research on desirable difficulties in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties — the finding that conditions which make learning harder in the moment often produce stronger long-term results. The agoge designed difficulty into the process of formation.

    However, the agoge also illustrates the limits of discipline without autonomy. Spartan education produced formidable soldiers but not self-determining individuals. The freedom of thought, creativity, and intellectual independence that characterise the Athenian tradition — and that this curriculum aims to develop — were systematically suppressed (Marrou, 1956). The agoge is a useful cautionary counterpoint to the Bushido tradition in Ch-37-Discipline-and-Self-Mastery: discipline that serves collective conformity is fundamentally different from discipline that enables individual agency.

    Nordic Coming-of-Age Traditions

    Scandinavian cultures have historically marked the transition to adulthood through a combination of practical tests and community recognition. In Norse tradition, the passage to adulthood was connected to demonstrations of skill, courage, and readiness to take on responsibilities within the community — farming, seafaring, craftsmanship, and governance. The Thing (the Norse assembly) was open to free adults, and participation in communal governance was itself a marker of adult status (Brink & Price, 2008).

    Connection to Dannelse

    The Nordic coming-of-age tradition connects naturally to the Danish concept of dannelse discussed in Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation. Dannelse frames formation as the development of a whole person who is capable of participating in democratic life — not merely skilled but ethically formed. The Nordic threshold was not simply about competence; it was about readiness to contribute to the community's collective decision-making. This resonates with Grundtvig's later vision of folkedannelse — the education of the whole people for democratic participation (Korsgaard, 2012).

    Bar/Bat Mitzvah

    In the Jewish tradition, the bar mitzvah (for boys, at age 13) and bat mitzvah (for girls, at age 12 or 13) marks the point at which a young person becomes responsible for their own moral and religious obligations. The key phrase is bar/bat mitzvah — literally "son/daughter of the commandment." Before this point, the parents bear responsibility for the child's actions; after it, the individual is accountable for their own choices (Hilton, 2014).

    The Transfer of Accountability

    What makes the bar/bat mitzvah particularly relevant to education is its focus on the transfer of responsibility. The ceremony does not celebrate the achievement of competence — a 13-year-old has not completed their formation. Rather, it marks the moment at which the young person accepts ownership of their own development. This maps precisely to the concept of the adult threshold in Ch-19b-Andragogy-and-Self-Determination: the shift from external regulation (parents and teachers are responsible for my learning) to internal regulation (I am responsible for my learning).

    Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey

    Joseph Campbell's The Hero with a Thousand Faces (1949) identified a narrative pattern — the monomyth or Hero's Journey — recurring across mythological traditions worldwide. The journey follows a consistent structure: departure from the ordinary world, initiation through trials and encounters with mentors and adversaries, and return with new knowledge or capacity. Campbell drew on Jung's analytical psychology, Fraser's comparative mythology, and van Gennep's rites of passage to argue that this pattern reflects a fundamental structure of human psychological development.

    The Monomyth as Learning Trajectory

    Campbell's three-act structure maps remarkably well to the learning trajectory described throughout this curriculum:

    Departure — the learner leaves the comfort of existing knowledge and enters new, unfamiliar territory (compare: the productive failure phase in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties, where students attempt problems before receiving instruction)

    Initiation — the learner faces challenges, receives guidance from mentors, confronts failure, and develops new capacities through struggle (compare: the liminal phase of van Gennep; desirable difficulties; the zone of proximal development)

    Return — the learner integrates new understanding and is changed by the experience, able to contribute what they have learned to others (compare: the transfer phase; teaching others as the deepest form of learning, discussed in Ch-18-Learning-to-Learn)

    ℹ Key Concept — The Hero's Journey as Learning Pattern

    Campbell's Hero's Journey describes, in narrative form, the same pattern that learning science reveals empirically: growth requires leaving the familiar, struggling through difficulty, and returning transformed. The pattern is recursive — it operates at the scale of a single lesson, a course, and a lifetime of learning (Campbell, 1949).

    Telemachus: The Son Who Became a Man

    Homer's Odyssey (c. 8th century BCE) is the story of Odysseus's long journey home, but it is also — and perhaps more importantly for education — the story of his son Telemachus's coming of age. At the poem's opening, Telemachus is a passive, frustrated young man unable to control the suitors who have overrun his household. He knows something is wrong but lacks the capacity and confidence to act.

    The Telemachy as Educational Model

    Books I–IV of the Odyssey, known as the "Telemachy," trace Telemachus's transformation through a specific educational process. The goddess Athena, disguised as the mentor figure Mentes and later as Mentor (from whom we derive the word mentor), does not solve Telemachus's problems. Instead, she gives him three things: a model of what adult behaviour looks like, a mission that requires him to leave home and act independently, and the confidence to believe he is capable of the task (Homer, Odyssey, I.252–305).

    This is precisely the scaffolding described in the research on teacher-as-mentor in Ch-22-Principles-of-Effective-Instruction: the gradual transfer of responsibility from the expert to the learner, providing support that is progressively withdrawn as competence develops. Athena does not fight the suitors for Telemachus. She equips him to fight them himself.

    » For Reflection

    Telemachus's journey begins when Athena tells him, in effect, "You are no longer a child. Act accordingly." Is there a moment in your own experience when someone expected more of you than you expected of yourself — and that expectation changed what you were capable of? What role does being treated as an adult play in becoming one?

    The Common Thread: You Cannot Become an Adult Without Passing Through Difficulty

    Across all these traditions, the passage to adulthood is not a gift — it is earned through challenge. The walkabout demands survival in the wild. The agoge demands endurance under hardship. The bar/bat mitzvah demands the acceptance of responsibility. The Hero's Journey demands departure from comfort.

    The educational implication is clear: students do not become autonomous learners by being told they are autonomous. They become autonomous by being placed in situations that require autonomy and supported through the difficulty of exercising it. This is the argument for productive struggle, for student-led inquiry, for authentic assessment — and it is the argument that every culture in human history has made through its coming-of-age rituals.
