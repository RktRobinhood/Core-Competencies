Chapter 17 — Productive Struggle & Desirable Difficulties

◉ Overview

One of the most persistent and damaging misconceptions in education is that effective learning should feel easy. Research from Robert and Elizabeth Bjork, Manu Kapur, and others demonstrates the opposite: conditions that make learning feel harder in the moment often produce stronger understanding and longer retention. This chapter examines the science of productive struggle, the phenomenon of counterproductive adjustment, and how teachers can design learning experiences that are challenging in the right ways.

The Paradox of Difficulty

There is a fundamental mismatch between how learning feels and how learning works. Students, given a choice, consistently prefer study strategies that produce a sense of fluency — re-reading highlighted passages, massed practice, blocking similar problems together. These approaches feel productive because information comes to mind easily during the study session. But as Robert Bjork's decades of research at UCLA have demonstrated, this fluency is often an illusion. High performance during initial learning frequently masks low storage strength, meaning the knowledge is accessible now but will fade rapidly (Bjork & Bjork, 2011).

The Bjorks introduced the term desirable difficulties to describe conditions that slow down initial acquisition but enhance long-term retention and transfer. Spacing practice over time, interleaving different problem types, generating answers before being shown them, and testing oneself rather than re-reading — all of these strategies feel harder. And that feeling of difficulty is precisely the signal that deeper cognitive processing is occurring.

ℹ Key Concept — Desirable Difficulties

A desirable difficulty is a learning condition that introduces challenge during encoding in ways that strengthen long-term memory and transfer. The difficulty is "desirable" because it triggers deeper processing — but only when the learner has sufficient prior knowledge to engage with the challenge meaningfully (Bjork & Bjork, 2011).

This insight has profound implications for classroom culture. When students encounter difficulty and interpret it as evidence that a strategy is not working, they make what Justin Sung, a researcher in self-regulated learning at the National Institute of Education, calls a counterproductive adjustment — they abandon the effective-but-challenging approach and return to a method that feels easier but produces shallower learning (Sung, 2025). Recognising and interrupting this pattern is one of the most impactful things a teacher can do.

Productive Failure: Struggling Before Instruction

Manu Kapur, an educational psychologist at ETH Zurich, has formalised a specific instructional design built around productive struggle. His framework, Productive Failure, involves two phases: students first attempt to solve a complex problem using concepts they have not yet been formally taught, and only then receive direct instruction on the target material (Kapur, 2008; Kapur & Bielaczyc, 2012).

The initial problem-solving phase is designed so that students will generate multiple approaches but will typically not arrive at the canonical solution. This is the "failure." But the process of activating prior knowledge, generating representations, and confronting the limits of one's current understanding creates what Kapur calls preparation for future learning. When instruction follows, students have a richer network of activated knowledge to connect the new concepts to.

» In Practice — Productive Failure in Mathematics

In Kapur's landmark studies with secondary school students learning standard deviation, the productive failure group first attempted to devise their own measures of consistency for a dataset — without having been taught the formula. They generated multiple imperfect solutions. The direct instruction group received a clear explanation first, then practised. On procedural tasks, both groups performed similarly. But on conceptual understanding and transfer problems, the productive failure group significantly outperformed their peers (Kapur & Bielaczyc, 2012).

A major meta-analysis by Sinha and Kapur (2021), published in the Review of Educational Research, examined 53 studies with 166 comparisons between problem-solving-first and instruction-first sequences. The evidence strongly favours allowing students to struggle with problems before receiving instruction, particularly for conceptual understanding and transfer — the kinds of learning that matter most for competency development.

⚠ When Struggle Becomes Unproductive

Not all difficulty is desirable. Struggle becomes unproductive when students lack the prior knowledge to engage meaningfully with the challenge, when the task is so far beyond their current ability that no meaningful generation can occur, or when emotional frustration overwhelms cognitive engagement. Hiebert and Grouws (2007) emphasise that productive struggle means working within the student's zone of proximal development — challenge that stretches but does not break. The teacher's role is to calibrate the difficulty and provide enough structure that students can generate ideas without drowning.

The Metacognitive Illusion

The reason productive struggle is so difficult to implement is not primarily pedagogical — it is psychological. Both students and teachers carry deep-seated beliefs about what effective learning looks like, and these beliefs are often wrong.

When teachers observe a lesson where students are answering questions correctly, working fluently, and appearing engaged, they tend to rate that lesson highly. When they observe a lesson where students are making errors, pausing, struggling, and revising their thinking, they rate it lower. Yet the second scenario often reflects deeper learning in progress. Bjork's research reveals that high retrieval strength during a lesson can create an illusion of learning, masking the fact that actual storage strength may be quite low (Bjork & Bjork, 2011).

Students experience the same illusion from the inside. Re-reading a chapter feels productive because the material seems familiar on the second pass. But familiarity is not the same as understanding, and recognition is not the same as recall. The student who re-reads and highlights may feel confident going into an exam and be shocked by the result. The student who closed the book and tried to reconstruct the argument from memory — a much harder and less pleasant experience — is more likely to perform well.

ℹ Key Concept — The Counterproductive Adjustment

When a learning strategy feels difficult, learners often interpret this as evidence that the strategy is ineffective. They then switch to a strategy that feels easier — typically one that produces fluency rather than deep processing. This counterproductive adjustment is one of the primary reasons why evidence-based study strategies are underused despite strong research support (Bjork & Bjork, 2011; Sung, 2025).

This is where the philosophy of "every student is a teacher" becomes essential. If students understand why struggle is valuable — if they can articulate the cognitive science behind desirable difficulties — they are far less likely to make counterproductive adjustments. Metacognitive awareness transforms struggle from something to be avoided into something to be recognised and even welcomed as a sign of deep processing.

Orders of Learning and Encoding Quality

Justin Sung's framework of orders of learning provides a practical lens for understanding why some forms of struggle are more productive than others (Sung, 2024). Lower-order learning — rote memorisation, surface-level encoding — produces knowledge that decays quickly and transfers poorly. Higher-order learning — building connections between ideas, relating new material to prior knowledge, organising information into meaningful structures — produces knowledge that is more durable and more flexible.

The key insight is that higher-order encoding inherently feels more difficult than lower-order encoding. Connecting ideas requires more cognitive effort than copying them down. Explaining a concept in your own words is harder than highlighting someone else's. And this is exactly the point — the additional effort is the mechanism by which stronger memory traces are formed.

When we ask students to engage in productive struggle, we are asking them to do higher-order encoding. When we allow them to fall back to re-reading and highlighting, we are allowing them to default to lower-order encoding. The teacher's job is to create conditions where higher-order encoding is the path of least resistance — not by making it easy, but by making the purpose clear and the support sufficient.

? For Reflection

Think about a recent learning experience — your own or your students'. At what point did it feel most difficult? Was that difficulty a sign of ineffective teaching, or was it the moment when the deepest processing was occurring? How might you have recognised the difference in real time?

Implications for Classroom Design

The research on productive struggle and desirable difficulties points to several design principles for teachers committed to developing students as competent, self-aware learners.

First, normalise difficulty. The classroom culture must explicitly communicate that struggling with material is not a sign of low ability — it is a sign that real learning is happening. This connects directly to the growth mindset research explored in Ch-16-Growth-Mindset, but goes further: it is not enough to believe that ability can grow. Students must also understand how it grows, and that the mechanism involves productive discomfort.

Second, make the science visible. Share the research on desirable difficulties and the counterproductive adjustment with students. When learners understand why a strategy feels hard and why that hardness is valuable, their tolerance for difficulty increases markedly. This is metacognitive empowerment — turning students into informed participants in their own learning rather than passive recipients of instructions they do not understand.

Third, design for generation before instruction. Kapur's productive failure model can be adapted across subjects. Before explaining a concept, give students a problem that requires them to grapple with the underlying ideas. Their attempts need not be correct — the value lies in the activation and differentiation of prior knowledge that prepares the ground for instruction.

Fourth, use retrieval as the default mode. Rather than asking students to review material by re-reading it, ask them to reconstruct it from memory. Rather than presenting information, ask them to generate predictions first. These are not add-on activities — they should be the primary structure of learning time. As explored in Ch-13-Retrieval-Practice-and-Spaced-Learning, retrieval practice is one of the most robust findings in the science of learning.

» In Practice — The Diagnostic Cycle

Sung (2025) recommends a diagnostic approach to learning: after encoding new material, students attempt retrieval and then assess the quality of their own recall. Where are the gaps? Where is the understanding superficial? This diagnostic cycle — encode, retrieve, diagnose, re-encode — transforms studying from a passive process into an active investigation. When students internalise this cycle, they become their own teachers, capable of identifying and addressing their own learning needs without external prompting.

The Connection to Danish Educational Philosophy

The concept of productive struggle resonates deeply with the Danish educational tradition of dannelse (explored in Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation). Dannelse emphasises the development of the whole person through challenge, reflection, and engagement with ideas that resist easy resolution. A classroom built around productive struggle is inherently a classroom built around dannelse — one where the goal is not correct answers but deeper understanding, where difficulty is not an obstacle to learning but its very medium.

In the context of expanding TOK from 100 to 150 hours, productive struggle provides both a philosophical grounding and a practical toolkit. The additional hours are not for more content delivery — they are for creating the conditions in which students can wrestle with ideas, confront their own misconceptions, and emerge as more capable, more reflective, and more autonomous learners.
