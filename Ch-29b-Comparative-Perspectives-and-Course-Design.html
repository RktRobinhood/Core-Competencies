    Chapter 29b — Comparative Perspectives on Character Formation and Course Design — Formation for What?

    ◉ Thesis

    Every civilisation that takes education seriously eventually confronts the same question: formation for what? Denmark answers: for self-determination within democratic community. China answers: for harmonious participation within social and political order. The difference is not academic — it produces radically different institutions, assessment systems, and daily classroom practices. China's experiment in collective formation is the most instructive comparative case available because it reveals both what works (embodied practice, ritual, exemplar modelling) and what fails catastrophically (aspirational reform destroyed by unreformed assessment). The suzhi jiaoyu reform — China's own version of holistic education — has been systematically undermined by the gaokao examination system for four decades, demonstrating a principle that Danish educators implementing competency-based education must absorb: when assessment contradicts philosophy, assessment wins every time. The practical implications for course design are concrete. Frame competencies within dannelse philosophy so they carry formative weight, not just instrumental value. Use Danish language for core concepts because the words themselves carry philosophical meaning that English cannot capture. And design assessment systems that reward formation, not just performance — because without that alignment, even the most compelling educational philosophy remains rhetorical.

    The World's Largest Experiment in Character Education

    The dannelse tradition treats character formation as the cultivation of autonomous individuals within a democratic community. But what happens when a different civilisation pursues the same fundamental goal — developing whole persons through education — from radically different philosophical premises? China offers the most instructive comparative case, because its educational practices are simultaneously the most deliberate large-scale attempt at character formation in the modern world and the most revealing contrast with Nordic approaches.

    Same Goal, Opposite Direction: Confucian Formation Starts with the Collective

    China's approach to character formation is grounded in Confucian philosophical traditions that predate European Bildung by centuries. The Confucian concept of the junzi (exemplary person) shares with dannelse the conviction that education should form the whole person — morally, socially, intellectually, and civically. Both traditions reject the reduction of education to technical skill transfer. Both insist that who a student becomes matters as much as what they know.

    The critical difference lies in method and orientation. Where dannelse proceeds from the individual toward society — cultivating self-determination (selvbestemmelse) that enables meaningful participation — the Chinese tradition proceeds from the collective toward the individual. The Confucian framework emphasises that the self is fundamentally relational: a person becomes fully human through properly ordered social relationships, filial piety, and participation in communal ritual (Wu, 2016). Education in this tradition is not primarily about developing autonomous individuals but about forming persons who fulfil their roles within a harmonious social order.

    This philosophical difference produces strikingly different institutional practices — practices that deserve careful examination because they illuminate both the power and the limits of different approaches to formation.

    Embodied Listening: What Synchronised Movement Actually Produces

    The most visible expression of China's collective formation practices is guangbo ticao (广播体操) — national radio calisthenics. Launched in 1951, this programme requires millions of Chinese students and workers to perform synchronised exercises to broadcast music every day. The programme is now in its ninth official set of movements and remains mandatory in schools across the country (Liu & Tobin, 2018).

    Scholars of embodied cognition have analysed guangbo ticao as far more than physical exercise. The synchronised combination of amplified music with choreographed body movements creates what researchers call "embodied listening" — a daily ritual in which participants physically enact collective belonging. The music retains military-style elements that connect participants to the socialist state's founding narrative, while the synchronised movement produces a visceral experience of coordination with others (Liu & Tobin, 2018).

    Liu and Tobin's comparative study in Comparative Education Review found that guangbo ticao carries implicit embodied practices rooted in traditional Chinese cultural, ethical, and aesthetic beliefs that produce distinct meanings and social functions beyond what Western observers might initially perceive. Participants report genuine experiences of solidarity, shared purpose, and collective identity. The programme also delivers measurable health benefits through structured daily physical activity.

    Yet the programme simultaneously functions as what scholars identify as a coercive cultural activity. Participation is mandatory, not voluntary. Individual expression is subordinated to collective synchronisation. The programme has persisted essentially unchanged despite three decades of educational reforms toward child-centred pedagogy — a persistence that reveals the depth of China's commitment to collective formation over individual autonomy (Liu & Tobin, 2018).

    When Collective Punishment Becomes a Pedagogical Method

    Guangbo ticao is one element in a broader architecture of collective formation practices in Chinese education.

    Military training (junxun, 军训) is compulsory for all Chinese high school and university students, typically lasting two to four weeks. Formalised nationally following 1989, the programme combines formation drills, synchronised marching, strict living schedules, and patriotic education. Its most distinctive feature is collective punishment: when one student violates a rule, the entire group faces consequences. This mechanism deliberately subordinates individual accountability to group responsibility, embedding the Confucian principle that the self exists only in relation to others (Wu, 2016).

    Flag-raising ceremonies (shengqi yishi, 升旗仪式) are mandatory in all Chinese primary and secondary schools, typically conducted weekly with patriotic speeches and collective oath-taking. These rituals cultivate what Chinese educators describe as national consciousness, collective identity, and respect for institutional order.

    The Young Pioneers and Communist Youth League provide age-graded structures for political socialisation, beginning at age six and extending through university. These organisations embed civic formation within party ideology, creating a continuous institutional pathway for the development of political identity.

    Jinting Wu's foundational analysis identifies three interconnected mechanisms in Chinese educational discipline: exemplarity (the use of model figures for students to imitate), ritual (embodied social practices that enact proper order), and examination (academic assessments that reinforce hierarchical distinctions). Together, these mechanisms constitute a comprehensive system of formation that operates through the body, the community, and the institution simultaneously (Wu, 2016).

    Aspirational Reform Meets Unreformed Assessment — and Assessment Wins

    China's awareness that examination-driven education (yingshi jiaoyu) fails to develop whole persons led to the suzhi jiaoyu (素质教育, "quality education") reform, first articulated by Deng Xiaoping in 1985 and pursued through curriculum revisions since the 1980s. Suzhi jiaoyu explicitly aims to develop students across moral, intellectual, physical, and aesthetic dimensions — a framework that resonates with dannelse's five dimensions of formation (Pang, Cheng, Yu & Wu, 2020).

    The parallel is striking: like dannelse, suzhi jiaoyu rejects the reduction of education to examination performance. Like dannelse, it insists on holistic development encompassing character, culture, and civic responsibility. Like dannelse, it attempts to embed formation within subject-specific learning rather than treating it as an add-on.

    But suzhi jiaoyu reveals a fundamental implementation challenge that dannelse proponents should study carefully. Despite decades of policy aspiration, implementation remains hampered by insufficient resources, conceptual ambiguity, and — most critically — the persistence of the gaokao (national university entrance examination) as the dominant sorting mechanism in Chinese society. Teachers, parents, and students rationally prioritise examination success over holistic development, because examination outcomes carry life-long consequences (Pang et al., 2020). The goals of the reform, as scholars note, are still not reflected in the examinations and frequently conflict with the goals of teachers, students, and parents in a society where examination results determine life trajectories.

    ⚠ Caveat and Nuance — The Assessment Alignment Problem

    China's suzhi jiaoyu reform demonstrates a principle that applies universally: aspirational frameworks for holistic education fail without systemic alignment. When assessment systems reward narrow academic achievement, character formation becomes performative regardless of how compelling the philosophy behind it is. This challenge is not unique to China — any school implementing competency-based education faces the same tension between formative aspiration and summative accountability (see Ch-33-Implementation-Science and Ch-25-Assessment-Literacy-Students-as-Assessment-Agents).

    Separating the Insight from the Coercion

    An honest comparative analysis must address the coercive dimension directly. Chinese collective formation practices involve mandatory participation without genuine consent, group punishment mechanisms that subordinate individual moral agency, ritualization of state ideology through daily patriotic activities, and symbolic violence that research shows falls disproportionately on low-achieving students (Wu, 2016). Thomas Heberer's analysis of social disciplining in China argues that unlike in the Western European context — where disciplining processes emerged from multiple institutional sources — China's central state has remained the originator and key promulgator of the disciplining process (Heberer, 2023). This centralised control raises fundamental questions about the relationship between formation and freedom.

    Yet the Chinese case also contains genuine insights for anyone designing character education.

    The power of embodied collective practice is real. Synchronised physical activity creates emotional and social bonds that abstract discussion cannot achieve. Regular, repeated ritual practices are demonstrably more effective for value internalisation than occasional lectures or workshops. Music, movement, and sensory engagement enhance learning retention and social cohesion (Liu & Tobin, 2018). Western and Nordic educators could integrate more structured collective practices — morning gatherings, shared physical activities, community rituals — without requiring ideological conformity.

    The Confucian insight that character develops through practice and imitation, not merely through instruction, has empirical support. The emphasis on exemplar modelling (zuoren, becoming a person through observing and imitating worthy examples) connects to social learning theory and the pedagogical use of modelling described in Ch-22-Principles-of-Effective-Instruction.

    The Chinese case also provides a sobering demonstration of what happens when holistic education reform meets an unreformed assessment system. Danish schools implementing competency-based education should take this lesson seriously: if the assessment system does not change, the reform will remain rhetorical.

    Self-Determination Versus Social Harmony: The Fork in the Road

    The deepest difference between dannelse and Chinese collective formation is not one of method but of purpose. Dannelse, rooted in Klafki's (2001) framework, aims to develop three capacities: self-determination (the ability to direct one's own life), participation (the ability to engage with others and society), and solidarity (commitment to justice and the common good). The goal is autonomous individuals who choose to participate in democratic life.

    Chinese collective formation aims to develop individuals who fulfil their roles within a harmonious social order defined, ultimately, by the state. The Confucian virtues of loyalty, self-discipline, and respect for authority are compatible with this goal; the dannelse virtues of critical consciousness, independent judgment, and democratic participation are frequently in tension with it.

    This is not a neutral difference. It reflects fundamentally different answers to the question that lies at the heart of any educational philosophy: Formation for what? Dannelse answers: for self-determination within democratic community. Chinese collective formation answers: for harmonious participation within social and political order. A school committed to dannelse should understand both answers, learn from the strengths of each, and be clear about which it is pursuing and why.

    ? For Reflection

    Consider two schools: one practises daily synchronised morning exercises where all students participate together in a shared physical routine; the other allows students to choose their own morning activities. Both claim to build community. Which approach better serves the goal of character formation — and does the answer depend on whether the formation aims at collective harmony or individual autonomy? What might a school committed to dannelse take from the Chinese approach without sacrificing the principle of self-determination?

    Compounding Applied to the Species: The Obligation to Leave Things Better

    The dannelse tradition aims to form individuals who can direct their own lives and participate responsibly in democratic community. But there is a dimension of responsibility that extends beyond the individual and even beyond the immediate community: the obligation to leave the world in better shape than you found it. This is compounding applied not to personal growth but to the species — the idea that each generation has a responsibility to hand on something improved, however modestly, to the next.

    This principle has deep philosophical roots. Hans Jonas (1984), in The Imperative of Responsibility, argued that the power of modern technology creates a new kind of ethical obligation: because we can now affect the conditions of future generations in ways no previous civilisation could, we bear a responsibility that extends far beyond our own lifetime. Edmund Burke (1790), from a very different tradition, described society as a partnership between the living, the dead, and those yet to be born — a compact that places obligations on each generation to preserve and improve what it has inherited. In the Scandinavian context, the concept of folkedannelse (formation of the people) has always contained this intergenerational dimension: the purpose of education is not only to develop the individual but to strengthen the culture and institutions that future individuals will inherit (Korsgaard, 2004).

    For students, this principle connects directly to several threads in the core. The compounding research in Ch-03a-Six-Skills-Under-Pressure demonstrates that small, consistent actions produce disproportionate long-term results. Applied to civic character, the same logic holds: a student who habitually leaves shared spaces cleaner than they found them, who contributes to group work rather than free-riding, who takes responsibility for maintaining institutions rather than merely consuming their benefits, is compounding social capital. The effect of any single action is negligible. The effect of a lifetime of such actions — multiplied across a generation — is transformative.

    The practical challenge is that teenagers often lack a felt sense of responsibility for anything beyond their immediate circle. This is developmentally normal — adolescent identity formation is necessarily self-focused (Erikson, 1968). But the core should name this gap and address it directly. Students need to be shown, not merely told, that their actions have consequences beyond themselves. This might take the form of service learning framed through an intergenerational lens, community projects that require maintaining something rather than merely creating it, or structured reflections on what they have inherited and what they owe.

    ? For Reflection

    Think about the school you attend, the community you live in, the institutions you rely on. Someone built those things before you arrived. What obligation, if any, do you have to maintain and improve them for those who come after you? Is this a moral duty or merely a nice idea? What would change if every student in the school adopted the principle of leaving things better — not as a rule imposed from above, but as a personal commitment to the shared environment?

    The danger of this principle is sentimentality — reducing it to a feel-good slogan rather than a genuine ethical claim. The core should present it with philosophical weight. Jonas's argument is not that we should care about the future because it feels good, but that the asymmetry of power between present and future generations creates an inescapable obligation. The future cannot defend itself against the present. That asymmetry is what makes intergenerational responsibility not optional but foundational — and it is a principle that connects the personal (tidying a classroom) to the civilisational (sustaining democratic institutions). For the course, this could anchor a unit on civic character within the dannelse framework, or sit as a through-line connecting the discipline chapters (Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit) to the motivation chapters (Ch-19-Motivation-Autonomy-and-the-Adult-Learner) — discipline in service of something larger than yourself.

    Translating Philosophy into Structural Decisions

    When designing a course that expands the IB's ATL framework in a Danish context, several principles from the dannelse tradition should guide the work.

    Frame competencies within dannelse philosophy. Skills should not be taught as isolated, technical capacities. Each competency should be connected to a larger question about human formation: Why does self-regulation matter? Because developing the ability to manage yourself is part of becoming an autonomous person. Why does critical thinking matter? Because it develops your capacity to question, judge, and participate responsibly in democracy. Why does collaboration matter? Because learning to work well with others—especially those different from you—is part of learning to live in fællesskab.

    Use Danish language for core concepts alongside English. The terms dannelse, fællesskab, medbestemmelse (co-determination), and selvbestemmelse (self-determination) carry meaning that no English translation fully captures. Teaching them in Danish—and explaining them—honors the cultural tradition while making the concepts more concrete for Danish students.

    Include reflection and identity work, not just skills training. Competency development should include structured opportunities for students to reflect on who they are becoming, what they value, and how their learning is shaping their identity. This might take the form of learning journals, portfolio reflections, or structured conversations with teachers.

    Draw on VIA character strengths as a bridge between frameworks. The VIA strengths provide a common language that connects international research and Danish practice. They allow a school to speak both the language of character education and the language of contemporary competency frameworks.

    Treat well-being and achievement as integrated, not separate. The Danish concept of trivsel (well-being) is not opposed to academic achievement; it is foundational to it. Students who feel belonging, who believe their learning matters, and who experience their strengths are recognized are more likely to engage deeply with challenging academic work.

    Help students see themselves as carriers of culture. There is a dimension of dannelse that deserves particular attention in an international school context: the idea that each student is simultaneously a unique individual and a living representative of their culture. No single person is their culture — no one student embodies all of Denmark, or all of Europe, or all of any tradition. And yet every student carries their culture with them, expressed in their language, assumptions, values, habits of thought, and ways of being in the world. This is the individualist view of culture: culture is not an abstraction that exists apart from people. It exists only in the individuals who enact it, challenge it, and transmit it.

    This idea has deep roots. Grundtvig's vision of education was precisely this — that each person, through their formation, becomes a living expression of the communal and historical traditions that shaped them, while simultaneously contributing to the renewal of those traditions (Korsgaard, 2004). In the IB context, this connects directly to the CAS programme (Creativity, Activity, Service), which asks students to engage with the world beyond the classroom (IBO, 2015). When CAS is taught through a dannelse lens, the student is not simply performing acts of service or creativity in isolation. They are acting as someone — as a member of their community, as a citizen of their country, as a participant in a broader human project. The question shifts from "What did you do?" to "Who were you being when you did it?" and "What does your action say about the culture you carry and the culture you are creating?"

    ? For Reflection

    Consider the phrase: "You are Denmark." Not in the nationalist sense, but in the formative sense — you carry Danish culture, values, and ways of seeing the world wherever you go, and your actions either strengthen or weaken the traditions you have inherited. What does this mean for how a student approaches their CAS project? Their academic work? Their relationships with peers from other cultures? How might this awareness — of being a living, atomised part of a larger cultural whole — transform a student's sense of responsibility and purpose?

    This framing also raises genuinely interesting TOK questions about the nature of culture, identity, and knowledge. If culture exists only in the individuals who carry it, then what happens when those individuals change? Is the culture preserved or transformed? Who has the authority to define what a culture is or means? These are not peripheral questions — they sit at the heart of the Knowledge and the Knower theme in TOK and connect to AOK explorations in Human Sciences and History. The idea of students as cultural representatives is therefore not only a CAS and dannelse concept but a rich source of knowledge questions for TOK teaching (see A1-Curriculum-Hook-Map).
