    Chapter 1 — Why Core Competencies Matter: The Case for Intentional Development

    ◉ Overview

    Across the world, educational systems are recognizing that the traditional curriculum—organised around disciplinary knowledge—is insufficient for preparing students to think critically, work collaboratively, and navigate complexity. This chapter explains why core competencies matter, what research tells us about how they develop, and how a Danish school can ground this work in both global evidence and local philosophical tradition.

    The Problem with Implicit Development

    For decades, schools have operated on an assumption that critical thinking, communication, and collaboration will develop "naturally" as students engage with content. Students study history, literature, and mathematics, and through exposure to these disciplines, they will somehow become better thinkers, communicators, and collaborators.

    The research is clear: this assumption is wrong.

    Across multiple domains—critical thinking, metacognition, collaboration, and digital literacy—the strongest and most consistent finding is that these competencies must be explicitly taught, modelled, and scaffolded. They do not develop through immersion alone. In a meta-analysis of critical thinking interventions, Abrami and colleagues (2008) found that explicit instruction of critical thinking produced an effect size of d = 0.94—a very large effect—while students learning through exposure to content showed almost no gains (d = 0.09). This is not a matter of debate. The evidence is decisive.

    When schools attempt to develop skills like the IB's Approaches to Learning (ATL) without explicit instruction, implementation falters. Teachers are unsure what to teach and how to teach it. Students, left to infer what "thinking critically" or "collaborating effectively" means, develop incomplete or inconsistent competencies. The school that wants its students to become strong critical thinkers but never explicitly teaches critical thinking reasoning processes will be disappointed with the results.

    ℹ Key Concept — Explicit Instruction

    Explicit instruction means directly teaching the skills, strategies, and processes that underlie competencies. Rather than hoping students will develop critical thinking through exposure to challenging content, explicit instruction involves teaching them the specific reasoning patterns, evidence evaluation methods, and reflection practices that constitute critical thinking.

    What the Research Converges On

    Despite their different origins and emphases, the major international competency frameworks—Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21), the IB Approaches to Learning (ATL), the OECD Learning Compass 2030, and the Assessment and Teaching of 21st Century Skills (ATC21S)—converge on four core competency areas. These are the 4Cs: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and creativity (or innovation). Beneath these broad categories lie more specific capabilities: problem-solving, metacognition, information literacy, digital literacy, and self-regulation.

    This convergence matters. When independent frameworks developed by different organizations reach the same conclusions, it suggests they have identified something fundamental about what matters for human flourishing and success in modern society. These are not fashionable buzzwords; they are persistent findings about what equips people to think well, work with others, and adapt to change.

    » In Practice

    A student studying the factors that led to the Industrial Revolution might develop factual knowledge (dates, names, inventions) through traditional instruction. But if she never explicitly learns how to evaluate historical sources for bias, how to construct a causal argument, or how to communicate her findings persuasively to an audience, she has not developed critical thinking, collaboration, or communication competencies—even though she has been immersed in rigorous content.

    Yet the frameworks differ in important ways. P21 emphasises workforce readiness and economic outcomes. OECD's framework foregrounds student agency and transformative learning. ATC21S integrates cognitive science and assessment. And the Danish educational tradition of dannelse—often translated as "formation" or "Bildung"—offers something unique: a philosophical foundation for competency education that reaches beyond employability to ask deeper questions about what kind of person we are forming, what kind of citizen, what kind of thinker.

    The Danish Advantage: Dannelse as Foundation

    Denmark's educational tradition provides a richer conceptual home for competency development than any international framework offers alone. The concept of dannelse frames education not as skill acquisition for the labour market, but as holistic formation—the development of the whole person as a critical, ethical, culturally engaged individual. Associated concepts like fællesskab (community), trivsel (well-being), and karakterdannelse (character formation) embed competency development in a larger vision of human growth.

    This is not mere philosophy. It is actionable. When students understand that learning to think critically is part of forming themselves as informed citizens, not merely preparing for a job, the stakes feel different. When teachers see collaboration not as a 21st-century workplace skill but as an expression of fællesskab—the human need to work together, to belong, to contribute to something larger than oneself—the pedagogies they choose reflect that deeper purpose.

    The Danish context also offers practical examples. The government's recent Forberedt på fremtiden V agreement (2025) has introduced a new gymnasium track—the epx (erhvervs- og professionsrettet eksamen)—that bridges academic and vocational education through practice-oriented, competency-based learning. And Silkeborg Gymnasium, Denmark's largest, has created a Learning Laboratory (Læringslaboratoriet) where teachers collaborate systematically to develop pedagogical approaches to competency development, including structured integration of artificial intelligence as a learning-promoting tool grounded in the digital dannelse framework.

    ? For Reflection

    How might competency education feel different if framed as "formation" (becoming a better thinker, person, citizen) rather than "skill acquisition" (preparing for employment)? What changes in how you would teach?

    What Cognitive Science Reveals About Learning

    To teach competencies well, teachers need to understand how learning works. Three findings from cognitive science are particularly actionable:

    First, retrieval practice works. Dunlosky and colleagues (2013) reviewed hundreds of studies on learning strategies and found that retrieval practice—repeatedly bringing knowledge to mind—is the single most effective study technique. Yet many students spend their study time rereading or cramming, which feels productive but produces shallow, fragile learning. Effective teachers help students test themselves, space their practice over time, and interleave different types of problems.

    Second, spaced learning dramatically outperforms massed practice. Studying one topic intensely for six hours produces worse retention than studying it for one hour, then returning to it after days or weeks. The struggle of remembering something you learned before is where learning happens. Curriculum design that returns to important ideas across the year, rather than covering them once and moving on, produces stronger learning.

    Third, deep processing beats shallow processing. When students engage in semantic processing—thinking about meaning, making connections, personalising content—they remember far better than when they engage in surface-level encoding (reading the same sentence three times, highlighting key words). This finding explains why the note-taking strategies students often use (verbatim transcription on laptops) are less effective than slower, more reflective approaches like handwriting, which forces paraphrasing and synthesis.

    These cognitive science findings explain why certain classroom practices work. They provide the evidence base for intentional design.

    Building Thinking Classrooms as a Transformation Model

    Liljedahl's research on Building Thinking Classrooms (BTC) offers a concrete, research-backed model for transforming classrooms from passive to active while simultaneously developing the competencies this curriculum aims to cultivate. The fourteen practices that constitute BTC are grounded in cognitive science and have been tested in thousands of classrooms across multiple countries, including in Danish schools.

    Three of these practices produce the highest impact and deserve special emphasis. Visibly Random Groups—assigning students to work with different partners each lesson through random selection—breaks social barriers and creates an expectation of 100% thinking engagement after about six weeks. Vertical Non-Permanent Surfaces—having students work on whiteboards or chart paper on the wall rather than at desks—changes thinking itself: students think more continuously, for longer periods, and take greater intellectual risk because their thinking is external and temporary rather than recorded permanently.

    Thinking tasks given verbally, early in the lesson—rather than written, later—produce significantly more engagement and thinking than their written counterparts. These three practices, implemented together, create an environment where critical thinking, collaboration, risk-taking, and metacognition develop naturally from the structure of the classroom itself, rather than from exhortations to "think critically" or "collaborate better."

    The Digital Dilemma: Protecting Essential Skills

    While digital technology offers genuine learning benefits, research has identified real risks to several essential skills that require intentional protection. Four warrant particular attention in the Danish context.

    Deep reading—the slow, immersive engagement with complex texts that builds comprehension and analytical thinking—faces pressure from digital habits. Meta-analyses show a "screen inferiority effect" (d = −0.21): reading on screens produces worse comprehension than reading on paper, especially for demanding informational texts. The Stavanger Declaration (2019), signed by over 130 literacy researchers, concluded that this effect is real and that digital reading fosters more superficial processing.

    Sustained attention is threatened not by technology itself, but by the specific problem of digital distraction and task-switching. Students with laptop access during lectures perform significantly worse on memory tests than those without devices. Most strikingly, the mere presence of a smartphone—even turned off—reduces available cognitive capacity. The design of digital tools deliberately exploits involuntary attention using reward schedules, infinite scroll, and autoplay, making sustained focus harder.

    Handwriting activates broader neural networks than typing and appears important for memory and learning. High-density EEG studies show that handwriting produces significantly greater brain connectivity than typing, particularly in regions associated with learning and memory. Students who take handwritten notes outperform laptop note-takers on conceptual questions, likely because handwriting's slower pace forces summarisation and semantic processing.

    Critical evaluation of sources has become harder, not easier, in the digital age. Even university students struggle to reliably distinguish credible from non-credible online sources. Generative AI compounds this challenge, creating plausible-sounding but false content that existing literacy frameworks do not fully address.

    The response should not be technology rejection. Rather, it should be what might be called "intentional design": protecting time for print reading, handwriting, and focused work; explicitly teaching digital literacy and source evaluation; and making deliberate, visible choices about when technology adds value and when analogue approaches serve learning better.

    ⚠ Caveat and Nuance

    It is crucial to avoid "doomster" narratives about technology destroying cognition—a concern as old as Plato's warning about writing weakening memory. Technology is not monolithic. Many of these skills are at risk not from technology itself but from how it is used. Educational technology designed with deep processing principles can enhance learning. Context matters: passive technology use has different effects than creation and collaboration. The goal is intentional integration, not rejection.

    AI Changes Everything—But Dannelse Provides the Answer

    The emergence of generative AI accelerates the challenge and clarifies the imperative. AI accelerates the erosion of skills already under pressure—deep reading, sustained effort, memory—because the motivation to engage in these skills weakens when an AI can summarise any text, answer almost any question, or generate competent prose in seconds. Simultaneously, generative AI makes human competencies like critical thinking, metacognition, ethical reasoning, and questioning more valuable. The person who cannot think critically about AI-generated content is not equipped for the future.

    The Danish concept of digital dannelse provides a productive response. Rather than treating AI as a technology problem, digital dannelse frames it as a formation question: "What kind of thinker am I becoming if I outsource this to AI?" "When does using AI help me learn, and when does it prevent me from learning?" These are not technology questions; they are questions about the kind of person one is becoming.

    Silkeborg Gymnasium exemplifies this approach. Rather than banning or uncritically embracing AI, they have positioned it as læringsfremmende—learning-promoting—and have trained all teachers to make deliberate choices about when AI enhances thinking and when it replaces it. This is dannelse applied to the most urgent challenge schools currently face.

    Growth Mindset: Real but Modest

    One finding warrants careful attention: growth mindset works, but the effect is smaller than initially believed. Meta-analyses show that growth mindset interventions have an effect size of d = 0.08–0.10. This is real, but much smaller than Dweck's early work suggested. The strongest effects appear for lower-achieving and disadvantaged students in supportive school cultures. The practical takeaway is important: mindset interventions are insufficient alone. But Building Thinking Classrooms, through its structural practices (VRG, VNPS, thinking tasks), creates a growth mindset environment without relying on motivational slogans or poster campaigns. Structure trumps exhortation.

    The Implementation Layer: Making It Real

    The research on competencies, learning, and pedagogy is strong. But there is a critical gap between research and classroom practice. Two practical tools bridge this gap:

    Effective feedback has a large average effect size (d = 0.73) according to Hattie and Timperley (2007), but the type of feedback matters enormously. Process feedback and self-regulation feedback—feedback that helps students understand the strategies and thinking they used—accelerate learning. Person-level praise ("You're so smart") is counterproductive. The implication is direct: schools must teach teachers to give feedback that develops competencies.

    Visible Thinking Routines—protocols developed by Harvard Project Zero such as See-Think-Wonder, Claim-Support-Question, and I Used to Think/Now I Think—make thinking explicit and student-facing. They complement BTC's structural practices by giving students language and process for engaging in the thinking moves that constitute critical thinking and metacognition.

    Connections

    This chapter establishes the case for why competency development must be intentional, explicit, and grounded in evidence. The following chapters explore what competencies matter in the 21st century (Chapter 2) and what skills require deliberate protection in a digital age where technology shapes how students think (Chapter 3). Together, Part I makes the foundation of the case: schools that want to develop students as learners and individuals must do so intentionally, using approaches grounded in cognitive science and Danish educational values, while remaining vigilant about protecting essential skills in a digital age.
