Chapter 11 — Creativity & Creative Thinking

◉ Overview

Every major 21st-century competency framework names creativity as essential. Yet it remains the competency most often left to chance — assumed to be innate, relegated to the arts, or crowded out by content coverage. This chapter argues that creativity is a cognitive skill as teachable as critical thinking, examines the evidence for how it develops, and offers practical frameworks for embedding creative thinking into a TOK classroom that presents learning experiences rather than top-down instruction.

The Creativity Misconception

There is a persistent cultural myth that creativity is a fixed trait — something you either have or you don't. Ken Robinson's famous 2006 TED talk put the counter-argument memorably: children enter school bursting with creative confidence, and education systematically trains it out of them by stigmatising mistakes, privileging certain subjects over others, and rewarding convergence on correct answers rather than divergence toward novel ones.

Robinson's critique resonated because it described something many teachers recognise intuitively. But the research base goes further than cultural critique. Meta-analyses across cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and education consistently find that creativity follows a "use it or lose it" pattern — it is a capacity that develops with deliberate practice and atrophies without it. A 2025 systematic review of 58 empirical studies identified 22 specific teacher behaviours that foster creativity in students, organised across six dimensions (Brauer, Ormiston, & Beausaert, 2025). These are learnable practices, not personality traits.

The implication for educators is clear: creativity is not something we wait for students to display. It is something we design for.

ℹ Key Concept — Creative Confidence

Tom and David Kelley, founders of IDEO and the Stanford d.school, define creative confidence as the belief in one's capacity to generate ideas and create change. Their research shows that the primary barrier to creativity is not lack of talent but fear — fear of being wrong, fear of looking foolish, fear of ambiguity. Building creative confidence means creating environments where intellectual risk-taking is safe and valued (Kelley & Kelley, 2013).

Divergent and Convergent Thinking: The Double Engine

J.P. Guilford's foundational distinction between divergent thinking — generating multiple possible solutions — and convergent thinking — narrowing toward the best solution — remains the backbone of creativity research (Guilford, 1967). But recent work has refined the relationship between these two modes. A 2025 study published in Thinking Skills and Creativity demonstrates that divergent and convergent thinking are linked, not opposed: both are necessary for effective creativity, and individuals strong in one tend to be strong in the other.

Divergent thinking has four measurable components: fluency (generating many ideas), flexibility (varying one's approach), originality (producing novel ideas), and elaboration (developing ideas in detail). These can be assessed — Guilford's Alternative Uses Test, which asks students to generate as many uses as possible for a common object, remains widely used in research — and they can be trained.

The relationship to critical thinking, the subject of Ch-04-Critical-Thinking, is not competitive but symbiotic. Critical thinking without creativity is merely fault-finding — it can identify weaknesses but cannot generate alternatives. Creativity without critical thinking is undirected ideation — it produces possibilities but cannot evaluate them. In a TOK classroom, students need both: divergent thinking to generate multiple perspectives on a knowledge question, and convergent thinking to synthesise, evaluate, and construct a justified position.

? For Reflection

Think about a recent lesson you taught or experienced. At what moments were students asked to generate multiple possibilities (divergent thinking)? At what moments were they asked to evaluate and select (convergent thinking)? Was one mode dominant? What would happen if you deliberately designed for both?

The OECD Creative Thinking Framework

The 2022 PISA assessment included, for the first time, a large-scale measure of creative thinking across over 60 countries and 140,000 fifteen-year-olds. The OECD framework defines creative thinking as the competence to generate, evaluate, and improve ideas that result in original and effective solutions, advances in knowledge, and impactful expressions (OECD, 2022).

The framework identifies three facets of creative competence. First, generating diverse ideas — flexible thinking that produces variety across domains. Second, generating creative ideas — searching for appropriate and original solutions within specific contexts. Third, evaluating and improving ideas — assessing limitations and finding original improvements. This third facet is crucial because it embeds critical evaluation within the creative process itself, reinforcing the symbiotic relationship between creative and critical thinking.

For TOK, the OECD framework validates what the course already does: examining knowledge claims requires generating diverse perspectives (facet one), developing original arguments (facet two), and refining those arguments through evaluation and counter-argument (facet three). The expanded 150-hour programme creates space to make this creative process explicit and deliberate rather than implicit and accidental.

Design Thinking as Pedagogy

The Stanford d.school's design thinking model offers a practical classroom framework for structuring creative inquiry: Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. What makes design thinking particularly powerful for education is its insistence that everyone has creative capacity and that the process is iterative — you are expected to fail, revise, and improve.

Applied to a TOK classroom, design thinking maps naturally onto epistemological inquiry. Empathise becomes understanding multiple cultural and disciplinary perspectives on a knowledge question. Define becomes clarifying what the question actually asks — what are its assumptions, its scope, its stakes? Ideate becomes generating diverse knowledge claims and counter-arguments without prematurely judging them. Prototype becomes drafting an essay structure or exhibition concept. Test becomes revising based on peer feedback and self-assessment, refining justifications and addressing weaknesses.

This approach transforms the TOK essay and exhibition from products students produce for a teacher into iterative inquiry processes students undertake for themselves. The teacher's role shifts from assessor to facilitator — providing structure, asking questions, and creating the conditions for creative-critical thinking to emerge.

» In Practice — Design Thinking in TOK

A TOK teacher introduces the prescribed title: "How can we distinguish between good and bad interpretations?" Rather than lecturing on theories of interpretation, the teacher runs a design thinking cycle. Students first empathise by interviewing classmates from different IB subjects about how interpretation works in their discipline. They define the core tension by mapping where disciplines agree and diverge. They ideate by brainstorming at least ten possible thesis positions without evaluating them. They prototype by writing a one-page argument for their strongest position. They test by exchanging prototypes for peer critique. The final essay emerges from three iterations, not one.

Creativity and Student Agency

The deepest connection between creativity and this textbook's philosophy is agency. When students are positioned as recipients of knowledge — when the classroom operates top-down, with the teacher as the source and students as the destination — creative thinking is structurally impossible. Creativity requires authorship: the student must be the one generating, selecting, and refining ideas.

This connects to the project's central mantra, developed in Ch-18-Learning-to-Learn: every student is a teacher. When students explain their reasoning to peers, defend their interpretations, and teach concepts to others, they are engaged in the deepest form of creative-critical thinking — they must generate a coherent account, anticipate objections, and adapt their explanation in real time. The protégé effect is, in this sense, a creativity practice as much as a learning strategy.

Practically, this means designing TOK lessons as learning experiences rather than information deliveries. Open-ended questions replace closed ones. Students choose which Areas of Knowledge to explore. Peer dialogue replaces teacher monologue. Assessment rewards the quality of thinking, not conformity to a model answer. The classroom becomes what Peter Liljedahl, in Ch-21-Building-Thinking-Classrooms, calls a space where thinking is the default activity — and creative thinking is a natural subset of that default.

⚠ The Implementation Gap

Research consistently finds that teachers believe creativity is important but do not implement creative pedagogies in practice. The barrier is not belief but confidence and practical knowledge. Teachers worry about losing control, covering content, or not knowing how to assess creative work. This chapter's practical strategies — design thinking, divergent-convergent cycles, rubric co-creation, peer critique — are designed to address that gap directly. Creativity does not require abandoning structure; it requires structuring for openness.

Practical Strategies for the Classroom

Several evidence-based approaches can embed creative thinking into daily practice without requiring a complete curriculum overhaul.

Open-ended questioning is the simplest shift. Instead of asking students to describe a concept, ask them to generate multiple interpretations of it, then evaluate which is most compelling and why. This single change activates both divergent and convergent thinking within a single task.

Collaborative brainstorming with constraints uses the creative paradox that limitations enhance rather than suppress creativity. Give students a knowledge question and a constraint — "generate five arguments, but none can use examples from the natural sciences" — and watch the quality of thinking increase as students are forced out of familiar territory.

Thinking routines from Harvard's Project Zero, discussed in Ch-23-Teaching-Techniques-and-Thinking-Routines, are inherently creative tools. "See-Think-Wonder" asks students to observe, interpret, and question — a divergent process. "Claim-Support-Question" asks students to assert, justify, and challenge — a convergent process. Used together, they create a complete creative-critical cycle.

Project-based learning, explored in Ch-27-Project-Based-Learning, provides the extended context in which creative thinking can develop fully. Real-world problems with multiple valid approaches, authentic audiences, and iterative feedback cycles are the richest environments for creative competence development.
