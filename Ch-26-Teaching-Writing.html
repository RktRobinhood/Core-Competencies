Chapter 26 — Teaching Writing Effectively

◉ Overview

Writing is central to learning across the curriculum. It is how students organise ideas, how they communicate understanding, and how they develop the critical thinking that is at the heart of this research project's competency focus. Yet writing instruction is often incidental—students are assigned essays with the assumption that exposure to exemplars is enough to develop writing skill. The What Works Clearinghouse practice guide for secondary writing provides a compact, research-based architecture: explicitly teach writing strategies through a Model-Practice-Reflect cycle, integrate writing and reading to emphasise key features, and use assessment and feedback to drive revision. This chapter operationalises that framework across subjects and scales.

The Core Finding: Explicit Strategy Instruction Works

The What Works Clearinghouse guide synthesises decades of writing research and identifies three high-priority recommendations. The strongest evidence (rated "strong") supports explicitly teaching writing strategies using a Model-Practice-Reflect cycle. This finding is not new, but it is often ignored in practice. Many classrooms assign writing without teaching the thinking behind it. A student is asked to "write a persuasive essay" without explicit instruction in how to construct an argument, without models of the thinking involved, and without guided practice before independence.

Explicit strategy instruction means:

Modeling: The teacher models the thinking involved in writing. This is not simply reading a finished exemplar (passive) but thinking aloud while writing or revising. "I need to state my position clearly in the opening sentence, so my reader knows what I am arguing. I could say... or I could say... The first option is clearer because..." This externalises the invisible decision-making that experienced writers do automatically.

Guided practice: Students attempt the strategy under teacher support. They might complete a partially written paragraph and explain their choices. They might write a single paragraph while the teacher observes and provides feedback. They might use sentence stems that scaffold the strategy: "I claim that… because…" or "The evidence shows… which suggests that…" Guided practice is not independent; the teacher is actively checking, redirecting, and affirming.

Reflection: Students reflect on what they did and why it worked (or didn't). "What did you do to make your opening paragraph engaging?" "How did you decide what evidence to include?" This reflection makes the strategy conscious, not just procedural, and helps students transfer the strategy to new writing tasks.

This three-part cycle mirrors Rosenshine's principles (modeling, guided practice, checking for understanding, independence) and can be embedded in lessons or across a unit.

Subject-Specific Applications

The strategic framework adapts to different writing purposes across subjects.

Language and Literature

In literature and composition, the focus is often on argumentation and evidence-based claims. The strategy to model is evidence-based paragraph construction:

Opening with a claim (topic sentence) that is specific and debatable, not vague ("Shakespeare used metaphor effectively" is too broad; "In Macbeth, Shakespeare uses metaphors of blood to show Macbeth's moral corruption" is specific and arguable).

Providing specific evidence from the text (quotations, scenes) that supports the claim.

Explaining the reasoning that connects evidence to claim ("This suggests that… because…" or "This reveals that…" not just "This shows").

A teacher might model: "I've chosen this quote because it directly supports my claim. Now I need to explain why it supports my claim, not just repeat the quote. I'll explain what the quote reveals about the character or theme." Then students practice with a different text, possibly using a template:

My claim is [state specific argument]. The evidence is [quote with page number]. This evidence supports my claim because [explain the connection].

Guided practice uses shared texts and supported writing. Reflection asks students to identify their strongest claim-evidence-reasoning paragraph and analyse why it works.

Science

In science, the focus is on evidence-explanation writing: describing observations, using data, and reasoning to conclusions.

The strategy to model is claim-evidence-reasoning (CER): making a claim about what happened or what caused something, providing evidence (data, observations), and giving reasoning that uses scientific principles to explain why the evidence supports the claim.

A teacher might model: "My observation was that the temperature stayed constant even though I added heat. My claim is that a phase change was happening. My evidence is the data showing temperature plateau. My reasoning is that during a phase change, all energy goes into breaking molecular bonds, not increasing temperature." Students then write CER statements about their own lab data, with sentence stems:

We claim that [state what happened or the cause]. Our evidence is [describe observations or data]. Our reasoning is that [use scientific principles to explain why the evidence supports the claim].

Guided practice might begin with a teacher-provided data set and a partially written CER, with students completing the explanation. Reflection focuses on clarity of reasoning: "Does the reader understand why your evidence supports your claim, or does it just seem like a list?"

Humanities and Social Sciences

In history and social sciences, students must integrate source analysis with argumentation. The strategy is source-integration writing: using evidence from sources to support historically contextualised claims.

A teacher models: "My claim is that the Industrial Revolution transformed family structure. My evidence is this historical document that describes factory work conditions and their effect on family time. Rather than just quoting the source, I need to contextualise it: who wrote this, when, and why does their perspective matter? Then I need to explain what the source reveals about my claim." Students practice with a new source, guided toward:

My argument is [state the historical claim]. This is supported by [source type and basic info], which states that [specific evidence]. This reveals that [interpretation] because [historical reasoning about context and significance].

Languages

In language learning, explicit strategy instruction addresses both mechanics and communication. Students need explicit teaching of text structure (how to open and close an email, how to structure a narrative), vocabulary use, and error correction patterns.

A teacher models: "I'm writing an email asking someone for help. The formal greeting is important—it sets a respectful tone. The opening sentence should clearly state my purpose. The body should provide necessary context. The closing should thank them and indicate next steps." Students then write a guided-practice email while the teacher checks structure and mechanics. Reflection focuses on clarity and appropriateness.

Integrating Writing and Reading

The second recommendation from the WWC guide (moderate evidence) is integrate writing and reading to emphasise key writing features. Students learn more about writing when they read as writers, noticing features that good writers use. This integration can happen through:

Shared reading with analysis of writing features. Before writing persuasively, students read a model argument and label the claim, evidence, and reasoning. They notice how the writer opens with a question to engage the reader or how the writer acknowledges counterarguments. This makes the structure visible.

Annotation and discussion. Students annotate a text for rhetorical features ("This is an emotional appeal" or "This is a logical fallacy") and discuss why the author made these choices. This builds conscious awareness of choices writers make.

Comparison of texts with different purposes. Students read a news report and a persuasive essay on the same topic and discuss how the writing differs. This helps them understand that writing is purposeful and audience-aware.

Noticing and naming. Rather than jargon-heavy instruction, the teacher and students notice: "The writer starts with a story—that's a hook. It makes me curious." Naming what good writers do, and then having students try it, is more powerful than teaching terminology first.

Assessment and Revision Cycles

The third recommendation (minimal evidence but practical importance) is use assessment to inform instruction and feedback. This operationalises the effective feedback principles from Chapter 18 in the writing domain.

Rubrics with clear criteria tell students what quality looks like. A typical rubric for argumentation might include: clarity of claim, quality of evidence, strength of reasoning, organisation, and conventions. Rather than a single holistic score, separate criteria allow targeted feedback and revision. "Your evidence is strong and specific; your reasoning explaining why it supports your claim needs development."

Low-stakes opportunities to draft and revise before high-stakes assessment reduce anxiety and allow students to practise the writing process. A student might write a draft of an argument, receive process-level feedback, revise, and then submit the final version for a grade. This makes it clear that good writing is revised writing, not first-draft writing.

Peer feedback protocols trained explicitly (see Chapter 18) help writers improve. A peer using a feedback frame ("Your strongest paragraph is… because…. You might strengthen… by…") is more helpful than generic praise or criticism.

Revision work done in class ensures that students act on feedback while it is still relevant. Time carved out for revision—even 20 minutes in class—signals that revision is valued and makes it more likely to happen. Feedback without revision time is wasted.

Implementation: A 6-Week School Model

A school piloting improved writing instruction across subjects might structure it:

Week 1: Staff calibration. All teachers, across subjects, learn the Model-Practice-Reflect cycle and examine a shared rubric. Departments map writing strategies (mathematics: explaining problem-solving process; science: CER; humanities: evidence-based argument; languages: clear communication) onto the general cycle.

Week 2: Pilot one unit per subject using the cycle. Teachers model a writing strategy, guide practice with feedback, and reflect with students. Data collection: What proportion of students can produce writing that meets the basic criteria?

Week 3: Introduce structured peer feedback. Using a simple protocol (warm/cool feedback or a rubric-based checklist), students provide feedback on each other's writing. Teachers observe and adjust the protocol based on what they see.

Week 4: Integrate reading-to-writing tasks. Teachers select exemplars in their subjects and explicitly teach students to notice writing features before they write.

Week 5: Mid-pilot writing sample moderation. Teachers review a selection of writing samples from across subjects and discuss calibration: Are our standards for "clear argument" or "well-explained reasoning" aligned?

Week 6: Revision cycles and summative assessment. Students revise earlier work based on feedback. Teachers assess a new writing sample per subject, compare to Week 2 baseline, and reflect on progress.

Common Pitfalls

Assigning extended writing before explicit instruction. "Write a 5-page essay" without teaching how to structure argument, provide evidence, and reason is setting many students up to fail. Start with single paragraphs and guided practice.

Giving generic feedback without next-step direction. "Needs more detail" is not actionable. "Your reasoning paragraph needs to explain why the evidence supports your claim, not just repeat the evidence" is specific and actionable.

Assessing final product without teaching process. If students have not been taught how to organise ideas, develop evidence, and revise, assessing the final essay product seems unfair. Assessing process (Did you use the strategy? Did you engage with peer feedback?) alongside product is more equitable.

Assuming writing is the language department's responsibility. Writing is central to all subjects. When mathematics, science, and humanities all explicitly teach writing strategies—adapted to their disciplines—students transfer the skills across contexts. When writing is siloed in language arts, transfer is limited.

Alignment with Thinking Classrooms and Competencies

Explicit writing instruction supports the thinking emphasis of Building Thinking Classrooms. Writing requires thinking (what is my claim? what evidence supports it? how do I explain the connection?). Writing makes thinking visible. When students write to explain their reasoning in science, to argue a position in social sciences, or to analyse literature, they are externalising thinking that would otherwise remain private.

Writing also develops the critical thinking and communication competencies central to this research project. When students must justify claims with evidence, when they must consider counterarguments, when they must explain their thinking to an audience, they are developing disciplinary and transferable intellectual capacities.

» In Practice

A Year 10 history class is studying colonisation. The teacher models how to write a historically contextualised argument: "I want to argue that early colonisers were motivated by economic gain, not primarily by religious or humanitarian concerns. My evidence is this journal entry from a merchant, written in 1620. Rather than just quoting it, I need to contextualise it: who is the merchant, why does his perspective matter, and what does this reveal about motivation in that historical moment? Then I need to reason about what this evidence tells us." Students then write a paragraph using a new source, guided by a template. They receive feedback focusing on clarity of reasoning. They revise. This cycle—modeling, guided practice, feedback, revision—takes four lessons but builds genuine historical thinking, not just content knowledge.

Connections

Writing instruction operationalises the thinking and feedback systems discussed in previous chapters. When combined with Building Thinking Classrooms, where students think continuously, and effective feedback, where students revise based on process-level guidance, writing becomes a powerful thinking and learning tool. Writing is also central to project-based learning, where written communication is often a key product, and to reflective practice, where students write to articulate their learning.
