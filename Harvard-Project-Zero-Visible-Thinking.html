    Project Zero — Visible Thinking

    Bibliographic Details

    • Institution: Harvard Graduate School of Education, Project Zero
    • Principal Investigators: David Perkins and Shari Tishman
    • Origin: Emerged from the Innovating with Intelligence project at Lemshaga Akademi, Sweden
    • URL: https://pz.harvard.edu/projects/visible-thinking

    What Is Visible Thinking?

    Visible Thinking (VT) is a "flexible and systematic research-based conceptual framework" that integrates thinking skill development with subject-matter learning across disciplines. It does not treat thinking as a separate subject but as something cultivated within and through content areas.

    The framework emphasises three mutually reinforcing elements:

    1. Thinking routines — Structured prompts and processes that scaffold specific thinking moves
    2. Documentation of student thinking — Making thinking visible so it can be reflected on and assessed
    3. Reflective professional practice — Teachers attending to the culture of thinking in their classrooms

    Core Thinking Dispositions

    Originally designed to foster thinking dispositions including:

    • Truth-seeking
    • Understanding
    • Fairness
    • Imagination

    The framework has expanded to include thinking through art and cultural objects.

    Thinking Routines (Selected)

    Thinking routines are structured processes that "loosely guide learners' thought processes and encourage active processing." Key routines relevant to this textbook:

    Routine Purpose
    See-Think-Wonder Careful observation before interpretation
    Think-Pair-Share Individual thinking before collaborative articulation
    I Used to Think… Now I Think… Metacognitive reflection on changed understanding
    Claim-Support-Question Evidence-based reasoning
    Circle of Viewpoints Perspective-taking across stakeholders
    Connect-Extend-Challenge Linking new to prior knowledge
    3-2-1 Bridge Bridging initial to refined understanding
    Chalk Talk Silent written dialogue to surface thinking
    Color-Symbol-Image Non-verbal synthesis of ideas
    Compass Points Exploring Excites, Worries, Needs, and Suggestions
    Question Sorts Distinguishing factual from interpretive questions

    Application in Schools

    VT routines make student thinking visible in classrooms, helping educators:

    • Identify misconceptions before they consolidate
    • Develop a culture where thinking is expected, valued, and shared
    • Build metacognitive awareness without making it a separate subject
    • Create equitable participation (all students engage with routines, not just the willing)

    Relevance to Textbook

    Chapter 23 (Teaching Techniques and Thinking Routines) draws heavily on VT as one of two main frameworks alongside Doug Lemov's TLAC techniques. The routines are particularly relevant to:

    • Ch-04 (Critical Thinking): Claim-Support-Question and Question Sorts scaffold critical analysis
    • Ch-15 (Metacognition): "I Used to Think… Now I Think…" is an explicit metacognitive protocol
    • Ch-21 (Building Thinking Classrooms): VT complements Liljedahl's structural practices with specific thinking moves
    • Ch-28 (Reflective Practice): "I Used to Think… Now I Think…" is directly cited as a reflection tool
