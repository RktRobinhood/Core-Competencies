    Appendix A4 — TOK Exhibition Object Ideas: AI, Knowledge, and Human Competency

    ◉ Overview

    The TOK exhibition requires students to present three objects with commentaries showing how TOK manifests in the world around them. This appendix provides a curated object bank organized by TOK prescribed prompts. These are not model submissions but brainstorming resources. Students should choose their own authentic objects and build original commentaries that show genuine thinking about knowledge.

    How to Use This Object Bank

    For students: Read through the prompts and object ideas to understand what kinds of objects work well and why. Use these examples to inspire your own ideas, not to copy them. The strongest exhibitions use objects that are personally meaningful to the student.

    For teachers: Share this bank with students as inspiration, not as a menu to choose from. Use it to teach what makes an object work: it must be specific, real, and connected to a genuine knowledge question. Guide students to generate their own objects using the brainstorming process in A2-Pedagogical-Playbook.

    Objects Organized by Common TOK Prompts

    Prompt: What Counts as Knowledge?

    Object ideas:

    • A screenshot of a calculator app showing a result versus a handwritten mathematical derivation
    • A generative AI response with no sources versus a citation list from a peer-reviewed journal article
    • A student's first draft (rough, uncertain) versus the same essay after feedback and revision
    • A selfie with a landmark versus a professional travel photograph of the same landmark (what makes a photograph "authoritative"?)
    • A Wikipedia page for a topic that's been heavily edited versus the same topic in an academic encyclopedia
    • A news article published immediately after an event versus the same event analyzed in a long-form investigation weeks later

    Why these work: - They make visible the difference between claimed knowledge and verified knowledge - They show how sources, method, and process affect what counts as knowledge - They are concrete objects students can analyze

    Knowledge questions raised: - "Does the source of knowledge matter to its validity?" - "Is a fluent explanation the same as knowledge?" - "Does knowledge require justification, or is access enough?"

    Prompt: On What Grounds Might We Doubt a Knowledge Claim?

    Object ideas:

    • A confident chatbot response on a topic followed by fact-check showing the response was inaccurate
    • A news article with an editor's correction note appended later
    • Social media posts on a topic with conflicting claims (climate, vaccines, etc.)
    • A student's exam answer that sounded confident but was later marked as incorrect
    • A historical document showing how "experts" once believed something now known to be false
    • A screen recording of an AI being asked the same question twice and giving different answers

    Why these work: - They make visible the gap between confidence and accuracy - They show how knowledge changes and how certainty can be misplaced - They are concrete evidence of the need for verification

    Knowledge questions raised: - "When should we doubt a claim even when it's confidently stated?" - "How do we tell the difference between certainty and truth?" - "What would convince me to change my mind?"

    Prompt: Are Some Types of Knowledge Less Open to Interpretation Than Others?

    Object ideas:

    • A mathematical proof showing a single right answer versus a historical debate about the same historical period with multiple defensible interpretations
    • A periodic table (periodic and definite) versus different translations of a poem (each translation makes different choices)
    • A statistical model output with confidence intervals showing uncertainty ranges
    • Multiple eyewitness accounts of the same traffic accident, conflicting but all sincere
    • A scientific abstract versus an art critic's review of the same artwork
    • A piece of code that either works (executes correctly) or doesn't versus a piece of music that people interpret differently

    Why these work: - They show that some domains have more definite answers than others - They reveal why methods differ (because domains differ) - They show that "interpretation" doesn't mean "anything goes"

    Knowledge questions raised: - "Is a difference between domains or a difference in the nature of knowledge?" - "Can mathematics be interpreted, or is it purely objective?" - "What makes some interpretations better than others?"

    Prompt: Is Bias Inevitable in the Production of Knowledge?

    Object ideas:

    • Screenshots of AI image generation for the same prompt on different devices/accounts, showing demographic or stylistic skew
    • A Google search results page for a politically loaded term from different geographic locations or browser profiles
    • Historical photographs of "anthropological subjects" that reveal the photographer's perspective and power dynamics
    • A movie or TV show trailer that emphasizes different aspects depending on the target audience
    • An algorithm-generated content feed (news, music, social media) showing what the algorithm prioritizes
    • A textbook section on a historical event compared with a primary source document from a different perspective

    Why these work: - They make bias visible and concrete - They show that bias is not simply a flaw; it's built into how we see - They are authentic examples students can analyze

    Knowledge questions raised: - "Can we ever escape our perspective?" - "Is objective knowledge possible, or just less biased knowledge?" - "Whose perspective is excluded in this representation?"

    Prompt: Should Some Knowledge Not Be Sought on Ethical Grounds?

    Object ideas:

    • A public discussion or debate about deepfake generation technology and its risks
    • Research article abstract on dual-use biotechnology that could be used for harm
    • A corporate policy about data collection and privacy
    • Screenshots from a terms-of-service document revealing how personal data is used
    • An article about academic research with ethical constraints (e.g., limits on human experimentation)
    • A debate transcript or policy document about whether certain research should be funded or pursued

    Why these work: - They make ethics a practical knowledge issue, not just abstract principle - They show real tradeoffs and stakeholder perspectives - They reveal how knowledge choices have consequences

    Knowledge questions raised: - "Who should decide which knowledge is acceptable to pursue?" - "Can you unbake a cake? Can you un-discover something?" - "Is 'don't ask' the right response to a dangerous question?"

    Prompt: What Role Do Experts Play in Influencing Our Acquisition of Knowledge?

    Object ideas:

    • An expert panel statement on a policy question versus a viral social media summary of the same issue by a non-expert influencer
    • A statement from the American Medical Association about a health issue versus self-help claims on social media
    • A classroom rubric showing explicit criteria used to evaluate essay quality
    • A peer-reviewed research paper with acknowledgments showing the collective work behind the publication
    • Credentials and bios of panelists in a debate showing how expertise shapes participation
    • A before/after screenshot of a Wikipedia article that's been corrected by expert editors

    Why these work: - They show that expertise matters but is not always recognized - They raise questions about authority and how we decide who to trust - They are concrete examples of knowledge gatekeeping

    Knowledge questions raised: - "How do we recognize genuine expertise versus confident speaking?" - "Who decides what counts as expert knowledge?" - "Can non-experts contribute to knowledge, or do we need credentialed experts?"

    Prompt: How Can We Distinguish Between Knowledge, Belief, and Opinion?

    Object ideas:

    • A peer-reviewed research abstract (knowledge) versus a personal blog post on the same topic (belief/opinion)
    • Evidence table from a documented scientific debate showing the data both sides reference
    • A court transcript showing how the same evidence is interpreted differently by lawyers on opposite sides
    • A mathematical proof (knowledge) versus mathematical intuition or conjecture not yet proven
    • An editorial in a newspaper (opinion) versus the news article it comments on
    • Student work showing their initial hypothesis, then data collected, then revised understanding

    Why these work: - They make visible the difference between knowing and believing - They show that the same information can support different conclusions - They are concrete examples students can analyze

    Knowledge questions raised: - "Is the difference between knowledge and opinion objective or subjective?" - "Can something be true but not yet known?" - "How much certainty do you need before claiming to know something?"

    Pedagogical Section: Exhibition Object Studio Format

    Brainstorming Phase (8 minutes for 8 objects)

    1. Choose one TOK prompt that genuinely interests you
    2. Generate eight candidate objects in 8 minutes (1 minute per object)
    3. One related to AI or technology
    4. One from your daily life
    5. One that shows different perspectives
    6. One about bias, ethics, or authority
    7. One from a subject you study
    8. One that contrasts with something else
    9. One that shows how knowledge changes
    10. One surprising or counterintuitive

    Don't evaluate yet; just generate.

    Evaluation Phase (Filter)

    For each candidate object, ask:

    • Is it specific and real? Not "science" but "a laboratory experiment on photosynthesis." Not "social media" but "a TikTok video about climate change I watched."
    • Does it raise a clear knowledge question? Could you write one sentence: "This object matters for TOK because..."?
    • Can a meaningful commentary be written? Will you have something substantive to say, or will you run out of ideas after one paragraph?

    Eliminate candidates that don't pass all three filters.

    Selection Phase

    From remaining candidates, select three that create contrast:

    • Don't use three versions of the same object (three AI outputs, three news articles)
    • Ensure they raise different kinds of knowledge questions
    • Ensure you have something different to say about each
    • Consider which objects you could find/access (they must be real)

    Justification Phase (1 sentence per object)

    Write one sentence for each: "This object matters for TOK because..."

    Examples: - "This object shows how AI can sound authoritative while making factual errors, raising the question of how we should evaluate sources when fluent language is abundant." - "This object reveals how the same historical event is interpreted differently depending on perspective, showing that history requires interpretation, not just collection of facts." - "This object demonstrates that bias in AI is not a flaw to be removed, but a reflection of choices embedded in data and algorithms, raising questions about whether objective algorithms are possible."

    Quality Checklist for Teachers and Students

    When evaluating a three-object exhibition set:

    Object relevance: - [ ] Each object is specific and real (not abstract or hypothetical) - [ ] The connection to a knowledge question is explicit - [ ] The student can explain why the object matters without the commentary

    Commentary quality: - [ ] The commentary addresses knowledge issues, not just the features of the object - [ ] Student voice and judgment are visible (not parroted definitions) - [ ] Different perspectives on the object are considered - [ ] Limitations or uncertainties are acknowledged - [ ] Ethical implications are addressed where relevant

    Set coherence: - [ ] The three objects create productive contrast (they don't all make the same point) - [ ] Together, they raise interesting questions about knowledge - [ ] The student has engaged in genuine inquiry, not just illustration of predetermined ideas

    Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

    Pitfall: Objects that illustrate a concept but don't raise a question

    Wrong: Selecting a calculator to illustrate that "technology helps us do math."

    Right: Selecting a situation where a calculator gives a confident wrong answer, to raise the question: "What role does human judgment play when we use technology?"

    Pitfall: Commentaries that are description, not analysis

    Wrong: "This is a Wikipedia article. It was written by volunteers. It has been edited many times."

    Right: "This Wikipedia article shows how knowledge is collaborative and emergent—it changes as new information and perspectives are added. But does collaborative authorship make it more reliable or less? Compared to a peer-reviewed article, Wikipedia is more democratic but less systematically vetted. This raises the question: Is knowledge more reliable when it's vetted by experts, or when many people can contribute?"

    Pitfall: Objects disconnected from student's own inquiry

    Wrong: Selecting objects only from the "object bank" provided by the school.

    Right: Observing something in your own life, wondering about it, and developing that observation into an object. ("I noticed that my friends and I often cite AI summaries of articles we haven't read. I took a screenshot. This object raises a question for TOK...")
