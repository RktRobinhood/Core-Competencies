    Chapter 33a — Implementation Frameworks and the 90-Day Blueprint

    ◉ Overview

    Implementation science offers research-based structures for turning evidence-informed ideas into stable, reliable classroom practices. This section translates implementation science into a practical 90-day blueprint and addresses the inevitable barriers that arise in school change. Two frameworks guide the work: Active Implementation Frameworks from NIRN and staged guidance from the Education Endowment Foundation.

    Why Implementation Science Matters

    Educational research identifies many effective practices: spaced retrieval, metacognitive instruction, collaborative learning, competency-based assessment. Yet the translation from research to reliable classroom practice is notoriously weak. Teachers attend a workshop, return to school inspired, and encounter barriers: timetables that don't support the practice, colleagues who don't understand it, students unfamiliar with new routines, insufficient materials, leadership that shifts priorities. Within weeks, the innovation fades and schools revert to familiar patterns.

    Fixsen et al. (2005) argued that identifying evidence-based practices is only half the problem. Reliable implementation is a separate challenge requiring its own methods, structures, and leadership. Over two decades, implementation science has developed frameworks for managing this translation. Recent guidance from the Education Endowment Foundation (2024) brings these methods into direct conversation with school improvement, offering practical frameworks that schools can adopt without expertise in academic implementation research.

    The implication for a course on core competencies is direct: it is not enough to teach teachers about metacognition, critical thinking, or character development. Schools must create structures that make these competencies visible and valued in daily practice, protect time for their development, support teachers in teaching them well, and maintain fidelity over time as staff change and priorities shift.

    ℹ Key Concept — Implementation Fidelity

    Fidelity means faithful reproduction of the core elements of a practice. High fidelity does not mean rigid sameness; it means that the active ingredients—the core elements that make the practice effective—are present and recognizable. For example, thinking tasks in building thinking classrooms (Liljedahl, 2020) require three active ingredients: non-routine problems, tasks given early and verbally, and students standing in random groups. Other details (exact timing, specific topic) can vary. Implementation science uses fidelity checks to track whether schools are delivering the practice with sufficient quality to expect the intended effects.

    Two Practical Frameworks

    Active Implementation Frameworks (NIRN)

    The National Implementation Research Network (NIRN) at the University of North Carolina has developed Active Implementation Frameworks (NIRN, 2024; Fixsen et al., 2005) that identify five key elements that must work together:

    1. Usable innovations — The practice itself, with clear documentation of what it is and how to do it
    2. Implementation teams — Groups of people who lead, coordinate, and sustain the work
    3. Implementation drivers — Capacity components that support the competencies teachers need, the organizational supports (time, materials, feedback), and leadership
    4. Implementation stages — A sequence: exploration, preparation, initial implementation, and full implementation
    5. Improvement cycles — Structured reflection using Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycles to identify what's working and what needs adjustment

    What makes NIRN valuable is that it identifies implementation as a process with stages, not an event (Fixsen et al., 2005). Schools don't "implement" a practice once; they move through stages of increasing competence, with structured support at each stage.

    EEF School Implementation Guidance

    The Education Endowment Foundation (EEF, 2024) has translated implementation science into direct guidance for school leaders. Their staged model includes:

    Explore (Initial discovery) — Schools define the student problem precisely, select one high-leverage practice that addresses it, and identify the active ingredients (the core elements that make it work). Importantly, schools do not try to change everything at once; they choose one practice to implement well.

    Prepare (Getting ready) — Schools form an implementation team, build staff understanding through training, identify barriers in advance, and adapt timetables and resources to make the practice feasible. This is not quick; schools spend 2-3 weeks on preparation.

    Deliver (Launch and iterate) — Schools launch with coaching cycles where experienced practitioners observe, provide feedback, and solve barriers. Schools use short fidelity checks to track whether the active ingredients are present, not only outcome data. Barriers are addressed weekly; the focus is rapid problem-solving, not blame.

    Sustain (Making it permanent) — Once the practice is stable (typically 6-12 weeks in), schools embed it into normal planning and induction. New staff are trained in the practice. The school monitors for drift and retrains where needed. Only then does the school consider scaling the practice to other areas.

    » In Practice

    A school decides to implement daily retrieval practice (5-minute review at the start of each lesson, spaced to include previous units). The team uses the EEF framework: Explore phase identifies that weak long-term memory is limiting students' ability to tackle complex problems. The active ingredients are identified: retrieval questions covering prior learning, low stakes (no grading for correctness), brief (5 minutes), and frequent (daily). Prepare phase involves training all staff, identifying which retrieval questions teachers will use, and solving the timetable challenge of starting every lesson on time. Deliver phase involves department heads observing lessons, giving feedback, identifying that some teachers ask too many questions (violating the 5-minute constraint), and adjusting. Sustain phase embeds the routine into normal practice and new teacher induction.

    Practical Implementation Blueprint: 90 Days to Stable Practice

    Schools implementing a competency-focused approach can follow this timeline:

    Phase 1: Explore (Weeks 1–3)

    Task: Define the student problem and select one high-leverage practice.

    • Meet with implementation team: school leaders, interested teachers, potentially student representatives
    • Examine student data: Where are gaps in critical thinking? Where do students struggle with collaboration? Where is metacognition weak?
    • Define the problem precisely. Not "students need better thinking skills" but "Students struggle to revise their work; they resist feedback and often submit first drafts without reflection."
    • Select one practice that addresses this problem. Perhaps: structured revision protocols where students use thinking routines to reflect on their work before and after receiving feedback.
    • Identify active ingredients. What must be present for this practice to work? Explicit routines, low-stakes feedback, student voice in identifying what to revise, time for revision.

    Outputs: A one-page problem statement, a selected practice description, and a list of active ingredients.

    Phase 2: Prepare (Weeks 4–6)

    Task: Get the organization ready to implement.

    • Form implementation team (6-10 people): representatives from different departments, a building leader, ideally an external coach or critical friend
    • Build understanding through structured training. Not a one-off workshop, but multiple sessions where teachers understand the research behind the practice, see it modeled, and practice using it themselves
    • Identify barriers in advance: What timetable obstacles exist? What materials are needed? What concerns do teachers have? What previous experiences might teachers be bringing?
    • Adapt systems: If the practice requires time for revision, ensure that time exists in the timetable. If it requires specific feedback forms, design and prepare them. If it conflicts with existing practices, address the conflict explicitly.
    • Build staff buy-in through genuine dialogue, not mandates. Address concerns respectfully. Some resistance is normal and can point to real implementation challenges.

    Outputs: Training materials, adapted timetables, response to anticipated barriers, and visible leadership commitment.

    Phase 3: Deliver (Weeks 7–11)

    Task: Launch the practice with intensive support and rapid problem-solving.

    • Coaching cycles: Experienced practitioners observe lessons, provide feedback using a fidelity checklist, and help teachers refine implementation. This is not evaluation for judgment; it is support for improvement.
    • Fidelity checks: Weekly, the implementation team uses a short checklist to track whether active ingredients are present. If 80% of observed lessons show high fidelity, implementation is on track. If fidelity is lower, the team diagnoses why and adjusts training, materials, or expectations.
    • Barrier solving: Every week, the team meets to identify what's not working. Is time insufficient? Are students resisting the revision protocol? Are some teachers struggling to give good feedback? Each barrier gets a focused response.
    • Celebration of progress: Notice and celebrate early wins. When students show improvement in revision quality or teachers report that feedback feels more productive, make that visible.

    Outputs: Coaching notes, fidelity data, barrier logs, and refined implementation guidance.

    Phase 4: Sustain (Weeks 12+)

    Task: Embed the practice into normal school operations.

    • Induction: New staff learn the practice as part of onboarding, not as an optional professional development
    • Monitoring: Occasional fidelity checks continue to monitor for drift (gradual erosion of quality). If drift is detected, refresher training happens quickly.
    • Integration with curriculum: The practice becomes part of normal curriculum planning and resource development, not a separate initiative
    • Scaling: Only once the practice is stable and sustainable in the initial context do schools consider expanding it to other subjects or grade levels. Scaling too early, before the practice is truly stable, is a common failure mode.

    Outputs: Induction materials, monitoring schedules, integrated curriculum documents, and evidence of sustained implementation.

    Common Implementation Failure Modes

    Understanding these pitfalls helps schools avoid them:

    Launching too many initiatives simultaneously creates confusion and prevents any single change from becoming stable. Focus on one practice until it is reliable, then consider what to tackle next.

    Confusing announcement with implementation. Telling teachers about a new practice is not the same as implementing it. Teachers need training, practice, feedback, and time. Implementation takes weeks or months, not days.

    Tracking only outcome data and ignoring fidelity. If student engagement improves but fidelity to the practice is low, you may have improved outcomes by luck, not by implementing the practice well. Fidelity data tells you whether the practice is actually happening as designed.

    Weak middle-leader ownership. Department heads, curriculum coordinators, and team leaders must understand and own the implementation. If only senior leaders care, implementation fragments and fades.

    Scaling before stable use. Expanding a practice before it is reliable in one context almost always results in dilution and failure. Patience at this stage is important.

    Insufficient support during delivery. Coaching, feedback, and problem-solving during the launch phase are not optional add-ons; they are essential to success. Schools that try to implement without this support often fail.

    Example: Implementing Metacognitive Instruction Across a Subject Department

    A Mathematics department decides that students struggle because they don't monitor their own problem-solving. They select explicit metacognitive instruction using think-alouds and planning protocols as their practice.

    Explore phase identifies that active ingredients include: models of expert thinking (teacher think-aloud where the teacher verbalizes their reasoning), guided practice where students explain their thinking, and reflection prompts where students assess their own understanding.

    Prepare phase involves training on the research (EEF, 2025), viewing and discussing video models of think-alouds, and designing the planning protocol students will use. Teachers anticipate that think-alouds might feel awkward or that students might rush the reflection.

    Deliver phase involves department heads observing lessons weekly, providing feedback on whether think-alouds were genuinely explanatory (not just narrating steps), whether students had time to plan before solving, and whether reflection was genuine. When several teachers report that think-alouds felt forced, the team discusses and adjusts the approach. When students respond well to the planning protocol, that success is celebrated and shared.

    Sustain phase embeds the practice into curriculum materials (all problems include planning prompts), into new teacher induction, and into periodic check-ins (quarterly) to ensure the practice is still present and improving over time.
