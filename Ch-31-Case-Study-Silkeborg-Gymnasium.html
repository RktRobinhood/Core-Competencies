    Chapter 31 — Case Study: Silkeborg Gymnasium — What Happens When a School Takes Formation Seriously

    ◉ Thesis

    Philosophy without implementation is aspiration. The previous chapters established that dannelse is philosophically compelling, empirically grounded, and supported by Danish policy. But the hard question remains: can a real school — with real teachers, real budgets, and over a thousand real students — actually operationalise formation at scale? Silkeborg Gymnasium provides the best available answer, and the answer is both encouraging and honest. The school's Learning Laboratory demonstrates that sustainable pedagogical innovation requires formal structures, not individual heroism: professional learning communities where teachers research their own practice, observe each other, and share findings institutionally. Its Klimadannelse programme shows how dannelse extends to new domains without fragmenting. Its digital dannelse initiative models the only defensible approach to AI in schools — critical engagement rather than either panic or uncritical adoption. But Silkeborg also reveals what remains genuinely unresolved: student voice in defining competencies is limited, assessment of formation remains underdeveloped, and the gap between promising institutional process and measurable student outcomes has not been convincingly closed. The case is instructive precisely because it takes formation seriously enough to expose the tensions that less ambitious schools never encounter.

    A Mission Statement That Actually Means Something

    Silkeborg Gymnasium was founded in 1907 as Th. Langs Gymnasium and serves approximately 1,250 students in Central Jutland. As Denmark's largest gymnasium, it faces both the challenge and the opportunity of scale: How do you sustain coherent educational vision across dozens of teachers and hundreds of students?

    The school's stated values provide the answer: fællesskab og engagement (community and engagement), respekt og ansvar (respect and responsibility), and dialog og abenhed (dialogue and openness). The school's explicit mission is to develop personlig myndighed—personal authority or agency. In the school's language, students learn "to relate reflectively and responsibly to themselves and their surroundings—both local and global."

    This mission statement is not generic. It echoes the dannelse tradition explicitly. Personlig myndighed is the capacity to direct one's own life, to make responsible choices, and to engage with the world with agency and intention. It is what Klafki meant by selvbestemmelse (self-determination) (Klafki, 2001). And the school has chosen to organize its improvement work around developing this capacity in all students.

    ℹ Key Concept — Personlig Myndighed

    Personlig myndighed (personal authority/agency) refers to the capacity to understand oneself, make autonomous decisions, take responsibility for those decisions, and engage thoughtfully with the world. In Silkeborg's language, it is the core outcome of education. Developing personlig myndighed requires more than individual willpower; it requires structures, feedback, and opportunities to practice making decisions and reflecting on their consequences.

    Structure, Not Inspiration: The Learning Laboratory

    Silkeborg's most distinctive institutional structure is the Laeringslaboratoriet (Learning Laboratory)—a formal system for ongoing pedagogical improvement that functions as a practical embodiment of how a large school can sustain innovation without fragmenting.

    Teachers as Researchers in Their Own Classrooms

    The Learning Laboratory operates through Professional Learning Communities (PLF—Professionelle Laeringsfaellesskaber) (Silkeborg Gymnasium, 2025). Teachers form cross-disciplinary project groups that meet regularly, conduct lesson studies and classroom observations, collect student feedback, and test new practices. Each group tackles a specific pedagogical challenge with research and action-research methods.

    Recent PLF project groups at Silkeborg have included:

    • A group investigating how to develop students' Danish language competencies through critical AI use
    • A group exploring how to boost motivation in specific subject areas
    • A group examining how to integrate climate perspectives across the curriculum
    • A group working on assessment methods that support metacognition

    Each group follows a cycle: planning, implementation, supervision (in the form of colleagues observing and providing feedback), evaluation, and dissemination. The findings from each group are shared with the wider faculty through internal courses, faculty meetings, and written reports. This means that innovations do not stay in one classroom; they become part of the school's collective knowledge.

    This is sophisticated professional learning infrastructure. It is neither top-down mandate nor laissez-faire individual practice. It is structured inquiry where teachers are researchers in their own classrooms, working collaboratively to solve real pedagogical problems.

    » In Practice

    The PLF group on AI and Danish competencies operated roughly as follows: Teachers first examined the competency goals in Danish (reading, writing, speaking, listening, critical thinking). They hypothesized that generative AI could be used to develop certain competencies (e.g., revision and critical evaluation of texts) without replacing the human cognitive work that matters most. They designed lessons where students wrote first without AI, then critically evaluated AI-generated alternatives, then revised their own work. They observed each other's lessons, collected student feedback on the experience, and analyzed student work to see if the intervention actually developed the competencies they targeted. After iterating and refining, they shared their findings with all Danish teachers and made their lessons and rubrics available.

    Why Brilliant Teaching Practices Usually Disappear — and How PLF Prevents That

    The Learning Laboratory model directly addresses one of the key implementation challenges in competency education: how do you move from inspiring ideas to stable, evidence-informed classroom practices? Professional learning communities, embedded in the school's structure and supported by dedicated time and leadership, create the conditions for this translation to happen.

    Moreover, the PLF model makes competency development visible and collective, not hidden and individual. When one teacher develops a brilliant approach to assessing critical thinking, that practice does not disappear when the teacher retires or transfers. It is documented, shared, refined, and integrated into the school's repertoire.

    Klimadannelse: Formation Extends to New Domains Without Fragmenting

    Silkeborg Gymnasium runs a dedicated development project on Klimadannelse (climate formation or climate education). This initiative is significant because it demonstrates how dannelse philosophy extends to new domains and how a school can address contemporary challenges without fragmenting its core mission.

    The project is structured around three interconnected tracks:

    Curriculum integration embeds climate and sustainability perspectives across subjects. A History class might examine how climate shaped human migration and conflict. An Economics class might analyze carbon pricing. A Biology class might study ecosystem dynamics. A Literature class might read authors writing about environmental change. The integration is not superficial; it reflects genuine disciplinary inquiry into the role of climate and sustainability.

    School operations greens the physical environment: energy efficiency, waste reduction, sustainable food sourcing. Students see the school practicing what it teaches, and they often become involved in auditing and improving these systems.

    Student engagement connects climate action to democratic participation and student agency. Rather than positioning students as passive recipients of environmental education, the school creates opportunities for them to research problems, propose solutions, and advocate for change—both within the school and in the wider community.

    What makes Klimadannelse significant from a dannelse perspective is that it is not treated as a separate "green education" add-on. It is integrated into the larger question: What does it mean to be a well-formed, responsible person in an era of climate change? This extends Klafki's vision of allgemeine Bildung (general education) to explicitly include ecological literacy and environmental responsibility as dimensions of personhood that all students should develop (Klafki, 2001; Silkeborg Gymnasium, 2025).

    ? For Reflection

    How is climate education different when it is approached through a dannelse lens (as formation of responsible persons with respect for future generations) versus through a skills or knowledge lens (as learning about climate science and solutions)? What does each approach make possible and what does it leave out?

    Digital Dannelse: Neither Panic Nor Uncritical Adoption

    Silkeborg's strategic documents explicitly address digital dannelse (digital formation). In the school's language, "Digital dannelse as a concept connects general formation to a range of digital competencies and study competencies" (Silkeborg Gymnasium, 2024). This is exactly right: technology is not a separate domain or a threat to be managed; it is a dimension of what it means to be a well-formed person in a digital world.

    The school's approach to AI is instructive. Rather than banning AI or adopting it uncritically, Silkeborg has chosen to engage with it systematically and ethically:

    • All staff received AI competency training (Gymnasieskolen, 2025). Teachers are expected to understand generative AI, its capabilities and limitations, and how to use it as a tool in their teaching.
    • Pilot groups are exploring AI in subject-specific contexts. These are not generic "AI in education" experiments; they are disciplinary inquiries. How does AI support development of writing competencies in Danish? How can AI be used to personalize problem sets in Mathematics while maintaining cognitive challenge? These are the questions driving the work.
    • The guiding principle is that AI should be laeringsfremmende (learning-promoting). This means AI is used to deepen learning, to provide feedback, to free time for higher-order thinking—not to replace the human cognitive work that matters.

    This is digital dannelse in practice: engaging with technology critically, deliberately, and in service of human development rather than efficiency for its own sake.

    ⚠ Caveat and Nuance

    There is a difference between "using AI in education" and "digital dannelse." Using AI can mean implementing tools instrumentally to improve test scores. Digital dannelse means developing students' capacity to engage with AI systems ethically, critically, and with awareness of their own agency. It means asking not just "How can AI help me learn this?" but "What am I becoming as a person who outsources this thinking to AI? What do I want to outsource and what do I want to keep?"

    The Competency Map: What Silkeborg Expects Students to Become

    The school's explicit student outcomes reflect the competency frameworks discussed throughout this course. Silkeborg expects students to develop:

    • Faglige og sociale kompetencer (academic and social competencies)
    • Studiekompetence (study competence—the ability to function in higher education)
    • Personlig myndighed (personal authority/agency)
    • Kreative og innovative evner (creative and innovative abilities)
    • Digitale kompetencer (digital competencies)
    • Kritisk sans (critical sense)

    This list maps closely onto frameworks like the IB's ATL (IBO, 2015), the OECD's competency model (OECD, 2019), and 21st-century skills frameworks (Battelle for Kids, 2019). But notice the emphasis on personlig myndighed—a distinctly Danish concept that captures something not always emphasized in international frameworks: the formation of self-directed, responsible persons.

    The school's approach to developing these competencies is multifaceted. Some are embedded in subject teaching through the competency goal structure. Some are developed through the school's pastoral care structures. Some emerge through the PLF groups' deliberate pedagogy. And some are cultivated simply through the culture of dialogue, respect, and community that the school deliberately maintains.

    » In Practice

    Personal agency is not taught in a single class called "Personlig Myndighed." Instead, it is cultivated through multiple channels: Through genuine voice in school decisions (student representatives on school council, student feedback shaping PLF priorities). Through projects where students pursue questions that matter to them. Through feedback from teachers that emphasizes their capacity to improve and their responsibility for their learning. Through opportunities to make mistakes, reflect, and try again in low-stakes environments. Through relationships with teachers who model and discuss their own ongoing learning and growth.

    What Remains Unresolved — The Honest Questions

    Silkeborg provides valuable lessons, but examining the school also reveals important tensions and unresolved questions.

    How are students involved in shaping the competency agenda? The PLF groups are teacher-led. While student feedback is collected and considered, students are not positioned as co-designers of their own competency development. How much more powerful might the work be if students had genuine voice in defining what competencies matter and how they should be developed?

    How is competency development assessed at the student level? The case study documents innovations in pedagogy and school structure but is less clear about how student competency development is actually assessed. Are competencies being evaluated? If so, with what tools and with what validity? Or does assessment remain primarily content-focused, with competency development left largely unassessed?

    How does a very large school maintain coherence across many initiatives? Silkeborg is undertaking multiple significant initiatives: digital dannelse, climate education, competency development through PLF, study competence preparation for higher education. How are these initiatives coordinated? Is there a risk of fragmentation, where different teachers pursue different agendas without genuine integration?

    What is the impact beyond satisfaction surveys? The school tracks student well-being through surveys. But what evidence exists that the pedagogical innovations are actually changing learning outcomes or competency development? Without this evidence, initiatives risk being perpetually experimental without ever stabilizing into reliable practice.

    These are not criticisms but invitations to deeper thinking. Silkeborg is doing genuinely sophisticated work. Asking these questions is part of honoring that work seriously.

    Scale as Both Asset and Liability: Silkeborg and the EPX Transition

    As Denmark's largest gymnasium, Silkeborg is likely to be directly affected by the emerging epx (practice-oriented gymnasium) reform discussed in Ch-30-Danish-Education-Reforms. The school's existing experience is relevant in several ways.

    Its commitment to cross-disciplinary competency development prepares it well for epx, where competencies like collaboration and critical thinking must be visible and valued across academic and practice-oriented contexts. Its PLF structure provides a vehicle for the kind of ongoing pedagogical adaptation that an epx transition would require. Its experience with integrating new domains (digital dannelse, climate education) into existing curriculum suggests it can manage the curricular restructuring that epx would entail.

    Conversely, implementing epx would be a significant additional undertaking. It would require partnership development with businesses, assessment redesign, and potentially physical infrastructure changes. Silkeborg's scale—as the largest gymnasium—could be both an asset (economies of scale, more potential business partners) and a liability (complexity of managing large-scale change across many teachers and departments).

    Five Principles That Transfer Beyond Silkeborg

    What can a school designing a course on core competencies learn from Silkeborg's example?

    Embed competency work in structures. Do not rely on individual teacher initiative or ad hoc workshops. Create formal structures—PLF groups, regular collaborative planning time, documentation systems—that make competency development part of how the school operates.

    Extend dannelse to new domains. When new challenges emerge (AI, climate change, social media), frame them as dannelse questions, not technical questions. What kind of people do we want students to become in relation to these challenges?

    Balance tradition and innovation deliberately. Silkeborg explicitly holds together "tradition, history and deep engagement" with "reorientation, adaptability and focus on future challenges" (Silkeborg Gymnasium, 2025). This is not incoherence; it is wisdom. Educational institutions should honor what is enduring while remaining responsive to change.

    Make professional learning as intentional as student learning. The competencies you expect students to develop—collaboration, critical thinking, metacognition—should be developed by teachers too. Silkeborg's PLF groups are not just improving practice; they are developing teacher competencies in ways that parallel student learning.

    Measure what matters, but resist the temptation to reduce everything to measurement. Track outcomes on competencies, well-being, and engagement. But also hold space for dimensions of education—character, wisdom, reflection, community—that resist quantification.
