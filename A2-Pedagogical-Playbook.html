    Appendix A2 — TOK Pedagogical Playbook: Lesson Sequences and Routines

    ◉ Overview

    This appendix provides practical, tested lesson sequences and fast classroom protocols that TOK teachers can use immediately. The sequences are framed around knowledge questions and competency development, showing how abstract TOK concepts become concrete classroom experiences. All routines are designed to be flexible enough for different contexts and topics while maintaining the core learning architecture.

    Operating Principle

    AI on process (organizing, drafting, comparing), Human on purpose (judgment, ethics, interpretation, responsibility)

    Every routine in this playbook embeds this principle: AI can help organize thinking, generate alternatives, and prepare materials, but the human cognitive work—deciding what matters, making judgments, taking responsibility—remains with students.

    6-Lesson Mini-Sequence: From Knower to Knowledge Responsibility

    This sequence can be used as a standalone unit or integrated into longer TOK study. Each lesson is 50-60 minutes in a typical school timetable.

    Lesson 1: Framing the Knower

    Central Knowledge Question: How does AI change me as a knower?

    Learning objective: Students reflect on their own assumptions about knowing and how those assumptions shift in interaction with AI.

    Routine: Personal Assumption Audit

    1. Warm-up (5 min): Prompt: "When you 'know' something, what does that mean? What does it feel like?" Students write individually.
    2. Pair share (5 min): Share with a partner. Listen for what assumptions you both made.
    3. AI interaction (10 min): Ask students to pose a question to an AI system (ChatGPT, Claude, or similar) on a topic they think they know something about.
    4. Reflection (15 min): Students journal: "What did AI give me? Did it match what I thought? Did it change my thinking? Did I trust it? Why or why not?"
    5. Whole-class discussion (15 min): What assumptions about knowing became visible? How did AI challenge them?
    6. Exit ticket (5 min): "One question I have about how AI affects knowing..." Collect these as entry points for future lessons.

    Assessment: Collect journals. Look for evidence that students recognized their own assumptions and engaged genuinely with the question.

    Lesson 2: Evidence and Authority

    Central Knowledge Question: What counts as evidence when AI output is fluent but uncertain?

    Learning objective: Students develop practical strategies for evaluating AI claims and understanding the difference between confidence and accuracy.

    Routine: Claim Verification Ladder

    Students use a structured ladder to evaluate AI-generated claims:

    Level 1 — Unsupported: The AI says something with no evidence or source. ("The capital of Denmark is Copenhagen." No support provided.)

    Level 2 — Plausible: The claim sounds reasonable and might be true, but there's no hard evidence. ("AI could improve learning if used carefully." Sounds sensible but needs verification.)

    Level 3 — Corroborated: The claim is supported by multiple independent sources. ("The 2014 Folkeskole reform increased school days. Verified through three official government documents.")

    Level 4 — Contested: The claim is debated by credible sources with different interpretations or evidence. ("AI will increase unemployment. Some economists predict large-scale displacement; others argue new jobs will emerge.")

    Lesson structure:

    1. Present 4-5 AI-generated claims on a topic (history, science, policy—whatever you're studying)
    2. Students work in pairs to categorize each claim using the ladder
    3. For unsupported or plausible claims, students research and try to corroborate them
    4. Whole-class debrief: How did your categorization change as you researched? What surprised you?

    Assessment: Collect evidence maps showing how students categorized claims and what research they did. Look for critical thinking about source quality and the difference between AI confidence and truth.

    Lesson 3: AOK Comparison

    Central Knowledge Question: Do standards of justification differ across AOKs when AI is involved?

    Learning objective: Students recognize that different disciplines have different methods for evaluating knowledge claims, and these differences become more important in the age of AI.

    Routine: Same Claim, Two AOKs

    1. Select one claim (e.g., "Climate change is the result of human activity" or "Innovation drives social progress")
    2. Have students examine how Natural Sciences would evaluate this claim (what evidence? what experiments? what standards of proof?)
    3. Then examine how History would evaluate it (what historical sources? what interpretations? what contextual understanding?)
    4. Create a comparison table:
    Element Natural Sciences History
    Primary method Experimental; control variables Interpretation of documents and context
    Standard of evidence Reproducible; quantifiable Plausible narrative with supporting evidence
    Role of perspective Minimized through method Central; interpretation depends on perspective
    Use of AI Can analyze large datasets; but cannot replace experimental design Can organize sources and suggest connections; but cannot replace interpretation

    Assessment: Evaluate comparison tables for depth of understanding of AOK differences. Look for evidence that students see why different AOKs need different methods.

    Lesson 4: Ethics and Boundaries

    Central Knowledge Question: Should some knowledge not be sought or used through AI?

    Learning objective: Students engage with concrete ethical dilemmas about knowledge and technology, moving beyond abstract principle to nuanced judgment.

    Routine: Structured Ethical Dilemma

    Present a real or realistic scenario. Example: "An AI system can predict which students will drop out of school based on patterns in their data. A school could use this to intervene early. Should it? What are the harms and benefits? Who decides?"

    Structure the discussion:

    1. Stakeholder perspectives (10 min): Who is affected? What does each stakeholder want?
    2. Students at risk of dropping out
    3. Teachers and counselors
    4. School leaders
    5. Parents

    6. Society (does early intervention serve everyone's interests?)

    7. Harms and benefits (10 min): For each stakeholder, what are the harms and benefits?

    8. Values in tension (10 min): What values compete? (Privacy vs. help. Autonomy vs. intervention. Efficiency vs. human judgment.)

    9. Decision and consequences (10 min): If the school used this AI, what would happen? If it didn't, what would happen? What's the better choice and why?

    10. Reflection (10 min): Write a policy memo from the perspective of one stakeholder, arguing for a position.

    Assessment: Evaluate memos for evidence of perspective-taking, understanding of tradeoffs, and ability to reason with values.

    Lesson 5: Exhibition Workshop

    Central Knowledge Question: How does TOK manifest around us in AI-shaped contexts?

    Learning objective: Students develop genuine TOK exhibition objects grounded in their own inquiry.

    Routine: Object Triad Studio

    This is a 50-minute collaborative studio session where students generate, evaluate, and begin developing three exhibition objects.

    1. Brainstorm (15 min): In small groups, generate eight candidate objects in 8 minutes:
    2. Something related to AI or technology and knowledge
    3. Something from daily life that raises a TOK question
    4. Something that shows different perspectives
    5. Something about bias, ethics, or authority
    6. Something from a subject they study
    7. Something that contrasts with something else
    8. Something that shows how knowledge changes

    9. Something surprising or counterintuitive

    10. Filter (10 min): Evaluate each candidate using these criteria:

    11. Is it specific and real (not abstract or hypothetical)?
    12. Does it raise a clear knowledge question?

    13. Can a meaningful commentary be written about it?

    14. Develop a set (15 min): Select the three strongest candidates. Ensure they create contrast:

    15. Don't use three versions of the same object

    16. Ensure they raise different kinds of knowledge questions

    17. Draft justification (10 min): For each object, write one sentence: "This object matters for TOK because..."

    Assessment: Evaluate the three-object set for relevance, diversity, and clarity of knowledge connection. Provide feedback on potential commentaries.

    Lesson 6: Essay Transfer

    Central Knowledge Question: How do we construct a balanced TOK argument on AI and human competency?

    Learning objective: Students practice the scaffolded argument structure they will use for the external essay.

    Routine: Claim-Counterclaim-Implication Scaffold

    Introduce a framework for TOK essays:

    Claim: What is your position? State it clearly. ("Outsourcing thinking to AI weakens our capacity for independent reasoning.")

    Counterclaim: What's the strongest opposing view? ("AI can enhance reasoning by freeing us from routine tasks and providing different perspectives.")

    Evaluation: How do you assess the relative strength of the claim and counterclaim? ("Both are true in some contexts. The question is which effect dominates, and it depends on how AI is used and supported by other practices.")

    Lesson structure:

    1. Mini-lecture (10 min): Teach the scaffold using a worked example from a different domain (not AI, so students focus on structure, not content).

    2. Guided practice (15 min): Students work in pairs to develop claim, counterclaim, and evaluation for a specific KQ about AI and knowledge. You circulate, asking: "Is the counterclaim genuinely the strongest opposing view? Can you make it stronger? How do you evaluate the tension between them?"

    3. Timed paragraph writing (15 min): Each student writes a timed paragraph (one KQ, two AOKs, claim-counterclaim-evaluation structure). Tight time constraint forces focus on argumentation, not polish.

    4. Peer review (10 min): Exchange with a partner. Is the claim clear? Is the counterclaim genuinely opposing? Is the evaluation balanced or does it seem to favor one side unfairly?

    5. Reflection (5 min): "What made this argument stronger? What would you do differently next time?"

    Assessment: Evaluate paragraphs for argument structure, balance, and conceptual depth. Provide feedback on AOK comparative thinking.

    Fast Classroom Protocols (15 Minutes or Less)

    1. Human-First Then AI (15 minutes)

    1. Student answers KQ without AI (5 min): Working alone, students respond to a knowledge question or problem in writing.
    2. Student generates AI response (3 min): Using the same question, students query AI and copy the response.
    3. Student annotates comparison (5 min): Students mark what changed between their answer and AI's. What was better? What was weaker? What remains uncertain?
    4. Student records final judgment (2 min): Write a sentence stating what the student decided was the best thinking and why.

    Why it works: Students engage in genuine thinking first, then use AI as a tool for comparison and revision. They maintain ownership of their reasoning.

    2. Bias and Blind Spot Check (10 minutes)

    1. Identify potential model bias (2 min): "What would an AI system trained on [data source] likely emphasize or miss?"
    2. Identify personal confirmation bias (2 min): "What do I already believe about this topic? What would I tend to overlook?"
    3. Identify missing perspective (3 min): "What group or perspective is underrepresented in the conversation so far?"
    4. State corrective step (3 min): "What would I do to check my bias? Who could I listen to?"

    Why it works: Makes bias—both algorithmic and personal—visible and actionable.

    3. Implication Round (8 minutes)

    After discussing a claim or KQ, ask: "If this is true, what follows?"

    • For responsibility? ("If I accept that I can't fully understand AI's reasoning, what's my responsibility when using it?")
    • For fairness? ("If some people have access to AI assistance and others don't, what does that mean for equality?")
    • For trust? ("If I can't always tell whether content is human or AI-generated, how do I decide what to trust?")
    • For action? ("Given this knowledge question, what should we or I do differently?")

    Why it works: Implication questions pull thinking from abstract to concrete and personal. They show why knowledge questions matter.

    Assessment and Feedback Tips for TOK Teachers

    When assessing TOK work in this competency-developed framework:

    For exhibition prep: - Prioritize object-KQ fit over object novelty. A mundane object that raises a clear KQ is better than a spectacular object that doesn't. - Look for student voice and judgment in commentary, not just description of the object. - Assess whether the commentary addresses multiple perspectives and considers counterarguments.

    For essay prep: - Reward conceptual precision: Are concepts used correctly? Are knowledge terms (evidence, justification, bias) used with understanding? - Reward comparative depth: Are AOK differences actually explained and analyzed, or just listed? - Reward counterclaim quality: Is the opposing view represented fairly and strongly, or is it a strawman? - Reward implication depth: Does the essay explain what follows from the argument for knowledge, knowers, and action?

    Use feedback tags: - KQ precision: How clear is the knowledge question? Is it genuinely about knowledge? - AOK comparison: Are AOK differences explained? Are they meaningful? - Counterclaim quality: Is the opposing view the strongest possible version? - Implication depth: What follows from this argument? - Student voice: Can I hear the student's thinking, or only parroted ideas?

    Troubleshooting Common Challenges

    "My students struggle to separate opinions from knowledge claims."

    Teach them to ask: "On what grounds do I claim to know this? What evidence supports it? Who disagrees and why? What standard of justification applies?" These are metacognitive questions about knowledge itself.

    "They rush to use AI instead of thinking first."

    Build in the "human-first then AI" protocol. Make the human thinking visible and valued. Celebrate when students catch errors in AI output.

    "AOK comparisons feel forced."

    Start with just two AOKs at a time. Use the comparison table template consistently. Have students choose the AOKs rather than assigning them. Let comparison emerge naturally from discussion of how different disciplines approach the same question.

    "Exhibition objects feel disconnected from real knowledge questions."

    Help students practice the "This object matters for TOK because..." sentence until it's clear and knowledge-focused. An object that "shows the power of AI" is not enough. "This object shows how AI can generate plausible-sounding but false information, raising the question: what counts as evidence when language is fluent?" is better.
