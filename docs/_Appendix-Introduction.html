    Appendix — TOK-Competency Integration

    Why an Appendix on TOK?

    This course is designed for schools expanding the IB's Theory of Knowledge (TOK) from 100 to 150 hours over two years. TOK is not peripheral to competency development; it is central. TOK asks students to reflect critically on the nature of knowledge—how we know what we claim to know, what counts as evidence, whose perspective is included or excluded, what ethical limits apply to knowledge.

    All of these questions require the core competencies developed throughout this course: critical thinking, metacognition, perspective-taking, communication, information literacy, and ethical reasoning. Conversely, competency development without the reflective depth that TOK demands risks becoming merely instrumental—teaching students to think critically so they can succeed academically, rather than developing the capacity for genuine inquiry and responsibility.

    The Appendix bridges this gap. It shows TOK teachers and curriculum coordinators how to integrate competency development with TOK in ways that strengthen both.

    What the Appendix Contains

    A1 — Curriculum Hook Map maps the direct connections between TOK assessment objectives and core competencies. It provides a table showing which competencies support each assessment objective, and offers ready-to-use knowledge question stems that anchor TOK discussions in competency development.

    A2 — Pedagogical Playbook provides six complete lesson sequences (one for each major component of TOK learning: the knower, evidence, AOK comparison, ethics, exhibition, essay) and fast classroom protocols (5-15 minutes each) that teachers can use immediately. These sequences explicitly integrate competency work with TOK inquiry.

    A3 — Essay Angles offers four reusable argument frames that students can adapt to different prescribed essay titles. These frames are grounded in questions about how knowledge is produced, justified, and limited, and explicitly require AOK comparison and consideration of implications.

    A4 — Exhibition Object Ideas provides a curated bank of object ideas organized by common TOK prescribed prompts. These are brainstorming resources, not model submissions. Students use them to understand what kinds of objects work and why, then develop their own authentic objects.

    The Central Argument

    TOK and competency development are not separate initiatives. TOK is where competencies become conscious and deliberate. When students reflect on how they know something, defend their reasoning, consider alternative perspectives, and evaluate ethical implications, they are developing competencies. When students develop critical thinking, metacognition, and perspective-taking, they are becoming the kind of reflective thinker TOK demands.

    The problem is that TOK is sometimes taught as content about knowledge (epistemology, justification, truth) rather than as a practice of knowing. When taught as content, students memorize definitions of knowledge, learn about different AOKs, and write essays about abstract ideas. This is knowledge about knowledge, not genuine knowledge inquiry.

    The Appendix is designed to support the alternative: teaching TOK as a reflective practice where students develop competencies of knowing. They learn what it means to think critically about claims (competency: critical thinking). They learn to monitor their own reasoning and assumptions (competency: metacognition). They learn to understand why different disciplines approach problems differently (competency: perspective-taking, systems thinking). They learn to communicate their thinking clearly and defend it against challenge (competency: communication, critical thinking).

    How Schools Should Use the Appendix

    TOK teachers: Use the Appendix as a practical guide to daily teaching. Start with A2 (Pedagogical Playbook) and pick lesson sequences that match what you're currently teaching. Use the fast protocols to enliven discussions and protect time for thinking. Use A1 and A3 to show students the connections between what they're learning and TOK assessment.

    TOK coordinators and curriculum leaders: Use the Appendix as a framework for professional development. Show teachers how competency development supports TOK. Help them see that they are not being asked to add something external to TOK; they are being asked to teach TOK in ways that explicitly develop competencies.

    Curriculum architects: Use the Appendix to show how TOK fits into a whole-school competency development effort. If other subjects are embedding competencies explicitly, TOK can do the same—and TOK becomes the natural venue for students to reflect on and integrate the competencies they're developing across the curriculum.

    IB programme coordinators: Use the Appendix to demonstrate coherence between the 150-hour TOK expansion and the rest of the curriculum. Show how competency work in other subjects supports TOK learning, and how TOK serves as a reflective capstone for competency development.

    Key Principles in the Appendix

    Competencies are always in service of knowledge inquiry. TOK is not about developing communication skills or critical thinking skills in isolation. It's about developing the kinds of thinking needed to understand how knowledge works. Skills are instrumental to inquiry, not the other way around.

    Knowledge questions are always concrete. Rather than abstract KQs like "What is knowledge?", use specific KQs: "How does AI change what counts as evidence?" These are more engaging and more productive for both competency development and TOK learning.

    Student voice and authentic inquiry matter. The strongest TOK work comes from students asking questions that matter to them. The Appendix provides structures and prompts, but always directs students toward their own authentic inquiry.

    Different sources of authority should be compared, not prioritized. In A1, A3, and A4, we ask students to compare expert authority with algorithm authority, historical interpretation with scientific proof, etc. The goal is not to declare one superior, but to understand why different domains use different standards.

    Competency development is visible and assessed. Use the progression rubrics and assessment tips in the Appendix to make competency development visible in TOK work. When students see that critical thinking and perspective-taking are being assessed alongside TOK content, they develop those competencies deliberately.

    The Role of AI in the Appendix

    Throughout the Appendix, AI appears as a concrete, contemporary context for TOK inquiry. This is intentional for several reasons:

    1. AI is genuinely a knowledge question. When AI can generate fluent text, create images, and answer questions confidently, questions about knowledge (what is it? how do we justify it? whose knowledge is included?) become urgent and unavoidable.

    2. Students care about AI. They see it in their lives. Using AI as a context makes TOK feel relevant, not academic.

    3. AI integrates with competency development. Teaching students to evaluate AI claims critically, understand their own assumptions about technology, and think about ethical implications develops the exact competencies this course emphasizes.

    4. The Danish context supports this. The concept of digital dannelse (digital formation) positions engagement with AI as a formation question, not just a technical question. This aligns the Appendix with the Danish philosophical grounding in Part V.

    Connections to the Whole Course

    The Appendix depends on and integrates with the entire course:

    • Parts I-II (Foundations) provided cognitive science research that explains why competencies matter
    • Parts III-IV (Core Competencies) mapped out what competencies are and how they develop
    • Part V (Danish Context) showed the philosophical and policy grounding for this work
    • Part VI (Implementation) explained how to move from ideas to practice
    • Appendix (TOK) shows how TOK becomes a natural venue for students to integrate and reflect on competencies

    TOK is not an add-on. It is the capstone: the place where students reflect on what they're learning to do and who they're becoming through that learning.

    Next Steps

    After reading the Appendix introduction:

    1. If you are a TOK teacher: Read A2 (Pedagogical Playbook) and try one lesson sequence with your class.
    2. If you are a curriculum leader: Read A1 (Curriculum Hook Map) and use it to show colleagues how competency work serves TOK.
    3. If you are implementing the full 150-hour course: Use the Appendix as the TOK component of your implementation plan. Refer to it in teacher training and lesson planning.
    4. If you are integrating AI into curriculum: Use A1, A2, A3, and A4 as templates for how to integrate AI as both a practical tool and a knowledge question.
