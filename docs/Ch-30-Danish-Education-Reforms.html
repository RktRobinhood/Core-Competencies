    Chapter 30 — Danish Education Reforms: Folkeskole to Gymnasium

    ◉ Overview

    Two major reforms define contemporary Danish education: the 2014 Folkeskole reform, which restructured primary and lower-secondary education around competency goals and well-being, and the emerging Forberedt pa fremtiden V gymnasium reform, which proposes a new epx (practice-oriented gymnasium) track that bridges academic and vocational pathways. Both reforms are rooted in dannelse philosophy and provide institutional support for competency development. Understanding these reforms is essential context for designing a course on core competencies in a Danish school.

    The 2014 Folkeskole Reform: Three Overarching Goals

    The most significant restructuring of Danish primary and lower-secondary education in a generation arrived in 2014. The reform set three explicit objectives that shaped every subsequent policy decision and remain the benchmark for evaluating Danish schools today.

    Goal 1: All students must be challenged to reach their full potential. This meant raising the academic bar for all students, including both high-performing learners who were sometimes under-challenged and struggling students who were sometimes written off. The reform rejected both tracking and mere inclusion; it demanded that all students experience appropriate cognitive challenge.

    Goal 2: The significance of social background for academic results must be reduced. Educational equity was named explicitly. The reform recognized that family income, parents' education, and home language were predicting academic outcomes too strongly. The school's job was to interrupt this predictability and create genuine opportunity for all.

    Goal 3: Trust in schools and student well-being must be enhanced. Rather than imposing more testing and accountability measures, the reform invested in professional expertise and explicitly elevated student well-being (trivsel) to equal status with academic achievement. The reform assumed that teachers, given time and resources, could make good decisions; students, when they felt belonging and had voice, would engage more deeply.

    These three goals are not the language of narrow accountability. They reflect the dannelse tradition: the school's purpose is to develop each person fully, to reduce inequity, and to create conditions where young people can flourish.

    ℹ Key Concept — Trivsel in Danish Education

    Trivsel is often translated as "well-being," but it carries specific meaning in Danish schools. It encompasses both social belonging (feeling safe, included, and respected) and individual flourishing (experiencing voice, agency, and recognition of one's strengths). The 2014 reform elevated trivsel from a nice-to-have to a core educational goal, requiring annual well-being surveys and deliberate school policies to support it. This integration of well-being and academic learning is a direct expression of dannelse philosophy.

    Key Structural Changes in the Folkeskole Reform

    Extended School Day and Physical Activity

    The Folkeskole reform extended the school day and mandated a minimum of 45 minutes of daily physical activity. This was not merely adding content; it reflected a belief in embodied learning and the integration of physical development with intellectual growth. The extended day also created time for understøttende undervisning (supported study)—targeted interventions for students needing additional support—rather than expecting all learning to fit into a standard class period.

    Competency Goals Replace Knowledge Lists

    The Fælles Mål (Common Objectives) system was simplified and reoriented. Instead of approximately 3,000 separate knowledge and skill objectives, schools now work with a smaller number of competency goals that ask what students should be able to do. This shift aligns with international competency frameworks like the IB's ATL and 21st-century skills models, but it was also deeply rooted in Danish educational philosophy: learning is not the accumulation of facts, but the development of capability.

    The competency goal structure allows teachers more autonomy in how they teach while maintaining clarity on what students should achieve. A goal might read: "Students can identify and analyze a problem, propose solutions, and evaluate the effectiveness of those solutions." How teachers structure that learning—through project-based work, case studies, simulations, or traditional instruction—is a professional decision. The competency is the target; the pedagogy is flexible.

    Well-Being as Institutional Priority

    Annual well-being surveys became mandatory, and schools were required to use the data to inform policy decisions. Student voice—through well-being surveys, focus groups, and representation on school councils—became institutionalized, not optional. This is significant: it made student agency a systemic feature, not dependent on individual teacher initiatives.

    Professional Collaboration and Co-Teaching

    Teachers were expected to collaborate more intensively, including through co-teaching and cross-disciplinary planning. Pedagogues (pædagoger)—who have professional training distinct from teachers—were given expanded roles in the school day. This reflected a belief that good teaching is not an individual act but a collective practice.

    » In Practice

    In a school implementing the 2014 reform well, a Mathematics teacher and Danish teacher co-plan a unit on communication and argument. Students learn to construct mathematical arguments in Mathematics class and rhetorical arguments in Danish class. The competency—reasoning and communication—is developed in both subjects, reinforced through deliberate connection. Teachers meet regularly to discuss student progress on this cross-disciplinary competency, not just in their individual subjects.

    Tensions and Criticisms

    Like any significant reform, the 2014 Folkeskole reform has faced challenges in implementation and philosophical critique.

    Workload concerns emerged quickly. The extended school day and new requirements increased teacher workload at precisely the moment when resources were being constrained. The 2013 lockout between teachers' unions and the government reflected these tensions. Many teachers report that the reform added expectations without adding sufficient time or support.

    Implementation gaps are documented in research. Rasmussen et al. (2015) found uneven implementation across Danish municipalities and schools. Some schools fully embraced the competency goal approach and restructured pedagogy accordingly. Others continued teaching much as before, simply relabeling activities as competency-focused. The reform itself was sound, but its uptake was inconsistent.

    The simplified goals debate continues among some educators. The reduction from 3,000 to a smaller number of competency goals was intended to increase clarity and teacher autonomy. Some educators argued it resulted in loss of specificity and depth, especially in subjects like Science and History where rich content knowledge is essential.

    Tensions between well-being and achievement have not been fully resolved. Defining what schools should prioritize when well-being and academic outcomes pull in different directions remains contested. Some see the emphasis on trivsel as diluting academic rigor; others see attempts to maintain traditional academic pressure as harmful to student mental health.

    ? For Reflection

    How can a school system maintain both high academic standards and genuine well-being? Are these in tension, or can they be integrated? What does this question reveal about our assumptions about learning and human development?

    The Gymnasium Reform: Forberedt Pa Fremtiden V and the EPX Model

    While the Folkeskole reform addressed ages 6–16, a new initiative is reshaping upper-secondary education (ages 16+). The Danish government's Forberedt pa fremtiden V: Flere i gymnasiet ("Prepared for the Future V: More in the Gymnasium") proposes the most significant structural change to Danish gymnasium education in decades.

    The Problem the Reform Addresses

    The current Danish system forces young people at age 15–16 to choose between vocational education (erhvervsuddannelser) and academic gymnasium (stx or hhx). This is a false choice. Young people signal through their actual behavior that they reject a sharp theory-practice divide: enrollment in hybrid programmes (hhx, htx, hf, eux) that combine practical and academic elements has been growing, while traditional stx and pure vocational pathways have declined.

    The reform's diagnosis is simple: students are already telling us they don't want a hard boundary between theory and practice. The epx model follows their lead and makes policy to match behavior.

    The EPX Model: Bridging Theory and Practice

    The erhvervs- og professionsrettet eksamen (epx)—practice-oriented gymnasium examination—is a new qualification that combines three elements that the traditional system kept separate.

    Academic study competence (studiekompetence) provides the general academic foundation. Students study Mathematics, Danish, English, and other subjects at gymnasium level, maintaining rigor and breadth.

    Practice-oriented learning embeds work with businesses, project-based learning, and practical application. A student studying Mathematics might solve real optimization problems for a local company. A student studying Psychology might engage with hospital professionals. A student studying Marketing might develop campaigns for actual businesses.

    Line-based specialization allows students to choose a linje (track) based on interest and career direction: Welfare and Security, Crafts and Technology, Business and Administration, and others. This gives coherence and meaning to the practical component; it is not scattered, but organized around a genuine pathway.

    The genius of the model is that it dissolves a false binary. There is no contradiction between theory and practice; they are the same learning, instantiated in different contexts. Critical thinking applied to a workshop problem is the same competency as critical thinking applied to a historical text. Collaboration on a business project is the same competency as collaboration in an academic project. The competencies are universal; the contexts are diverse.

    ⚠ Important Note

    The epx reform was announced as part of the government's Forberedt pa fremtiden programme and requires significant infrastructure investment. Implementation involves consultation with regions, municipalities, and existing institutions. As of the date of this writing, detailed curriculum and assessment frameworks are still under development. Schools should monitor the official implementation timeline.

    Implications for Competency Education

    The epx reform has several profound implications for how core competencies are conceptualized and taught.

    Theory-practice integration becomes natural, not forced. Schools no longer need to argue that practical work is "valuable too." It is the site of academic learning. This removes the status hierarchy that traditionally placed theory above practice and allows both to be equally rigorous.

    Competencies become the bridge between domains. A student on the Welfare and Security line and one on the Crafts and Technology line need the same core competencies: communication, collaboration, critical thinking, self-regulation, ethical reasoning. The competencies are constant; the content and contexts vary. This makes it clear that competencies are not optional add-ons; they are the infrastructure of all learning.

    Dannelse is extended, not abandoned. The reform explicitly frames gymnasium education within the dannelse tradition. If dannelse includes preparation for democratic citizenship and personal formation, then engagement with the practical world of work, community, and professions is itself a form of dannelse. Students are not just preparing for jobs; they are forming themselves as capable, responsible citizens and professionals.

    Assessment becomes more authentic. The commitment to practice-oriented assessment—exams that reflect project-based, applied learning—creates an opportunity to embed competency assessment authentically. Instead of artificial "collaboration rubrics" applied to a group project, schools can assess how students actually collaborate in real work contexts, with real stakes and real feedback from partners.

    » In Practice

    Under the epx model, a student in the Business and Administration line might undertake a project to improve a local company's customer service process. This involves research (gathering data, interviewing customers and staff), analysis (identifying problems and bottlenecks), creativity (proposing solutions), collaboration (working with company staff and classmates), and communication (presenting findings and recommendations). Each of these is a core competency. The assessment happens partly through traditional exams, but also through the quality of the actual project and the student's ability to reflect on and justify their work. The learning is not simulated; it is real.

    Connection to Course Design

    For a school designing a course on core competencies in the context of Danish education, these reforms provide both policy support and practical direction.

    Policy tailwind: The government has invested politically and financially in competency-oriented, well-being-focused education. A course aligned with the Folkeskole competency goals and the dannelse tradition has institutional support. Teachers can point to official policy when designing competency-focused instruction.

    Institutional coherence: When core competencies are connected to the Fælles Mål and the dannelse framework, they are not seen as an optional enrichment. They become part of what students are expected to develop in every subject.

    Integration with practice: For schools moving toward or preparing for the epx model, a course on core competencies provides the conceptual and pedagogical foundation. Teachers learn how to teach and assess competencies in both academic and practice-oriented contexts.

    Equity alignment: Both reforms explicitly aim to reduce educational inequity. A competency-focused course that addresses character strengths, supports metacognition, and develops agency supports this goal by making learning less dependent on home background and more dependent on the school's deliberate development of capability.

    The Tension: Holding Multiple Goals

    The Danish education reforms, rooted in dannelse, ask schools to hold multiple goals simultaneously: academic rigor and well-being, content mastery and competency development, support for all students and challenge for advanced learners, tradition and innovation. This is genuinely difficult work. It resists the comfort of single-measure accountability or narrow optimization.

    But this is exactly what the dannelse tradition demands: the formation of whole persons, not the production of test scores. The reforms, however imperfectly implemented, represent a national commitment to that vision. A course on core competencies, designed in this context, should honor that commitment by teaching competencies not as isolated skills but as dimensions of becoming a capable, responsible, thoughtful person.
