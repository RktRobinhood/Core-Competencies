    Chapter 24 — Effective Feedback for Learning

    ◉ Overview

    Feedback has an average effect size of d = 0.73 (Hattie & Timperley, 2007)—among the highest of any teaching intervention. However, this average masks enormous variation: some feedback powerfully accelerates learning while other feedback has little or no effect, and some feedback actually undermines learning. Understanding this variation is critical for translating feedback research into practice. John Hattie and Helen Timperley's model of feedback levels and Dylan Wiliam's framework of formative assessment strategies provide practical structures for giving feedback that moves learners forward.

    The Feedback Problem

    In most classrooms, feedback exists but is inconsistent in quality, timing, and impact. A student receives a returned essay with a grade (B-) and vague comments ("Good work, but needs more detail"). The feedback arrives a week after submission; by then, the student has mentally moved on. The student pays attention only to the grade, not the comments. No clear next step is specified. The student does not act on the feedback before the next assignment. This is typical feedback, but it is not effective feedback.

    Effective feedback requires several conditions: the learner must understand the learning goal, the feedback must address the work not the person, the feedback must be specific and actionable, the feedback must arrive in time for the learner to act on it, and the learner must have time to respond to the feedback before the next assessment. These conditions are surprisingly rare.

    ℹ Key Concept — The Feedback Gap

    The feedback gap is the difference between the learner's current work and the learning goal. Effective feedback narrows this gap by helping the learner understand what is wrong and what could be done better. However, feedback cannot do the work for the learner. If feedback is so specific that it is a solution (e.g., "Change this sentence to…" or "Your answer should be 2.5 because…"), it does the thinking for the student. The sweet spot is feedback specific enough to guide action but open enough to require the learner to think and revise.

    Hattie and Timperley's Model: Four Levels of Feedback

    Hattie and Timperley (2007) propose that feedback should answer three questions:

    Where am I going? (Feed-up) — the learning goal and success criteria.

    How am I going? (Feed-back) — evidence of progress toward the goal.

    Where to next? (Feed-forward) — what to do to make better progress.

    Without clarity on the first question, feedback is noise; the learner does not know what the feedback is about or why it matters. Without the second, the learner cannot assess whether they are on track. Without the third, the learner cannot act.

    Hattie and Timperley also identify four levels at which feedback can be aimed, with dramatically different effects:

    Task-level feedback addresses the correctness of the work. Example: "This answer is incorrect because 2+2=4, not 5." This feedback is most appropriate for procedural errors and simple factual mistakes. It is effective for straightforward tasks but limited for complex learning; the learner knows what is wrong but may not develop understanding of why.

    Process-level feedback addresses the strategies and processes used. Example: "Try breaking this problem into smaller parts first" or "Check your work by solving it a different way." Process feedback promotes deeper understanding and transfer because it targets the thinking moves, not just the answer. It is more effective than task feedback for complex learning.

    Self-regulation feedback addresses the learner's own monitoring and correction. Example: "What strategy could you use to check your answer?" or "How do you know that is right?" This level is the most powerful for long-term learning because it develops the metacognitive processes that support independent learning. It prompts the learner to monitor their own work and devise corrections, not relying on external feedback.

    Self-level feedback addresses the person rather than the work. Example: "You're so clever" or "You're not a maths person." This level is least effective and often counterproductive. Person-level praise (especially praise of ability) can undermine learning by shifting attention from the task to the ego. When success is attributed to fixed ability ("You're a natural"), failure feels catastrophic. This feedback level should be used rarely, if at all, and only in very careful forms (e.g., "You worked hard on this").

    The implications are clear: feedback should be aimed at task, process, and self-regulation levels; self-level feedback should be minimal. Moreover, feedback is most powerful when it is a dialogue, not a pronouncement. "What strategy could you use?" invites the learner into problem-solving; "Use this strategy" tells them.

    Dylan Wiliam's Five Formative Assessment Strategies

    Dylan Wiliam (2011) embeds feedback within a broader framework of formative assessment—assessment aimed at informing instruction and the learner, not just measuring final outcomes. Wiliam's five strategies operationalise the feedback process:

    Clarifying learning intentions and success criteria (with learners) — The teacher does not simply post the learning objective ("Students will understand the water cycle"); rather, the teacher co-creates criteria with students. What does understanding the water cycle look like? What evidence would show that you understand? This clarity is foundational; feedback without clear goals is purposeless. Moreover, co-creating criteria engages student agency and helps them internalise standards.

    Eliciting evidence of learning — The teacher uses questions, tasks, discussions, and observations to gather evidence about what students can and cannot do. This is not incidental; evidence elicitation is deliberate and systematic. Do all students understand, or just the vocal ones? What misconceptions are evident? What partial understandings are present? Without good evidence, feedback is based on assumption.

    Providing feedback that moves learners forward — This is the heart of formative assessment. Feedback should be specific, timely, actionable, and aimed at process or self-regulation. It should close the gap between current work and the goal in a way that the learner can act on. It should be dialogue, when possible, inviting the learner to think rather than passively receive judgement.

    Activating learners as instructional resources for one another — Peer feedback is powerful when structured. Peers can identify strengths in each other's work, ask clarifying questions, and provide suggestions. The key is training: students need explicit instruction in giving and receiving feedback. They must learn to focus on the work, not the person, and to offer specific, actionable comments. Unstructured peer review often devolves into vague praise or destructive criticism; structured protocols prevent this.

    Activating learners as owners of their own learning — Self-assessment is the other critical form of feedback. When learners compare their work against success criteria and identify gaps, they take ownership of learning. Self-assessment is not self-grading (scoring yourself on a scale); it is genuine self-evaluation using agreed criteria. "What did you do well? What could be better? What will you do next time?" Self-assessment develops metacognition and intrinsic motivation.

    Timing, Frequency, and Logistics

    The timing of feedback interacts with task complexity. For procedural tasks (e.g., "solve these equations"), immediate feedback is most effective because misconceptions can be corrected before they calcify. For complex tasks requiring struggle (e.g., "solve this multi-step word problem"), delayed feedback can sometimes be more effective because it allows productive struggle and prevents premature reliance on external correction. However, "delayed" does not mean "weeks later"; feedback within a day or two, while the task is still fresh, is better than feedback weeks later.

    Frequency is critical. A single piece of feedback on a single assignment is insufficient. Learners need multiple feedback cycles, with opportunities to act on feedback before the next assessment. A practical rhythm might be: feedback on daily low-stakes quizzes or exit tickets (same day), feedback on problem sets within a day or two with opportunity to revise, feedback on more substantial work within a week.

    Who gives feedback matters less than the quality of feedback itself. Teacher feedback, peer feedback, and self-assessment all have roles. Teacher feedback carries authority and can address conceptual errors that peers might miss. Peer feedback develops the giver's understanding (explaining to a peer deepens knowledge) and diversifies the feedback the receiver gets. Self-assessment develops ownership and metacognition. An effective system uses all three.

    Common Feedback Pitfalls

    Grades kill comments. Research by Butler (1988) found that when students received grades alongside written comments, the comments had essentially no effect on learning—students looked at the grade and ignored the feedback. Comments without grades led to the best learning. The implication is that formative feedback should be separated from grades. Formative feedback helps learning; grades measure (and often prematurely judge) learning.

    Too much feedback is counterproductive. Overloading students with corrections is overwhelming. Focus on the most impactful 1–2 points. If a student has five errors, choosing which two will most powerfully improve the next attempt is more helpful than listing all five.

    Delayed return of work breaks the feedback loop. If an assignment is submitted on Friday and feedback arrives the following Thursday, the feedback is effectively useless. The student has mentally moved on. Feedback should return quickly—ideally within a day or two, certainly within a week.

    Ego-involving feedback undermines learning. Feedback that compares students to each other, that uses public ranking, or that offers person-level praise or criticism shifts attention from the task to the self. Students become focused on protecting their image rather than learning. Feedback should focus on the work, not the worker.

    Assuming student understanding of feedback. A comment like "Needs more evidence" assumes the student knows what "evidence" means in this context, knows how to find it, and knows how to incorporate it. Often, these are false assumptions. Effective feedback includes a brief explanation of the goal and sometimes a hint about process.

    Peer Feedback and Self-Assessment in Practice

    Structured peer feedback requires training and protocols. One simple structure is "warm and cool" feedback: warm feedback identifies what worked well ("Your opening paragraph really grabbed my attention because..."), and cool feedback identifies what could be stronger ("The section on... seemed to need more evidence because..."). Students are trained to give specific, evidence-based feedback on the work, not personal praise or criticism.

    Self-assessment using rubrics is more powerful than self-grading. Rather than asking "What grade does this deserve?" (a judgment students struggle with), ask "Using the rubric, where is your strongest section and why? Which criterion is most challenging for you?" This deepens understanding and identifies learning edges.

    Revision protocols connect feedback to action. After receiving feedback, students should have class time to read it, ask clarifying questions if needed, and begin revision. An exit ticket ("Which piece of feedback will you act on first? Why?") ensures students leave with intention.

    Integration with Building Thinking Classrooms and Rosenshine's Principles

    Effective feedback is embedded throughout the systems discussed in previous chapters. In Building Thinking Classrooms, Liljedahl's practices for sustaining thinking (particularly Practice 5: answering only keep-thinking questions) and consolidating thinking (Practice 13: formative assessment for learning) are fundamentally feedback structures. When a student asks "Is this right?" and the teacher responds with a question that prompts checking, that is feedback—process-level feedback that develops self-regulation.

    In Rosenshine's framework, frequent checking for understanding is built in. The feedback given during these checks—"You got the concept but missed a computational step" (process level) rather than "Wrong" (task level)—is what makes the checking powerful.

    In Teach Like a Champion techniques, cold-calling and re-voicing serve as formative feedback. When a teacher uses cold-call to surface understanding, and then asks a follow-up question or re-voices the student's answer more precisely, that is process-level feedback.

    The convergence is clear: a teaching system that produces learning is one where feedback is frequent, specific, aimed at process or self-regulation, tied to clear goals, and coupled with time for learners to act on it.

    » In Practice

    After a formative quiz on fraction concepts, a teacher identifies misconceptions. Two students misunderstood how to add fractions with different denominators. Rather than re-teaching the whole class, the teacher provides targeted feedback: "You understand why we need a common denominator, but how could you check that your equivalent fractions are correct?" The student tries a strategy. The teacher affirms and moves on. Later in the lesson, both students complete three guided-practice problems with the teacher checking. The next day, homework includes one item similar to the misconception item, with the opportunity to revise if needed. This sequence—misconception identified, process-level feedback, guided practice, review—is how formative assessment actually works.

    Connections

    Feedback is the mechanism by which Building Thinking Classrooms develops competencies, by which Rosenshine's guided practice actually guides learning, and by which Visible Thinking routines develop metacognition. It is also fundamental to developing effective writing and project-based learning, where revision cycles are central. The next chapters show how feedback is operationalised in specific domains.
