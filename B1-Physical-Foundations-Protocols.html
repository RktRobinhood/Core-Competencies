    Appendix B1 — Physical Foundations Protocols: Sleep, Nutrition, and Movement

    ◉ Purpose

    This appendix provides practical, actionable protocols for the three physical foundations discussed in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit. These protocols are separated from the chapter narrative so they can be cross-referenced from any section of the textbook that touches on cognitive performance, self-regulation, or student wellbeing — without duplicating content.

    The protocols below are grounded in the research reviewed in Chapter 20. Each protocol includes the research rationale (briefly), the actionable steps, and a reflection prompt suitable for classroom use. Teachers should adapt these to their context — the principles are universal, but the specifics will vary by age group, culture, and school setting.

    Protocol 1: Sleep Hygiene for Learners

    Research rationale: Sleep loss impairs attention, working memory, and executive function (Lim & Dinges, 2010). Memory consolidation — the process by which the day's learning transfers from short-term to long-term storage — occurs during slow-wave sleep (Walker, 2017). A student who studies effectively but sleeps poorly undermines the very process that makes studying worthwhile. Adolescents require 8–10 hours of sleep; most get significantly less (Lo et al., 2016).

    The Sleep Protocol

    Environment setup (do once, benefit every night):

    • Remove screens from the bedroom, or establish a charging station outside the room where all devices go at a set time each evening. The goal is to make the bedroom a sleep space, not a screen space.
    • Make the room as dark as possible. Blackout curtains or a sleep mask help. Even small amounts of light from standby LEDs or streetlights can suppress melatonin production.
    • Keep the room cool. Research suggests 16–19°C (60–67°F) is optimal for sleep quality.
    • If noise is an issue, consider earplugs or a white noise source.

    Daily routine (the "wind-down system"):

    • Set a consistent bedtime and wake time — including weekends. The body's circadian rhythm depends on regularity. Sleeping in on weekends creates "social jet lag" that makes Monday mornings harder, not easier.
    • Begin a screen-free wind-down 30–60 minutes before bed. Screens emit blue-spectrum light that suppresses melatonin, but more importantly, the content on screens (social media, messaging, video) is stimulating and keeps the brain in an alert state.
    • During the wind-down, do something low-stimulation: read a print book, write in a journal, prepare for tomorrow, stretch, or talk with family.
    • Avoid caffeine after midday. Caffeine has a half-life of 5–6 hours — a coffee at 3pm means half the caffeine is still in the bloodstream at 9pm.

    What to do when you cannot sleep:

    • If you are not asleep within 20 minutes, get up and do something quiet in dim light (not on a screen). Return to bed when you feel sleepy. Lying in bed frustrated teaches the brain to associate the bed with wakefulness.
    • Do not check the time. Clock-watching increases anxiety about not sleeping, which makes it harder to sleep.
    ◆ For Teachers

    The sleep protocol is not a health class handout — it is a learning intervention. When students understand that their sleep directly affects their capacity for the metacognitive work described in Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation and the retrieval practice described in Ch-13-Retrieval-Practice-and-Spaced-Learning, the conversation shifts from "you should sleep more" to "sleep is part of your learning strategy."

    Reflection Prompt

    Track your sleep for one week (bedtime, wake time, and a 1–5 energy rating at midday). At the end of the week, answer: What patterns do you notice? On which days did you feel sharpest? What was different about those nights? What is one change you could make to your sleep environment or routine that would be easy to sustain?

    Protocol 2: Nutrition for Cognitive Performance

    Research rationale: The brain consumes approximately 20% of the body's energy despite being roughly 2% of body mass. Dietary quality is consistently associated with academic performance in adolescents (Burrows et al., 2017). Mediterranean-style dietary patterns are associated with improved working memory (Vassiloudis et al., 2022). Diets high in processed foods and refined sugars are associated with poorer cognitive outcomes.

    The Nutrition Protocol

    The basics (not a diet plan — a cognitive fuel strategy):

    • Eat breakfast. Students who skip breakfast arrive at school with depleted glucose — the brain's primary fuel. Even a simple breakfast (wholegrain toast, yoghurt, fruit) makes a measurable difference to morning attention and recall.
    • Stay hydrated. Even mild dehydration (1–2% body water loss) impairs attention and working memory. Keep a water bottle accessible throughout the school day.
    • Favour whole foods over processed foods. The general principle is simple: food that looks like it came from a plant or an animal provides more sustained energy than food that was manufactured in a factory. This is not about perfection — it is about trending in the right direction.

    Before cognitively demanding work:

    • Avoid large, heavy meals immediately before intensive study or exams. Blood flow is redirected to digestion, reducing cognitive capacity temporarily.
    • A small, balanced snack (protein + complex carbohydrate) 30–60 minutes before demanding work provides stable energy without the crash that follows sugary foods.
    • Avoid energy drinks. The combination of caffeine and sugar produces a short spike followed by a crash, and the caffeine disrupts sleep — creating a cycle where poor sleep leads to more reliance on stimulants.

    Building awareness, not rules:

    • The goal is not to prescribe a meal plan. It is to help students connect what they eat to how they think. When a student notices that they focus better on days they eat breakfast, that is metacognitive awareness applied to physical foundations — the same skill described in Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation, applied to a different domain.
    ⚠ A Note on Sensitivity

    Nutrition is a sensitive topic. Some students face food insecurity and do not have full control over what they eat. Others may have eating disorders or disordered relationships with food. These protocols should be framed as awareness-building, not prescriptive. The question is never "what should you eat?" but "what do you notice about how what you eat affects how you think?" Teachers should be alert to signs of disordered eating and refer students to appropriate support.

    Reflection Prompt

    For three days, note what you eat before your most demanding class or study session, and rate your focus and energy during that session (1–5). Do you notice any patterns? What surprised you? If you could change one thing about your pre-study nutrition, what would be easiest to sustain?

    Protocol 3: Movement for Cognitive Function

    Research rationale: A meta-analysis of physical exercise and adolescent cognitive function found significant positive effects on attention, memory, and executive function (Chen et al., 2025). A single bout of moderate-intensity exercise can improve cognitive performance for up to two hours. Regular aerobic exercise produces structural changes in the hippocampus, including increased neurogenesis (Hillman, Erickson, & Kramer, 2008). Exercise also reduces anxiety and improves mood, supporting the emotional resilience discussed in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit.

    The Movement Protocol

    Daily minimum (for students):

    • Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity movement every day. This does not need to be "exercise" in the gym sense — walking briskly, cycling to school, playing a sport, dancing, or any activity that raises the heart rate counts.
    • If 30 minutes feels overwhelming, start with 10. The research on habit formation (Clear, 2018; Fogg, 2020) is clear: a small habit done consistently is more valuable than an ambitious routine done sporadically.

    Movement as a study strategy:

    • A 10–15 minute walk before a study session improves focus and recall. This is not a break from studying — it is preparation for studying.
    • If you feel your focus fading during a long study session, a brief movement break (5 minutes of walking, stretching, or bodyweight exercise) can restore attention more effectively than caffeine or pushing through fatigue.
    • Standing or walking while reviewing material (e.g., pacing while reciting key concepts) engages the motor system alongside memory, which can enhance encoding.

    Classroom movement breaks:

    • Teachers can build brief movement into lessons: stand-and-share activities, gallery walks, group work that requires moving to different stations, or a simple "stand up, stretch, sit down" between activities.
    • These breaks are not lost learning time. They protect attention for the learning that follows. A two-minute movement break that restores five minutes of focus is a net gain.
    » The Pre-Exam Walk

    Before a high-stakes exam or assessment, encourage students to take a 10-minute walk rather than cramming in the final minutes. The walk improves blood flow to the brain, reduces anxiety, and transitions the body from a stress state to a readiness state. The last-minute cramming, by contrast, often increases anxiety without meaningfully improving recall — the knowledge is either consolidated or it is not.

    Reflection Prompt

    This week, try adding a 10-minute walk before one study session and notice the difference. Rate your focus and energy during that session compared to a session without movement beforehand. What did you notice? Could this become a routine?

    Protocol 4: The Foundation Audit (Combining All Three)

    This protocol, also described in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit, brings the three foundations together into a single self-assessment exercise. It can be used as an opening activity for a unit on wellbeing, metacognition, or learning-to-learn, or as a periodic check-in throughout the year.

    Instructions for Students

    For one week, track three variables each day:

    1. Sleep: What time did you go to bed? What time did you wake up? How many hours of sleep? Rate your sleep quality 1–5.
    2. Nutrition: Did you eat breakfast? Rate the overall quality of what you ate today 1–5 (where 1 = mostly processed/skipped meals and 5 = mostly whole foods, regular meals).
    3. Movement: How many minutes of moderate physical activity? What did you do?

    At the end of each day, also rate:

    1. Energy: 1–5, how energetic did you feel?
    2. Focus: 1–5, how well could you concentrate during your most demanding task?
    3. Mood: 1–5, how would you rate your overall mood?

    End-of-Week Reflection

    After one week of tracking, answer:

    • What patterns do you see between your physical foundations (sleep, nutrition, movement) and your cognitive/emotional outcomes (energy, focus, mood)?
    • Which foundation seems to have the biggest effect on your learning?
    • Where are the gaps? Which foundation is weakest for you?
    • What would change if you improved just one foundation by 10%?
    • What is one specific, small change you could commit to for the next two weeks?
    ℹ Why This Works

    The Foundation Audit applies the measurement principle from Johnson's (2024) Blueprint framework: you cannot improve what you do not measure. But more importantly, it builds the metacognitive awareness (Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation) that connects physical habits to learning capacity. Students do not need to be told that sleep matters — they need to see it in their own data. The audit makes the invisible visible.
