    Chapter 35 — Being More Human in an AI Age

    ◉ Overview

    The arrival of AI systems capable of producing fluent prose, solving complex problems, and generating code from plain-language descriptions has triggered a crisis of professional identity across industries. A UC Berkeley study (Ranganathan & Ye, 2026) found that 62% of intensive AI users reported burnout and anxiety within six months — not from fear of replacement, but from the psychological toll of constantly redefining their role alongside machines that improve weekly. This chapter argues that the response, for education in particular, is not to race against AI but to invest more deeply in what makes learning human: productive struggle, reflective practice, ethical reasoning, and the cultivation of identity through genuine intellectual effort.

    The February 2026 Moment

    In February 2026, Matt Shumer, CEO of the AI company OthersideAI, published an essay titled "Something Big Is Happening" that accumulated over 60 million views in two days. His central claim was stark: he was no longer needed for the technical work of his own job. He could describe what he wanted built in plain English, walk away, and return to find the finished product — done well, done better than he could have done it himself (Shumer, 2026).

    The reaction was intense. Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, had already predicted that AI would eliminate 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years (Kamath, 2026) — and many in the industry considered this estimate conservative. The convergence of frontier model releases, tech layoffs (30,700 in the first six weeks of 2026 alone), and a steady erosion of entry-level job postings created what Shumer called a "February 2020 moment" — the point at which something enormous becomes undeniable.

    The speed of the shift has inverted assumptions that were conventional wisdom just four years earlier. In 2022, the standard career advice for young people was learn to code. By early 2026, employment of recent computer science graduates had fallen sharply, autonomous coding platforms were seeing revenue increases of 100x year-on-year, and the underemployment rate among new graduates had exceeded 50%. For the first time in recorded history, the unemployment rate among college graduates matched or exceeded that of non-college graduates (Yang, 2026). The implicit social contract — study hard, get a degree, secure a knowledge-economy career — was fracturing in real time.

    Perhaps the most telling detail was the reaction among those closest to the technology. Andrew Yang, who attended an AI industry conference in early 2026, described a "K-shaped response": some technologists were working around the clock, becoming supercharged by the new tools, while others — recognising that their skills were now obsolete — were quietly leaving the industry entirely. The divergence was not between optimists and pessimists but between those who could adapt their working identity fast enough and those who could not. For educators, this observation carries a sobering implication: the students we are preparing today will face not a single moment of disruption but a sustained, accelerating demand to redefine what they do and who they are, repeatedly, across a career.

    Yet the picture is more nuanced than the viral panic suggested. Gary Marcus, an NYU professor and prominent AI critic, described the Shumer article as overstated, noting that hallucinations remain common and that reliable AI coding is still far from proven at scale (Marcus, 2026). The Yale Budget Lab's analysis of labour market data found that, despite widespread anxiety, actual job displacement statistics remained remarkably stable through late 2025 — occupational shifts were no faster than they had been during the introduction of the internet (Yale Budget Lab, 2026). And Fortune published a detailed rebuttal arguing that Shumer's claims rested on flawed extrapolations from narrow use cases (Fortune, 2026).

    ℹ Key Concept — The Anxiety Gap

    The UC Berkeley study (Ranganathan & Ye, 2026) revealed a paradox: AI's most immediate impact on workers is not job loss but intensification. Rather than reducing workload, AI tools led workers to increase both the volume and variety of tasks they attempted. By month six, 62% of entry-level workers and associates reported burnout, anxiety, or decision paralysis. The real problem, as NetworkChuck argued in his widely viewed response, is not future unemployment — it is present-day anxiety, identity erosion, and the psychological toll of perpetual adaptation.

    We Are Not Our Tools

    The historical parallel that resonates most powerfully is the invention of the camera. When photography emerged in the nineteenth century, portrait painters had every reason to panic. Their livelihood depended on a skill that a machine could now perform faster, cheaper, and with greater fidelity. But the camera did not kill painting — it liberated it. Freed from the obligation to reproduce reality, painting exploded into impressionism, expressionism, abstraction, and a century of creative reinvention that would never have occurred if painters had remained tethered to portraiture.

    Eric Markowitz, a technology writer and managing partner at Nightview Capital, captured the principle in a phrase that deserves to become a mantra for educators: "We are not our tools. We never have been." What has defined humanity is not the specific technologies we use but the curiosity, tenacity, love, and passion that drive us to create, discover, and connect (NetworkChuck, 2026).

    » In Practice — The Camera and the Classroom

    If AI can generate a competent essay, then the essay-as-assessment loses its value — not because writing doesn't matter, but because the product is no longer proof of the process. The educational equivalent of photography killing portraiture would be AI-generated essays killing student writing. But the educational equivalent of photography liberating painting is this: freed from the need to produce polished final drafts as proof of learning, students can focus on the thinking that writing makes possible — the struggle to articulate, the revision of ideas, the discovery that you do not understand something until you try to explain it. The product becomes less important; the process becomes everything.

    This reframing has direct implications for the expanded TOK curriculum. If the goal of the additional 50 hours is to develop students as learners and as individuals, then the response to AI is not to ignore it, ban it, or compete with it. It is to use the existence of AI as a catalyst for asking deeper questions about what learning is for and what kind of people we are trying to become.

    The Case for Becoming More Human

    The capabilities that AI currently lacks — and that even optimistic timelines suggest will remain distinctly human for the foreseeable future — are precisely the capabilities that matter most for education focused on human development.

    The first is productive struggle. As explored in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties, the experience of wrestling with a problem, failing, revising, and eventually understanding is not an inefficiency to be optimised away — it is the mechanism by which durable learning occurs. AI can produce answers instantly; what it cannot do is develop understanding through effort. A student who uses AI to skip the struggle skips the learning. A student who understands why struggle matters — who has internalised the science of desirable difficulties — will choose to struggle, because they know it is the only path to genuine competence.

    The second is reflective practice. The capacity to step back from one's own thinking, evaluate it, identify gaps, and adjust is the hallmark of metacognitive sophistication (Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation). AI does not reflect on its own processes in any meaningful sense. Humans can — and when they do, they develop the kind of adaptive expertise that transfers across contexts and persists across a lifetime.

    ℹ Key Concept — The Human Advantage

    In an age where AI can generate, summarise, analyse, and produce at superhuman speed, the distinctly human capacities become the differentiators: the ability to struggle productively with ideas that resist easy resolution; the capacity for genuine empathy and ethical reasoning; the willingness to take creative risks without guaranteed outcomes; the metacognitive awareness to monitor and direct one's own learning; and the formation of identity through sustained intellectual and moral effort.

    The third is ethical reasoning. Theory of Knowledge is, at its core, a course about epistemic responsibility — about how we know what we claim to know and what obligations that knowledge creates. AI raises these questions with unprecedented urgency. When a student can generate a convincing argument without understanding it, what does it mean to "know" something? When AI produces confident but hallucinated claims, how do we cultivate the critical literacy to evaluate them? These are not abstract philosophical puzzles — they are practical skills that every student will need.

    The fourth is identity formation. The Danish concept of dannelse (Ch-29-Dannelse-and-Character-Formation) captures something that no amount of AI capability can replace: the development of a person's character, values, and sense of self through engagement with challenging ideas and difficult experiences. When a student works through a demanding text, debates a controversial claim, or teaches a concept to a peer, they are not just learning content — they are becoming someone. This process of becoming is irreducibly human and cannot be outsourced to a machine.

    ? For Reflection

    If your students could use AI to complete any task you assign, which tasks would still be worth doing? What does your answer reveal about what you actually value in education — the product or the process? And how might you redesign your practice to make the process itself the point?

    What This Means for Schools

    The temptation in an AI age is to react with either prohibition (ban the tools) or capitulation (let students use them freely). Neither response serves student development.

    A more productive approach begins with transparency. Teach students what AI is, how it works, where it fails, and what it cannot do. This is not a separate "AI literacy" unit — it is embedded in every subject, every day, as naturally as teaching students to evaluate print sources was embedded in the pre-digital era. The existing chapter on AI in education (Ch-32-AI-in-Education) provides the research foundation; this chapter provides the philosophical frame.

    The next step is revaluing process over product. When AI can produce the product, the product is no longer meaningful evidence of learning. Assessment must shift toward processes that AI cannot replicate: live oral examinations, reflective journals on the learning process, peer teaching demonstrations, problem-solving walkthroughs, and portfolio evidence of intellectual growth over time. These are also better assessments by any standard — they capture deeper learning than a polished final essay ever could.

    The third step is cultivating the "every student is a teacher" disposition described in Ch-18-Learning-to-Learn. When students understand how learning works, can diagnose their own knowledge gaps, and can explain concepts to others, they possess a form of competence that no AI can provide or replace. They are not just consumers of knowledge — they are agents of their own development.

    » In Practice — Redesigning Assessment for an AI Age

    A TOK teacher might replace a traditional essay assignment with a thinking process portfolio: students document their initial confusion about a knowledge question, their attempts to research and reason through it (including dead ends), their evolving understanding, and a final reflection on what they learned about their own thinking. AI can generate the essay; it cannot generate the authentic record of a mind grappling with uncertainty. The portfolio assesses exactly what matters: the capacity to think, struggle, and grow.

    IB CAS as a Vehicle for Foundational Skills

    If the scale of AI disruption described above is even partially accurate, then education must do more than develop cognitive competencies in the classroom. Students need opportunities to build foundational human capacities — creativity, initiative, ethical engagement, and the ability to act on their own values — in contexts that extend beyond academic exercises. The IB's Creativity, Activity, Service (CAS) programme provides exactly this structure (IBO, 2015).

    CAS requires Diploma Programme students to engage in sustained projects across three strands: creativity (exploring and extending ideas that lead to an original product or performance), activity (physical exertion contributing to a healthy lifestyle), and service (collaborative and reciprocal engagement with the community) (IBO, 2015). Critically, CAS is student-driven: learners identify their own interests, design their own projects, and reflect on their own growth. This is not curriculum delivered to students — it is agency practised by students.

    In an AI age, CAS becomes more important, not less. The foundational skills that will matter most — the capacity to identify a problem worth solving, to collaborate with diverse people toward a shared goal, to persevere through real-world complexity, and to reflect honestly on one's own development — are precisely the skills that CAS cultivates and that AI cannot replicate. A student who has organised a community workshop, trained for and completed a physical challenge, or created an original artistic work has reference experiences of human agency that no amount of AI-generated content can provide.

    The connection to the expanded TOK curriculum is direct. The competency research explored in this textbook — critical thinking, metacognition, self-regulation, resilience, collaboration — provides the theoretical foundation. CAS provides the practical arena where those competencies are tested against real-world demands. When a student applies the stress mindset research from Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit to manage anxiety before a public service presentation, or uses the collaboration frameworks from Ch-05-Collaboration-and-Teamwork to navigate conflict in a group CAS project, the competency moves from classroom concept to lived capacity.

    Moreover, CAS offers a natural structure for individualising the response to AI disruption. Students whose interests lie in technology can design CAS projects that explore ethical AI use in their community. Students drawn to creative expression can develop original work that demonstrates distinctly human creative capacity. Students concerned about social equity can investigate how AI affects different communities and design service responses. The three strands are broad enough to accommodate virtually any student interest while ensuring that every student develops the foundational human capacities — initiative, reflection, ethical engagement, and perseverance — that the AI age demands.

    » In Practice — CAS as AI-Age Resilience

    A student reads about AI's impact on creative industries and, through CAS, designs a project that pairs AI tools with traditional artistic techniques to produce a community art exhibition. The creativity strand develops original thinking; the service strand connects the work to the community; and the structured reflection required by CAS develops the metacognitive capacity to articulate what the student learned about human creativity, AI's role, and their own growth. This is not avoidance of AI — it is thoughtful engagement with it, grounded in human agency.

    Mapping the CAS Learning Outcomes to the Core

    The IB specifies seven CAS learning outcomes that every Diploma Programme student must demonstrate (IBO, 2015). When these outcomes are mapped explicitly to the competency research in this textbook, CAS stops being a separate obligation and becomes the practical arena where the core skills are tested under real-world conditions. The mapping also provides a means of accountability that extends beyond TOK teachers — CAS coordinators become partners in developing the same competencies.

    LO 1 — Identify own strengths and develop areas for growth maps directly to the metacognitive self-knowledge described in Ch-15-Metacognition-and-Self-Regulation and the Foundation Audit in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit. When a student identifies that their public speaking is a growth area and designs a CAS experience to address it, they are practising the plan-monitor-evaluate cycle.

    LO 2 — Demonstrate that challenges have been undertaken, developing new skills is the CAS equivalent of Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties. The requirement for new challenges aligns precisely with the desirable difficulties research — growth requires working at the edge of current capacity.

    LO 3 — Demonstrate how to initiate and plan a CAS experience operationalises the self-directed learning described in Ch-07-Self-Directed-Learning and the systems-not-motivation principle from Ch-18-Learning-to-Learn.

    LO 4 — Show commitment to and perseverance in CAS experiences connects to the resilience and grit research in Ch-20-The-Emotional-Learner-Wellbeing-Resilience-and-Grit and the self-rescue principle in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties.

    LO 5 — Demonstrate skills and benefits of working collaboratively maps to Ch-05-Collaboration-and-Teamwork and the social resilience dimension of McGonigal's framework.

    LO 6 — Demonstrate engagement with issues of global significance connects to Ch-08-Leadership-Communication-and-Global-Competence and the OECD's global competence framework.

    LO 7 — Recognise and consider the ethics of choices and actions is the CAS expression of the ethical reasoning that TOK develops — the bridge between epistemological awareness and ethical action.

    This mapping transforms the CAS coordinator from an administrative role into a development partner. When both the TOK teacher and the CAS coordinator are working from the same competency framework, they reinforce each other's work. A student's CAS reflection can reference the metacognitive tools learned in TOK; a TOK lesson can draw on the authentic experiences from CAS as real-life situations for epistemological analysis. The core is no longer the responsibility of one teacher — it is distributed across the programme.

    Digital Literacy as a CAS Club: Building Foundational Technical Skills

    A specific and pressing problem deserves its own treatment: many students are highly phone-dependent but lack foundational computer literacy. They can navigate Instagram but struggle with file management, spreadsheet formulas, email etiquette, or basic troubleshooting. This deficit is invisible because phone fluency is mistaken for digital competence, but it has real consequences for university preparation, workplace readiness, and intellectual autonomy.

    The expanded core could address this through a CAS club model — an extracurricular setting where students learn by doing and building rather than being taught at. The CAS framework is ideal because it provides structure (learning outcomes, reflection, documentation) without requiring classroom time, and it frames technical skill acquisition as personal development rather than remediation.

    A minimum viable digital skill set for a CAS digital literacy club might include: touch typing (a foundational skill that dramatically affects productivity and is rarely taught), file organisation and cloud storage management, spreadsheet fundamentals (formulas, functions, data visualisation), basic presentation design principles, email and professional communication norms, password management and digital security, and introductory coding or automation (scripting simple repetitive tasks). The emphasis should be on building and doing — students create a personal website, automate a workflow, design a data visualisation of something they care about — rather than on receiving instruction about technology.

    ◆ Design Principle — Autonomy and Making, Not Instruction

    The worst way to teach digital skills is through a lecture on how to use software. The CAS club model should mirror the maker education principles described in Ch-27-Project-Based-Learning: students identify a real problem or project, learn the tools needed to complete it, and reflect on what they learned. A student who builds a budget tracker in a spreadsheet to manage their own finances learns spreadsheet skills more durably than one who completes a worksheet about formulas, and the experience connects to the autonomy and self-directed learning principles in Ch-19-Motivation-Autonomy-and-the-Adult-Learner.

    This is not a bolt-on. The connection to the broader themes of the core is direct: digital literacy is a modern expression of the information literacy described in Ch-06-Information-and-Digital-Literacy, and the capacity to use tools effectively and independently is a form of the self-reliance that pervades the competency research. A student who can troubleshoot a technical problem without calling for help has practised the same self-rescue principle described in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties. The CAS club simply provides the structure and the motivation to develop these skills outside classroom time, with the accountability that CAS reflection and documentation provide.

    Relentless Optimism

    The instinct to panic about AI is understandable. But the history of technology and human culture suggests a different conclusion. Every major technological disruption — the printing press, the camera, the calculator, the internet — triggered predictions of human obsolescence that turned out to be wrong. Not because the technology was less powerful than predicted, but because humans are more adaptable, more creative, and more irreducibly human than the pessimists expected.

    The response for educators is what NetworkChuck called "relentless optimism" — not naive denial of AI's impact, but a grounded confidence that the skills we develop in students are the skills that matter most precisely because they cannot be automated. Curiosity, tenacity, empathy, ethical reasoning, the capacity for productive struggle, the ability to reflect on and direct one's own learning — these are not relics of a pre-AI era. They are the foundation of a fully human life, and they are exactly what an expanded TOK curriculum should develop.

    The camera did not kill painting. It made painting more essential, more honest, more itself. AI will not kill learning. It will make learning more essential, more honest, and more human — if we have the courage to teach that way.

    Counterpoint — Can Simple Habits Be Enough?

    Not everyone frames the response to digital disengagement through systematic educational reform. In a TEDNext 2025 talk, journalist Nayeema Raza proposed just three "old habits" to reclaim curiosity: pause (wait one second before reaching for your phone), wonder (resist googling every question immediately), and ask a question out loud (have a real conversation instead of retreating to your device) (Raza, 2025). Her argument is deliberately modest — not a curriculum redesign but a personal discipline.

    Raza's framing offers a useful point of comparison. She begins with an observation that echoes the research in Ch-03-Skills-at-Risk-in-a-Digital-Age: an 11-year-old named Sophie told her that FaceTime feels more connected than being physically together, because in person everyone is on their phones. The paradox — that we are most alone when physically together and most together when inside our devices — is a vivid restatement of the "mere presence" effect documented in the attention research.

    Where Raza's approach diverges from the framework developed in this textbook is in its theory of change. Her three habits locate the solution entirely in individual willpower: pause before you reach, resist the urge to search, ask a person rather than a machine. This is appealing and practical — and the one-second pause she describes is consistent with research on impulse control and prefrontal engagement (Diamond, 2013). But it leaves unaddressed the structural and pedagogical questions this textbook considers essential: How do we build environments where pausing is the norm rather than the exception? How do we cultivate the capacity for wonder in students who have never experienced sustained uncertainty as anything other than discomfort? How do we design collaborative structures that make asking questions of real people more rewarding than consulting an algorithm?

    The answer this textbook offers is that individual habits, while valuable, require institutional scaffolding. The expanded TOK curriculum, the productive struggle framework (Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties), the thinking classroom structures (Ch-21-Building-Thinking-Classrooms), and the CAS programme all create conditions under which Raza's three habits become not heroic acts of self-discipline but natural consequences of a well-designed learning environment. Raza's instinct is right — we do need to practise being more present, more curious, and more willing to engage with other humans. The contribution of education is to ensure that students do not have to do this alone.

    ? For Reflection

    Raza argues that presence, curiosity, and connection were once "possible outside of technology" and that we must practise these "old habits" to pass them on. Is this framing nostalgic or realistic? Can individual behavioural habits be sufficient to counter the structural incentives of an attention economy, or does meaningful change require the kind of institutional redesign this textbook advocates? What role should schools play in creating environments where presence and curiosity are the default rather than the exception?

    Carpe Diem: The Urgency of Beginning

    The Latin phrase carpe diem — seize the day — has survived for over two thousand years because it captures something that every generation needs to hear: the time to act is now, not when conditions are perfect. Horace's original poem (Odes 1.11, 23 BCE) was not a call to hedonism but to engagement — do not waste the present waiting for a future that may not arrive. In an educational context where AI disruption generates anxiety and paralysis, where the scale of change can feel overwhelming, this ancient insight has renewed force.

    The motivational tradition in education draws on this principle, often through speeches, videos, and stories that attempt to make the abstract urgency of self-improvement feel visceral and immediate. When a teacher shows students a compelling talk about seizing opportunity, or about the consequences of passivity, the effect is not merely emotional; it is epistemic. The student briefly sees their own situation from outside — recognises the gap between who they are and who they could become — and that recognition is itself a form of knowledge. The Stoic tradition calls this prolepsis: a sudden flash of understanding that is felt before it is articulated (Sellars, 2006).

    The danger is that motivational moments evaporate. A student watches a powerful speech on Monday and has forgotten the feeling by Wednesday. The educational challenge is to convert the carpe diem impulse into durable practice — to connect the emotional spike to the structural habits this textbook develops: goal-setting and identity work (Ch-19), public commitment and virtuous feedback cycles (Ch-19a), reflective journaling (Ch-28), and the documented transformation model (Ch-20).

    The most effective use of motivational content is not as a standalone event but as a hook — an emotional entry point that makes the deeper, harder work of competency development feel worthwhile. A student who has been moved by a speech about personal responsibility is more receptive to the metacognitive frameworks that make personal responsibility actionable. The speech opens the door; the curriculum walks through it.

    » In Practice — The Motivational Hook Sequence
    1. Hook (10 min): Show a short, compelling video or read a passage that challenges students to take ownership of their learning and their lives.
    2. Reflect (5 min): Students write: "What hit me? What do I want to change?"
    3. Connect (10 min): Teacher explicitly links the emotional insight to a specific competency or strategy from the course: "The speaker talked about not waiting. We've been learning about goal-setting and public commitment. Today we're going to use that feeling to set one concrete commitment."
    4. Act (remaining time): Students take a specific, concrete action — write a public goal, begin a difficult conversation, draft a plan.
    5. Follow up (next lesson): Return to the commitment. What happened? What was hard? What did you learn?

    This sequence respects both the power of emotional engagement and its fragility. The hook matters — without it, students approach competency work as compliance. But the hook without the structure is a sugar rush. The combination of motivational energy and systematic follow-through is what produces lasting change.
