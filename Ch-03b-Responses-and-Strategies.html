    Responses and Strategies: Digital Dannelse and Intentional Design

    ◉ Overview

    Rather than rejecting technology or lamenting decline, effective education requires deliberate strategies to preserve essential cognitive skills while leveraging genuine technological benefits. This sub-chapter presents the Danish framework of digital dannelse (digital formation) and practical implementation strategies for schools.

    See also: Six Skills Under Pressure: Understanding Cognitive Risk

    The Danish Response: Digital Dannelse

    Rather than treating AI and digital technology as purely technical issues, the Danish concept of digital dannelse (digital formation) provides a productive philosophical framework. Digital dannelse connects general formation (almen dannelse)—the development of the person as a critical, ethical, culturally engaged individual—to digital competencies and study strategies.

    This framing treats technology not as a neutral tool to be optimised but as a formation question: What kind of thinker am I becoming if I outsource this task to technology? When does using AI help me learn, and when does it prevent learning? What is my responsibility when I use AI-generated content?

    Silkeborg Gymnasium exemplifies this approach. Rather than banning or uncritically embracing AI, the school has positioned it as læringsfremmende—learning-promoting. All teachers have received AI competency training. Professional learning communities (PLF groups) experiment with AI in subject-specific contexts—for example, using AI to support Danish writing competencies rather than replace them. The guiding principle is that technology should enhance learning, not substitute for thinking.

    This is far more powerful than either technology rejection ("AI is dangerous, we cannot use it") or uncritical adoption ("AI is the future, let's integrate it everywhere"). Digital dannelse asks: For this particular learning goal, in this particular context, does this particular use of technology support or undermine the formation we are aiming for?

    Strategies for Preservation: Intentional Design

    Rather than rejecting technology or lamenting decline, effective education requires deliberate strategies to preserve essential cognitive skills while leveraging genuine technological benefits.

    Guiding Principles

    Technology should serve learning, not replace thinking. The goal is not to eliminate technology but to ensure it enhances rather than replaces the cognitive processes that drive deep learning. Technology is most beneficial when it supports deep processing, retrieval practice, and collaboration—and most harmful when it enables passive consumption.

    Teach the skill first, then add the tool. Students should develop foundational competencies before relying on tools that augment those skills. A student who cannot evaluate a source with technology will not do so more effectively with a search engine at hand. Critical thinking must be developed first; tools can then amplify it.

    Make technology choices visible and metacognitive. Help students develop metacognitive awareness of how different tools affect their thinking. "Am I using this tool because it helps me learn, or because it is easier?"

    Implementation Strategies by Skill

    For deep reading, the preservation strategy is sustained silent reading in print texts, combined with explicit close reading instruction (annotation, questioning, re-reading). Digital texts should be used for search and reference; demanding texts should be read on paper.

    For sustained attention, protect time for tech-free focus blocks, teach metacognitive awareness of distraction, establish single-tasking norms, and remove distracting devices during focused work.

    For handwriting, maintain daily handwriting for notes and reflection, use handwriting specifically in thinking tasks on vertical non-permanent surfaces (a core Building Thinking Classrooms practice), use typing for production tasks where speed matters, and explicitly teach note-taking strategies.

    For critical source evaluation, teach lateral reading strategies, create explicit digital literacy curriculum, use technology to access diverse sources while teaching how to evaluate them, and help students understand how AI generates content and where it fails.

    For independent thinking, deliberately create "analogue zones"—times and spaces where thinking happens without technological mediation. The Building Thinking Classrooms practices naturally create these zones: thinking tasks on VNPS happen without devices; group discussion is face-to-face; thinking happens through discussion before being recorded.

    School-Level Implementation

    At the school level, intentional design requires:

    1. A school-wide technology philosophy articulating when technology adds value and when analogue approaches serve learning better
    2. Technology audits identifying where technology is used by default rather than design
    3. Professional development training teachers in the cognitive science behind technology choices
    4. Curriculum protection building in designated time for skills that technology may displace—sustained silent reading, handwriting practice, focused collaborative work without devices
    ⚠ The Balance

    None of this advocates rejecting technology. The evidence shows technology has small but positive overall effects on learning. Digital tools can enhance collaboration, provide access to information, support multimedia learning, and offer practice and feedback at scale. The argument is for intentional integration, not technology rejection. And for protection—deliberate creation of time and space where certain skills are preserved and developed.

    What Changes With Artificial Intelligence

    The emergence of generative AI clarifies the imperative but also shows where intentional design can work. Four practical principles guide integration:

    AI as a tool for thinking, not a replacement. Good use: students generate a first draft with AI, then critically evaluate and revise it. Bad use: students submit AI output as their own work without engagement.

    AI literacy as part of information literacy. Students need to understand how AI generates output (statistical patterns, not understanding), why it "hallucinates" (confabulates plausible false information), and how to verify AI claims.

    Protect spaces for AI-free thinking. Some cognitive activities should remain deliberately AI-free: thinking tasks on vertical surfaces with no devices, handwritten reflection, sustained reading of print texts, face-to-face collaborative problem-solving.

    Redesign assessment. If AI can produce competent work quickly, traditional take-home assessments are obsolete. Assessment must capture thinking, process, and judgment—portfolios showing revision, oral defences, thinking routine documentation, in-class problem-solving.

    Connections

    This chapter completes Part I's argument. Chapter 1 established that competency development must be intentional. Chapter 2 identified what competencies matter through international frameworks and Danish philosophy. Chapter 3 shows what threatens the development of those competencies—and how intentional design can protect essential skills while leveraging technology's benefits.

    Part II turns to the detailed work of teaching specific competencies. Critical thinking, the foundation of all higher-order competencies, is the natural starting point. Subsequent chapters explore communication, collaboration, creativity, metacognition, and others. But the foundation established in Part I is essential: these competencies require explicit teaching, protection from technological erosion, and integration of both international research and Danish educational values.
