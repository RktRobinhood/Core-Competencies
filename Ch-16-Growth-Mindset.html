    Chapter 16 — Growth Mindset

    ◉ Overview

    Carol Dweck's theory of growth mindset has become one of the most influential ideas in education. Students who believe intelligence and abilities can be developed often show greater effort, persistence, and achievement than those with fixed mindsets. However, recent research reveals that mindset effects are real but modest, and that implementation quality matters enormously. This chapter presents a balanced assessment: what the evidence supports, what remains controversial, and how to implement growth mindset thinking productively in schools.

    The Core Theory: Fixed vs. Growth Mindsets

    Dweck (2006) distinguishes two implicit beliefs about intelligence and ability. Students with a fixed mindset believe that intelligence is a fixed trait. You are born with a certain amount of ability, and it does not change much. In this view, talent is innate, effort signals low ability (if you have to work hard, you must not be smart), and failure is proof of insufficient talent. Students with fixed mindsets tend to avoid challenges, give up when work is difficult, and feel threatened by others' success.

    In contrast, students with a growth mindset believe that intelligence and abilities are developed through effort, strategies, good instruction, and help from others. In this view, talent is a starting point, effort is the path to mastery, and challenges and failure are opportunities to learn. Students with growth mindsets tend to embrace challenges, persist through difficulty, see effort as productive, learn from criticism, and find inspiration in others' success.

    Dweck argued that these implicit beliefs shape motivation and learning outcomes. A student with a growth mindset is more likely to take on difficult tasks, try different strategies when stuck, and interpret struggle as part of learning. A student with a fixed mindset is more likely to avoid challenge, give up quickly, and interpret struggle as a sign of inadequacy. Over time, these different approaches compound: growth-mindset students tackle progressively harder problems and develop deeper expertise; fixed-mindset students avoid challenge and fall further behind.

    The Evidence: What Holds Up, What Is Weaker

    The Case for Growth Mindset

    Large-scale rigorous studies have found real effects of growth mindset. Yeager and colleagues (2019) conducted a pre-registered randomized controlled trial with 12,490 students across 65 U.S. schools. Students received either a brief growth mindset intervention (a lesson teaching that intelligence can be developed) or a control condition. The intervention improved grades for lower-achieving students by about 0.1 GPA points (roughly one letter grade over a semester for some students). The effect was stronger in schools with supportive peer norms that valued growth and mutual support. This study is notable for its rigor, large sample, and real-world school settings.

    Blackwell, Trzesniewski, and Dweck (2007) found that students taught about how the brain grows through challenge (a neuroscience framing of growth mindset) showed improved motivation and mathematics achievement across a school year, particularly students who began with lower expectations.

    Where mindset interventions show the strongest effects is with specific populations: lower-socioeconomic status students, academically at-risk students, students in school transitions (such as entering secondary school), and contexts where the school culture already supports growth beliefs.

    The Controversy: Replication and Effect Size Concerns

    However, recent research has complicated the initial picture. Sisk and colleagues (2018) conducted two meta-analyses examining the relationship between mindset and achievement. They found that the association between mindset beliefs and achievement was weak (r = 0.10), and the effect of mindset interventions on achievement was small (d = 0.08). While statistically significant across many studies, these effect sizes are practically modest.

    Several attempted replications of early mindset studies have produced weaker or null results. Macnamara and Burgoyne (2023) conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis, concluding that while mindset effects exist, they are smaller than initially believed and may not justify the resources many schools invest in mindset interventions.

    The 2019 Yeager et al. study addressed these concerns by being designed as a rigorous, preregistered test of mindset theory. Its finding of real but modest effects (0.1 GPA points) appears to represent a more accurate estimate than earlier, smaller-scale studies that may have overestimated effects.

    ℹ Key Concept — Growth Mindset Effects

    Students with growth mindsets show greater persistence, motivation, and achievement than fixed-mindset peers, particularly when facing challenges. However, mindset interventions produce modest effect sizes (d = 0.08–0.10) and are most effective for disadvantaged or at-risk students in schools with supportive cultures. Mindset is real but not a panacea.

    The "Saying vs. Doing" Problem: False Growth Mindset

    A critical distinction that Dweck (2015) herself has emphasized is between true growth mindset and what she calls "false growth mindset." Many schools implement mindset through motivational posters ("You can do it!"), pep talks, and praise for effort. While well-intentioned, this approach often misses the point.

    True growth mindset is not simply praising effort. Rather, it involves explicitly connecting effort to strategy improvement and outcomes. A student who works hard on a problem but uses an ineffective strategy gains little from effort alone. Empty praise for effort—"You tried so hard!"—can actually backfire, sending the message that the teacher doubts the student's ability (if you need praise just for trying, you must not be able to do this).

    Effective growth mindset practice involves strategy-focused feedback: "Your strategy of breaking the problem into steps worked well" or "Let's try a different approach to see if it works better." This connects effort to improvement, avoiding the pitfall of praising effort without linking it to actual learning gains.

    Controversies and Limitations

    1. Effect Sizes Are Modest

    As noted above, mindset interventions produce small effect sizes (d = 0.08–0.10). For a school investing significant staff time in mindset training, the return is genuine but limited. Macnamara and Burgoyne (2023) ask whether this justifies the resources spent. A fair answer is: mindset matters, but it is one of many factors affecting learning, and it is not a substitute for high-quality instruction, adequate resources, or effective pedagogy.

    2. The Individualism Critique

    Mindset theory can place the burden of success on students' individual beliefs, potentially obscuring structural inequities. A student's mindset certainly matters, but so do curriculum quality, teacher expertise, school resources, peer culture, and broader societal factors. Focusing exclusively on changing mindset risks implying that if a disadvantaged student fails, it is because of fixed thinking rather than because of inadequate educational resources. This misattribution can enable complacency about structural barriers.

    3. Implementation Variability

    Much of the variability in mindset effects likely reflects implementation quality. A brief, well-designed intervention taught by a credible instructor has shown modest but real benefits. In contrast, mindset posters alone, divorced from pedagogical change, are unlikely to move achievement significantly.

    4. Mindset Stability

    A question that remains open is whether brief mindset interventions produce lasting changes in beliefs, or whether effects fade over time without continued support. The most rigorous studies show benefits, but the duration of these benefits and the conditions needed to sustain them deserve more research.

    ⚠ Controversy

    The growth mindset movement illustrates a common pattern in education: an idea with solid theoretical foundations and promising research becomes popularized, sometimes in oversimplified or distorted forms, and then backlash occurs when outcomes fall short of inflated expectations. The truth lies in the middle: growth mindset is real, effects are meaningful, but modest. Mindset is necessary but not sufficient for educational success. Schools that have implemented mindset superficially (posters and slogans) without pedagogical change have understandably been disappointed. Schools that have embedded growth mindset into pedagogy, feedback practices, and classroom culture have seen more substantial benefits.

    What Works in Practice: A Grounded Approach

    Despite the controversies, the core pedagogical implications of growth mindset remain sound when implemented carefully:

    Do: Praise Process, Not Person. Rather than "You're so smart," say "Your strategy of breaking the problem into parts worked well." Rather than "You're a math person," say "You're getting better at solving equations through practice." This focuses learner attention on effort and strategy rather than fixed traits.

    Do: Normalize Struggle. Frame difficulty as a sign of learning, not failure. When students encounter challenging material, resist the impulse to immediately simplify or rescue them. Instead, explicitly connect struggle to learning: "This is hard. That means your brain is growing." Students who see struggle as productive are more likely to persist.

    Do: Teach the Neuroscience. Students who learn that the brain physically changes through challenge—that neural connections strengthen with use—show improved motivation. This is not false hope; it is actual neuroscience. A brief lesson on how the brain grows through challenge can shift motivation, particularly for students who previously believed ability was fixed.

    Do: Connect Effort to Strategy. Effort alone is not enough. Explicitly teach that different strategies work better for different problems and contexts. "You tried hard, and your approach didn't work. Let's try a different strategy." This connects effort to strategic improvement, avoiding the trap of praising effort unconnected to outcomes.

    Do: Create Environments That Support Growth. Mindset is not just about individuals' beliefs; it is about classroom culture. Practices like Visibly Random Groups (which reduce the social risk of failure), Vertical Non-Permanent Surfaces (which make mistakes erasable and low-stakes), and meaningful homework (which reframes practice as self-improvement) create environments where risk-taking and persistence are supported structurally.

    Don't: Reduce Growth Mindset to Posters and Slogans. Mindset posters alone, without corresponding changes in feedback, pedagogy, and classroom culture, are unlikely to shift outcomes. Mindset must be embedded in how teachers talk about learning, how they respond to errors, and how they structure tasks.

    Don't: Praise Effort That Isn't Productive. Telling a student "You worked hard" when their effort was unfocused or strategic sends a message that effort alone matters regardless of outcome. Be specific about what made the effort effective: strategy, persistence, seeking help, trying a different approach.

    Don't: Imply That Mindset Alone Overcomes Structural Barriers. While mindset is important, it cannot substitute for adequate resources, excellent teaching, and inclusive curriculum. A growth mindset helps students persist, but it does not eliminate the impact of inadequate school funding, undertrained teachers, or curriculum that does not include diverse perspectives.

    Don't: Assume a One-Off Intervention Creates Lasting Change. A single lesson on growth mindset, or even a year-long focus, may not produce durable effects without continued cultural support. The most effective schools have embedded growth mindset into their daily practices and expectations across years.

    » In Practice

    A secondary mathematics classroom embeds growth mindset through several practices. Teachers use process praise: "You tried three different strategies until one worked" rather than "You're good at math." When students make errors, teachers ask, "What strategy did you use? Did it work? What would you try next?" Tasks intentionally include struggle—students spend time on problems that require persistence. The classroom uses vertical non-permanent surfaces (whiteboards, chalkboards) so students can work, erase, and revise without the shame of a permanent written record. Feedback on homework and assessments always includes a growth statement: "This is not right yet. Here's what you understand, and here's a strategy to try next." Over time, students internalize these messages and develop resilience when facing difficulty.

    Growth Mindset in the Danish Context

    Interestingly, growth mindset aligns naturally with several foundational principles in Danish education. The concept of dannelse—understood as the continuous development of the person, never fixed or complete—is fundamentally a growth mindset about human potential. The Danish emphasis on fællesskab (community) also resonates: growth happens in community, not in isolation. The new epx reform in Danish secondary schools, which offers alternative pathways for students who have not thrived in traditional academic settings, is structurally a growth mindset initiative—a belief that every student can develop competencies when given appropriate conditions.

    Growth mindset complements rather than replaces these existing Danish values. In fact, framing growth mindset as a development of existing Danish educational philosophy may make it more coherent and culturally rooted rather than appearing as an imported American concept.

    Growth Mindset and Thinking Classrooms

    Liljedahl's (2020) framework of building thinking classrooms naturally creates a growth mindset environment through structure, not just through explicit mindset messaging. When students regularly encounter challenging thinking tasks, work in mixed-ability groups, and have work displayed non-permanently, the classroom embodies growth principles: everyone can think, struggle is productive, mistakes are opportunities, and learners grow through engagement with challenging material.

    BTC Practice Growth Mindset Mechanism
    Thinking tasks Normalizes challenge; struggle is the point
    Visibly Random Groups Removes fixed social roles; everyone contributes
    Vertical Non-Permanent Surfaces Non-permanence reduces the cost of failure
    Answer only keep-thinking questions Teacher doesn't rescue; students persevere
    Meaningful homework Reframes practice as self-improvement

    In such classrooms, growth mindset is not taught through posters or one-off lessons; it is lived through daily practice.
