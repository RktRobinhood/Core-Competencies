    Appendix A5 — TOK Year 1 & Year 2 Scope and Sequence

    ◉ Overview

    This appendix provides a practical, week-by-week scope and sequence for the expanded 150-hour TOK course across two years. It is designed to be picked up and used — not admired from a distance. Each unit connects to specific AOKs, themes, concepts, competencies, and textbook chapters. The sequence moves from concrete, accessible entry points toward abstract epistemological inquiry, building students' capacity progressively rather than expecting philosophical fluency from day one.

    Design Principles

    Concrete before abstract. Every unit begins with a real-world situation, a provocation, or an experience. Students encounter the phenomenon before they encounter the theory. Knowledge questions emerge from genuine puzzlement, not from a list on a whiteboard.

    Spiral, not linear. Core concepts (evidence, perspective, justification, truth) recur across both years. Each return is deeper — students revisit the concept with more experience, more examples, and more sophisticated reasoning.

    AOKs as lenses, not topics. Instead of teaching "the Natural Sciences block" followed by "the History block," AOKs are woven through thematic units. Students compare how different AOKs approach the same question rather than studying each in isolation.

    Assessment-integrated. Exhibition and essay preparation are embedded throughout, not bolted on at the end. By the time students face formal assessment, they have practised the skills dozens of times in low-stakes contexts.

    Competency-visible. Every unit explicitly develops and names the competencies involved. Students and teachers can see which competencies are being strengthened and can track development over time.

    Year 1 — Foundations of Knowing (Approximately 75 hours)

    Year 1 establishes the vocabulary, habits, and confidence students need for genuine epistemological inquiry. The focus is on making TOK personal, concrete, and non-threatening. Students learn to ask knowledge questions by first learning what puzzles them.

    ◆ Implementation Note — Weeks 1–2

    The personal knowledge audit works best when students physically map their knowledge on large paper: things they know from experience in one colour, things they know from authority in another, things they believe but cannot prove in a third. The visual makes the abstract tangible and generates immediate discussion about what "knowing" means.

    Year 2 — Deepening and Application (Approximately 75 hours)

    Year 2 moves from foundation-building to sophisticated application. Students are now comfortable with KQs, AOK comparison, and the core concepts. The focus shifts to nuance, complexity, and preparation for the external essay.

    Week Focus Core Concept AOK Connection Competency Textbook Link
    1–2 Returning to knowing. What has changed since Year 1? Students revisit their knowledge audit and identify growth. Certainty, Perspective Core theme: Knower Metacognition Ch-15, Ch-16

    5–6 Difficult conversations as knowledge practice. Using the FBI method and three-conversation model to practise disagreement as inquiry. Perspective, Values Core theme: Knower Communication, Perspective-taking Ch-08
    7–8 The replication crisis. Students examine how the human sciences are reckoning with unreliable findings. What does this mean for knowledge? Evidence, Certainty, Truth Human Sciences, Natural Sciences Critical thinking, Information literacy Ch-04, Ch-25
    9–10 Mathematics: invented or discovered? The ontological status of mathematical knowledge. Students debate and write position papers. Truth, Certainty Mathematics Critical thinking, Communication Ch-04
    11–12 Knowledge and technology deep dive. AI, algorithms, and epistemic authority. Students evaluate whether algorithmic recommendation constitutes a form of knowledge production. Power, Evidence, Perspective Optional theme: Technology Critical thinking, Information literacy Ch-32, Ch-35

    Using This Sequence

    Adapt, don't adopt. This sequence is a starting framework, not a prescription. Schools should adjust based on their optional theme choices, student interests, assessment timelines, and available hours. The principle — concrete before abstract, spiral not linear, AOKs as lenses — matters more than any specific week assignment.

    Connect to the textbook. Each unit references specific chapters. Use the textbook reading as background for teachers and selected excerpts for students. The Pedagogical Playbook (A2) provides ready-to-use lesson plans for key units.

    Track competency development. Use the Curriculum Hook Map (A1) to identify which competencies each unit develops. Over the two years, ensure that all seven core competencies receive sustained attention across multiple units.

    Build a KQ bank. From Week 1, students should be collecting knowledge questions — their own and others'. By Year 2, a class KQ bank becomes a shared resource that demonstrates collective growth. The scaffolding guide (A6) shows how to build this progressively.
