    Rosenshine (2012) — Principles of Instruction

    Overview

    Rosenshine synthesises decades of research on effective teaching — including studies of effective teachers, cognitive science of learning, and cognitive supports — into ten research-grounded instructional principles. This is one of the most widely cited papers in teacher education globally, and it is foundational to Chapter 22 of this textbook.

    The Ten Principles

    1. Begin a Lesson with a Short Review of Previous Learning

    Daily review of previously covered material strengthens memory and retrieval. Five to eight minutes at the start of a lesson reviewing vocabulary, concepts, or procedures from prior lessons produces lasting retention gains.

    2. Present New Material in Small Steps with Student Practice After Each Step

    Novice learners cannot process too much new information at once (cognitive load). Present one element at a time and check understanding before moving forward. Overloading working memory impairs learning.

    3. Ask a Large Number of Questions and Check Responses

    Frequent questions serve two purposes: they check comprehension and they prompt students to actively recall and process information. Rosenshine recommends checking all students, not only those who volunteer.

    4. Provide Models

    Explicit worked examples and think-alouds show students how to apply a procedure or approach a problem. Modelling reduces the cognitive load of figuring out process while simultaneously processing content.

    5. Guide Student Practice

    Guided practice, where the teacher circulates and provides feedback as students attempt problems, bridges between instruction and independent practice. Approximately 80% student success rate during guided practice predicts later independent mastery.

    6. Check for Student Understanding

    Continuously monitor comprehension using a variety of checking strategies (mini-whiteboards, cold calls, exit slips, turn-and-talk). Do not wait until formal assessment to discover misconceptions.

    7. Obtain a High Success Rate

    Students who experience approximately 80% success during guided practice and 90–95% during independent practice consolidate learning most effectively. Very low success rates indicate material is too difficult; very high rates may indicate insufficient challenge.

    8. Provide Scaffolds for Difficult Tasks

    For complex tasks, provide students with scaffolds (sentence frames, graphic organisers, worked examples, checklists) that reduce extraneous cognitive load while they are developing competence. Scaffolds should be gradually removed (faded) as competence develops.

    9. Require and Monitor Independent Practice

    Extended, independent practice is necessary to develop fluency and automaticity. Homework, seatwork, and practice exercises should be at an appropriate success level (90%+) so that practice reinforces correct procedures rather than cementing errors.

    10. Engage Students in Weekly and Monthly Review

    Beyond daily review, systematic weekly and monthly review is essential for long-term retention. Spaced review prevents forgetting and strengthens retrieval pathways.

    Three Evidence Streams

    Rosenshine's principles converge from three independent research traditions:

    1. Studies of master teachers — What do the most effective teachers actually do in classrooms?
    2. Cognitive science of learning — What does the science of memory and expertise tell us about instruction?
    3. Cognitive supports research — What types of scaffolds and supports help learners acquire new knowledge?

    That all three converge on the same practices provides strong evidence that the principles reflect how humans actually learn.

    Relevance to Textbook

    Chapter 22 (Principles of Effective Instruction) is built directly on this paper. Rosenshine's principles translate cognitive science (Ch-14 Cognitive Load, Ch-13 Retrieval Practice) into classroom practice. The framework provides the lesson-design architecture that Chapter 22 argues should structure every teaching sequence.
