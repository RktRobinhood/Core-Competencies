    Part II — Core Competencies: An Overview

    The expansion of IB Theory of Knowledge from 100 to 150 hours at a Danish school presents a unique opportunity to develop not only students' theoretical understanding but also their core competencies as learners and global citizens. While TOK traditionally focuses on the nature of knowledge itself, this extended programme grounds that philosophical inquiry in the practical skills that enable students to think critically, work collaboratively, navigate information responsibly, and reflect on their own learning.

    Part II of this textbook examines six core competencies that are both central to effective learning and increasingly demanded in higher education and professional contexts. These competencies appear consistently across major international frameworks—from the Partnership for 21st Century Learning (P21) to the International Baccalaureate's Approaches to Learning (ATL)—and align deeply with the Danish educational tradition of dannelse (character formation and human development).

    The chapters that follow explore each competency in depth: Ch-04-Critical-Thinking examines how students can analyse and evaluate claims with rigour and intellectual integrity. Ch-05-Collaboration-and-Teamwork investigates the evidence on what makes group work genuinely productive. Ch-06-Information-and-Digital-Literacy addresses the essential skills for navigating a world of abundant information and pervasive digital technologies. Ch-07-Self-Directed-Learning develops students' capacity to take ownership of their learning processes through metacognition and self-regulation. Ch-08-Leadership-Communication-and-Global-Competence extends these competencies into leadership, cross-cultural communication, and global awareness. Ch-09-Comparing-Competency-Frameworks draws together the major international frameworks and situates them within the Danish context. Ch-10-IB-Approaches-to-Learning examines the IB's own Approaches to Learning framework — the institutional backbone for embedding competency development across subjects — and explores how the expanded TOK hours create a unique opportunity to make ATL instruction explicit, scaffolded, and genuinely transformative. Finally, Ch-11-Creativity-and-Creative-Thinking argues that creativity is not a gift reserved for artists but a cognitive competency as teachable as critical thinking — and shows how design thinking, divergent-convergent cycles, and student agency can embed creative thinking into every aspect of a TOK classroom.

    Each chapter is grounded in current educational research, illustrated with classroom examples, and designed to help educators and students understand not only what these competencies are but why they matter and how to develop them systematically. The evidence is clear: when competencies are taught explicitly, scaffolded carefully, and embedded within authentic learning contexts, students develop deeper understanding and greater agency as learners.
