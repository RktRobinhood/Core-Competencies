    Chapter 32 — AI in Education: Competencies, Systems, and Human Formation

    ◉ Overview

    The rapid emergence of generative AI—large language models, image generators, code assistants—is not a marginal development for education. It reshapes which competencies matter most, which skills are at risk, and how schools can be organized efficiently. This chapter examines how AI changes the competency landscape, explores the case for an AI-first systems strategy, and argues for an explicit human-first outcome framework that prevents the efficiency gains from becoming losses in human capability and agency.

    How AI Changes the Competency Landscape

    When a tool can generate fluent text, create images, write code, and answer questions in seconds, the educational implications are profound (Kasneci et al., 2023; Holmes, Bialik, & Fadel, 2023). Some competencies become more important, some change in character, and some face accelerated erosion.

    Competencies That Become MORE Important

    Critical thinking elevates in importance. When AI output is fluent and confident but potentially inaccurate, the human capacity to evaluate claims, spot bias, and distinguish evidence from speculation becomes essential. A student must be able to ask: "Is this true? How do I know? What are the limitations of this response?" These are critical thinking questions, and they have become more urgent, not less.

    Metacognition and self-regulation become more essential. Students must monitor when to use AI and when to think independently, whether AI output meets their goals, and whether they are actually learning or just obtaining answers. Without strong metacognitive capability, AI becomes a cognitive shortcut that bypasses learning.

    Information and digital literacy evolve in importance. Source evaluation is harder when AI can generate plausible-sounding but false content (Stanford Report, 2025). Students must understand how AI works, why it "hallucinates" (confabulates false information with confidence), and how to verify claims from multiple independent sources.

    Creativity and originality shift in value. As AI handles routine generation, human value shifts toward original thinking, novel connections, and novel problems. The ability to ask good questions, to see problems others miss, and to create genuine novelty becomes a key differentiator.

    Ethical reasoning emerges as a new and essential competency (UNESCO, 2023). Students need to reflect on when and how to use AI responsibly: What should not be outsourced to AI? What is my responsibility when I use AI-generated content? What are the environmental and social costs? These are ethical questions, and they now require explicit attention in curriculum.

    Questioning and prompting becomes a skill. The ability to ask good questions—of AI and in general—becomes a differentiating skill. The quality of your AI output depends on the quality of your prompt. More broadly, the capacity to formulate clear, specific questions drives learning.

    ℹ Key Concept — Competency Shift

    AI does not make existing competencies obsolete. Rather, it shifts the balance and reveals which competencies are truly important. The competencies that depend on speed or routine processing (quick recall, basic summarization, standard problem-solving) face pressure. The competencies that depend on human judgment, creativity, ethical reasoning, and the ability to think about thinking become more valuable, not less.

    Competencies Whose Nature Changes

    Writing shifts from text generation to thinking through writing and revision. AI can produce fluent text instantly. But writing as a learning tool—where the act of articulating an idea helps clarify thinking—becomes more important to protect. The competency is not "writing beautiful sentences"; it is "thinking deeply and expressing that thinking clearly." AI can help with revision and alternative framings, but the human cognitive work of thinking is what matters.

    Research transforms from "finding information" to "evaluating, synthesizing, and judging" information from multiple sources including AI. A student must be able to triangulate sources, distinguish expert from popular interpretation, understand confidence levels and uncertainty, and make judgments under incomplete information. These are advanced research competencies, and they become more essential as information floods in from algorithmic and generative sources.

    Collaboration now includes human-AI collaboration. Students need to understand how to use AI as a tool within teams, how to divide work between human and machine reasoning, and how to maintain group cohesion when some members approach AI differently. This expands the collaboration competency; it does not replace it.

    Problem-solving shifts toward problem finding and evaluation. AI can solve many routine problems. Human value emerges in problem finding (noticing that a problem exists), problem framing (defining what the real problem is, not just the obvious one), and solution evaluation (is the proposed solution ethical, sustainable, good for all stakeholders?).

    » In Practice

    Consider a student in a science class who uses AI to help model a complex system. With an AI-first approach, the student might: (1) First understand the system well enough to describe it to AI, (2) Ask AI to model different scenarios, (3) Critically evaluate whether the model makes sense given what they know, (4) Revise their understanding based on surprising results, (5) Propose an improvement to the model. The student is not replacing their thinking; they are using AI to expand the sophistication of problems they can tackle. The competency that matters is their ability to think about the model critically and improve it—not their ability to code the simulation themselves.

    Skills at Risk of Further Erosion

    The skills already at risk from technology face accelerated pressure with AI in the environment.

    Deep reading faces new threats. If AI can summarize any text, the motivation to read deeply diminishes further. Yet deep reading—the ability to engage with complex texts, to catch subtle meanings, to hold multiple interpretations in mind—is essential for critical thinking and cultural understanding. Protecting time and space for deep reading becomes more important, not optional.

    Handwriting continues to erode. If AI generates polished text instantly, the perceived value of handwriting, with its documented cognitive benefits for memory and learning, drops further. Yet handwriting activates different neural pathways than typing and supports memory formation in ways digital input does not (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014; Van der Meer & Van der Weel, 2017).

    Sustained effort and tolerance for struggle decline when AI provides quick answers. Building thinking classrooms, where students struggle with hard problems, becomes more countercultural in an environment where easy answers are available instantly. Yet struggle is where learning happens, and the willingness to work through difficulty is a core competency.

    Memory and knowledge retention face pressure. If everything is "googleable" or "AI-able," students may devalue committing knowledge to memory. Yet research on cognition is clear: knowledge in long-term memory is essential for reasoning, transfer, and the kind of flexible problem-solving that AI cannot replace (Risko et al., 2019). The belief that you don't need to remember anything because you can look it up is cognitively naive.

    These are not alarmist concerns. They are documented vulnerabilities that become more serious in an AI-rich environment. Schools must act deliberately to protect them.

    The AI Learning Paradox: Survey Evidence

    Sung (2026) surveyed 923 learners—students and professionals—on their experience using AI for learning and found a striking pattern he terms the "AI Learning Paradox." When first asked how helpful AI was for their learning, respondents averaged four out of five. But when asked to reflect on whether AI had actually improved their learning outcomes, the average dropped by roughly one point. Among students, the number rating AI's helpfulness at one or two out of five tripled; among professionals, it more than doubled (Sung, 2026). In short, AI feels more helpful than it actually is for learning—a perception gap that drives over-reliance.

    Sung attributes the gap to two mechanisms. First, the fluency–truth illusion: because large language models generate confident, well-structured output, users mistake eloquence for accuracy. LLMs operate on probability, not truth; the most probable token sequence often tracks common knowledge but diverges from ground truth in complex, nuanced, or cutting-edge domains (Sung, 2026). A 90 per cent overlap with truth may sound acceptable, but the remaining 10 per cent compounds over time into significant misconceptions—especially for learners who lack the expertise to detect subtle errors.

    Second, non-productive over-reliance: Sung distinguishes productive over-reliance (relying on a tool that saves time without hurting outcomes—using a phone instead of posting a letter) from non-productive over-reliance (relying on a tool that feels efficient but degrades the deeper outcome you actually need). For students, much learning requires building durable knowledge and flexible mental models, not merely completing tasks. When AI does the cognitive heavy lifting, the student's experience of efficiency is real, but the learning is hollow (Sung, 2026). This aligns with the cognitive-offloading research discussed later in this chapter: offloading can boost immediate performance while weakening later retention and transfer (Burnett & Richmond, 2026; Grinschgl, Papenmeier, & Meyerhoff, 2021).

    Bloom's Taxonomy as a Decision Framework

    Sung (2026) maps AI usefulness onto Bloom's taxonomy to give learners a practical decision rule. At the lower levels—remember and understand—AI is genuinely efficient: generating flashcards, simplifying language, producing summaries of already-learned material. These tasks are low-risk, low-complexity, and high-volume; delegating them to AI frees time and mental energy.

    At the higher levels—analyse, evaluate, and create—AI's value drops sharply and the risk of non-productive over-reliance rises. Analysis requires seeing relationships and spotting assumptions; evaluation demands judgment grounded in context, values, and priorities; creation at the highest level involves novel synthesis and intuitive leaps. These are precisely the capacities that employers will increasingly expect of humans, because AI already handles the lower-order tasks millions of times faster (Sung, 2026; World Economic Forum, 2025).

    ◆ The Sung Rule of Thumb

    The less important the thinking process, the more you should delegate to AI. The more important the thinking process—analysing, building mental models, synthesising a map—do that yourself. Every time you offload higher-order thinking to AI, you rob yourself of an opportunity to get better at the skill that will matter most in your career (Sung, 2026).

    This framework resonates with the broader argument of this chapter: AI-first systems make sense for efficiency, but human-first outcomes must govern which cognitive work remains with the learner. Sung's survey data provide empirical grounding for a principle the chapter has so far argued on theoretical terms.

    The Case for AI-First Systems Strategy

    Despite the risks outlined above, there is a strong case for what we might call an "AI-first systems strategy"—deliberately reorganizing school workflows, administrative processes, and teaching preparation to leverage AI for efficiency and knowledge diffusion.

    Evidence for Productivity Gains

    Recent empirical work supports the possibility of real productivity gains. A field study by Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023) found that access to a generative AI assistant increased customer-support agent productivity by roughly 14% on average, with larger gains for less-experienced workers. This suggests AI can function as a knowledge-diffusion system, not only as a task-automation tool. New teachers, teachers new to a subject, teachers with less experience—all benefited more, implying AI can help equalize capability across a team.

    Adoption is moving quickly. Survey evidence from Bick, Blandin, and Deming (2025) shows rapid population and workplace adoption of generative AI, with meaningful shares of work hours already AI-assisted. Industry leaders describe the shift as transformative at every level of the economy (Kamath, 2026). This is not a niche development; it is becoming normal infrastructure.

    Within schools specifically, AI can reduce administrative and routine work: generating lesson planning scaffolds, creating differentiated materials, drafting class communications, organizing data on student progress. These are low-value friction layers that consume teacher time without directly improving learning. Removing them frees time for higher-value work: individualized feedback, challenging conversations with students, collaborative lesson design, and reflection on pedagogy.

    Why AI-First Becomes Necessary

    If schools do not adopt AI-first systems strategically, they risk several problems. Persistent administrative drag reduces time for high-value teaching and mentoring. Schools that systematize AI use will develop capability advantages over those that don't. In the absence of clear school policy, "shadow AI" practices emerge—students and teachers using AI informally with weak quality control and weak ethics guidance. This is worse than thoughtful adoption.

    Importantly, students will use AI regardless. The educational question is not whether AI is present in schools, but whether schools acknowledge it, teach about it, and guide its use—or whether it operates in shadows, with weak oversight and without educational purpose.

    The Case for Human-First Outcomes

    An AI-first systems strategy is necessary for practical effectiveness, but it is dangerous if not explicitly designed with human-first outcomes. The research on cognitive offloading reveals a recurring pattern: external tools can improve immediate task performance while weakening later memory formation, transfer, and judgment when users offload without a deliberate learning goal.

    This is not an argument against using technology. It is an argument against using technology thoughtlessly. Meta-analytic evidence on cognitive offloading is nuanced: offloading can be beneficial and can reduce performance variability, but effects depend on context—whether offloading is forced or chosen, task type, and design. The right question is not "Offloading good or bad?" but "What should be offloaded, when, and under what learning conditions?"

    A second pressure comes from attention economics. In 2017, Netflix's CEO stated the company competes "with sleep, on the margin." This captures a broader incentive landscape where many systems optimize for time capture and engagement, not development. If an AI-first strategy is deployed inside that incentive logic, time saved by AI can drift toward passive consumption rather than deeper work.

    Yet the core concern is human formation. If AI frees time from routine work, what becomes possible during that freed time? Without an answer, schools move toward a soft dystopia of convenience, dependency, and reduced agency. With a clear answer—time freed by AI becomes time for dialogue, mentorship, deep projects, reflection, community service—AI-first becomes a lever for human formation.

    ⚠ AI-First Must Be Human-First

    An AI-first systems strategy only makes sense if it is coupled with an explicit, defended human-first outcome framework. The framework answers: What competencies and capacities must remain distinctly human? What does the school do with the time that AI frees? How do you prevent saved time from becoming consumed time? What does it mean for your school to form capable, responsible, thinking persons in an AI-augmented world?

    Practical Principles for AI in Education

    How should schools integrate AI in ways that strengthen competency development and protect human capability?

    1. AI as Tool, Not Replacement for Thinking

    Use AI to enhance the cognitive processes that matter, not bypass them. A good use: students generate a first draft with AI, then critically evaluate and revise it, engaging critical thinking and metacognition. A bad use: students submit AI output as their own work without engagement.

    Teachers should ask: Does this AI use deepen learning, or does it substitute for learning? If a student uses AI to generate a first draft and then reflects critically on it, learning might deepen. If a student uses AI to avoid thinking about the problem, learning likely suffers.

    2. Teach AI Literacy as Part of Information Literacy

    Students need to understand:

    • How AI generates output (statistical patterns, not genuine understanding)
    • Why AI "hallucinates" (confabulates plausible-sounding false information)
    • How to verify AI-generated claims against independent sources
    • The ethical dimensions of AI use (attribution, bias, environmental costs, labor implications)

    This is not separate from information literacy; it is an evolution of it (Stanford Report, 2025; UNESCO, 2023). The competency is understanding when to trust a source and when to be skeptical. AI raises the stakes and the sophistication of that competency.

    3. Protect Spaces for AI-Free Thinking

    Some cognitive activities should be deliberately AI-free. Thinking tasks on a whiteboard with no devices, sustained silent reading of print texts, face-to-face collaborative problem-solving, and handwritten reflection all deserve protection. These are not quaint traditions; they are specific cognitive activities that develop specific competencies.

    A school might establish "AI-free blocks" in the day or in specific assignments: the first draft must be the student's own thinking, without AI assistance. This forces engagement with the problem and protects the struggle that produces learning.

    4. Redesign Assessment for the AI Era

    If AI can produce a competent essay in seconds, take-home essays are no longer valid assessments of writing ability. Assessment must adapt:

    • In-class writing and problem-solving (with or without AI, but observed)
    • Process portfolios showing drafts, revision, and thinking
    • Oral defences and presentations where students explain and justify their work
    • Thinking routine documentation showing explicit reasoning
    • Formative assessment that tracks where students are in real time

    Assessment that only looks at final product is vulnerable to AI use obscuring the student's actual capability. Assessment that includes process reveals genuine learning and develops metacognition.

    5. Frame AI Within Dannelse

    Help students reflect on deeper questions:

    • "What kind of thinker am I becoming if I outsource this to AI?"
    • "When does using AI help me learn, and when does it prevent me from learning?"
    • "What is my responsibility when I use AI-generated content?"

    These are dannelse questions: they ask not just about the technical use of AI, but about formation, responsibility, and the kind of person the student is becoming. This frame elevates AI discussion from tool use to ethical engagement with technology.

    The Opportunity and the Risk

    Used well, AI can actually strengthen competency education. It makes the case for human competencies more visible and urgent. It allows schools to provide personalized practice and feedback at scale. It can free class time for the higher-order thinking, collaboration, and creativity that AI cannot replace. It generates rich source material for critical evaluation exercises.

    The risk is not AI itself. The risk is the failure to be intentional about how it is integrated. If schools adopt AI without deliberate protective structures, without attention to which competencies must remain human, and without a vision of human formation, they risk deskilling and disengaging learners. The opportunity and the danger are equally real.

    ? For Reflection

    What would a school that is genuinely AI-first and genuinely human-first look like? How would it organize its time, its assessment, its curriculum? What would students be learning, and what would they be doing that AI could not do?
