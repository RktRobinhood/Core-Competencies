    Chapter 23 — Teaching Techniques and Thinking Routines

    ◉ Overview

    Where Rosenshine's principles specify lesson architecture—review, input, guided practice, independence—this chapter addresses the micro-techniques and routines that operationalize that architecture. Doug Lemov's Teach Like a Champion framework provides high-frequency, low-ambiguity classroom techniques that stabilize instructional quality, while Visible Thinking routines from Harvard's Project Zero provide protocols that make student thinking visible and scaffold metacognitive reflection. Together, these frameworks enable teachers to execute well-designed lessons with consistency and to engage students in the kind of deep thinking that BTC and Rosenshine's principles aim to create.

    Part A: Teach Like a Champion Techniques

    Doug Lemov's Teach Like a Champion (TLAC) framework provides a curated collection of classroom techniques that research has associated with high student achievement and engagement (Lemov, 2021). The framework is built on the premise that teaching is a craft that can be systematized through technique, that consistency matters, and that high-ambiguity ("teach dynamically according to your style") often results in high variability in student experience across classrooms.

    The TLAC Architecture

    The TLAC 3.0 framework organizes techniques around five domains that research associates with effective instruction (Lemov, 2021; Lemov, McCleary, Solomon, & Woolway, 2023): checking for understanding, raising academic expectations, increasing the ratio of student cognitive work, improving discussion quality, and maintaining productive behaviour.

    Checking for Understanding is elevated as a central practice. In typical classrooms, understanding is assumed when teachers see no hands raised. In reality, many students are confused but unwilling to reveal it. TLAC techniques for checking include:

    Cold-calling and its variants ensure that all students, not just volunteers, respond to questions. Randomisation (e.g., drawing names) removes the social anxiety of being "put on the spot" unpredictably and raises the expectation that everyone will participate. Following up with probing questions ("How did you solve it?") or "no opt out" (ensuring the student gets the right answer through questioning, not allowing them to say "I don't know") keeps the cognitive demand high.

    Whole-class response checks use simultaneous responses (hand signals, white cards with answers, fist-to-five on a scale of confidence) so the teacher sees everyone's answer at once rather than listening to one student while inferring that the others understand. This technique surfaces misconceptions held by half the class that would otherwise remain hidden.

    Turn-and-talk pairs students for structured brief conversations about a prompt or question. This technique increases participation (every student talks, not just one per question) and provides cognitive processing time before whole-class discussion. The accountability is raised by asking pairs to report out or requiring written follow-up.

    Raising Academic Expectations involves techniques that keep the cognitive demand high and prevent the "dumbing down" of tasks for students perceived as lower-ability. Techniques include:

    Repeat, rephrase, and request is a response to student non-response or off-task behaviour. Rather than accepting a low-effort response or moving on, the teacher repeats the question, rephrases it, and requests a complete answer. This is not harsh; it is communicated matter-of-factly. The message is: "I expect you to engage fully."

    Accountable talk uses sentence stems and language frames that require students to use evidence and reasoning, not just assertion. Instead of "I think X," students say, "The evidence for X is… because…" Over time, this language becomes internalized, and students spontaneously produce reasoned explanations.

    Increasing Student Cognitive Work is the practice of designing tasks and routines that maximize the proportion of mental effort spent by students rather than by the teacher. Rather than the teacher explaining everything and students listening, the balance is shifted toward student thinking, problem-solving, and explanation. Techniques include:

    Pre-teaching of vocabulary or concepts in brief, targeted ways before a complex task ensures that students' cognitive capacity is devoted to the main learning (solving the problem) rather than deciphering unfamiliar terms.

    Intentional wait time after asking questions (3–5 seconds of silence) gives students time to think and formulate responses rather than racing to respond. This particularly benefits students who process more slowly.

    Equity of talk ensures that all students contribute, not just the fastest or most confident. This is operationalized through cold-call, turn-and-talk, and structured response formats.

    Improving Discussion Quality involves techniques for moving beyond surface-level exchanges to genuine discourse. Stretch the answer uses follow-up questions that deepen reasoning: "Why did you do it that way?" "Can you give me an example?" "How does this connect to what we learned yesterday?" These follow-ups signal that the teacher is interested in understanding, not just in correct answers.

    Re-voice and praise precisely involves the teacher re-stating a student's idea (sometimes more precisely or with additional clarity) to make it more visible to the class, and praising specific cognitive moves ("You made a prediction based on the pattern—that's good scientific thinking") rather than generic praise ("Good job").

    TLAC in Integrated Lesson Design

    These techniques are most powerful when integrated into Rosenshine's lesson structure. During the review phase, cold-call ensures all students retrieve prior learning. During input and modeling, the teacher uses think-aloud, not just narration. During guided practice, the teacher checks for understanding constantly using whole-class response checks and turn-and-talk, adjusting pace and support based on what is revealed. During independent practice, students work with the expectation that they will be accountable for explaining their thinking.

    ℹ Key Concept — Technique as Stability, Not Prescription

    TLAC techniques are tools for consistency, not rigid formulas. A teacher who has mastered cold-call, turn-and-talk, and whole-class response checks will have reliably higher participation and faster misconception detection than a teacher who relies on "organic discussion" or volunteers only. However, the techniques are deployed with judgment: a teacher might cold-call individuals in mathematics (where procedures can be checked quickly) but use more turn-and-talk in history (where discussion is dialogic). The techniques stabilise execution quality while allowing flexibility in application.

    Part B: Visible Thinking Routines

    Visible Thinking is a complementary approach developed by Harvard's Project Zero (Project Zero, n.d.) that emphasizes making student thinking explicit through structured protocols and conversations. While TLAC techniques operate at the moment-to-moment level (how to check understanding, how to question), Visible Thinking routines provide longer, multi-step structures for specific thinking moves.

    The Purpose and Design of Thinking Routines

    Thinking routines are short (typically 10–30 minutes), repeatable protocols that scaffold and make visible specific cognitive processes (Ritchhart, Church, & Morrison, 2011). They share common features: they are easy to learn, they can be used consistently across subjects, and they scaffold metacognition without requiring abstract discussion of "metacognition." Instead of a lesson on "how to think about your thinking," a routine like "I Used to Think… Now I Think…" makes the student's cognitive change visible and creates a record of learning.

    Ritchhart, Church, and Morrison (2011) describe routines as "the micro-moves that shape the culture of the classroom." When routines are used consistently, the classroom gradually becomes a place where explicit thinking and articulation of reasoning are normalised (Ritchhart, 2015; Salmon, 2008). Over time, students internalise the thinking moves and begin to apply them spontaneously.

    High-Impact Routines

    Several routines are particularly useful in secondary settings:

    See-Think-Wonder is an observation and reasoning protocol with three prompts: "What do you see?" (careful observation without interpretation), "What do you think is going on?" (interpretation and reasoning), "What does it make you wonder?" (curiosity and question-generation). This routine is useful at the beginning of a unit or when examining data, primary sources, or artwork. It surfaces diverse interpretations and ensures that observation precedes hasty interpretation.

    Think-Puzzle-Explore serves a similar introductory function but emphasises misconception and difficulty. The prompts are: "What do you think you know about this topic?", "What puzzles you about it?", "How might you explore those puzzles?" This routine activates prior knowledge (including misconceptions, which can be addressed), identifies learning edges, and engages students in planning inquiry.

    Claim-Support-Question scaffolds argumentation: "What claim can you make?", "What evidence supports it?", "What questions remain?" This routine makes the structure of evidence-based reasoning visible and can be used across history, science, and language arts. Students learn that a claim alone is not argument; evidence must be marshalled and reasoning must be explicit.

    Circle of Viewpoints develops perspective-taking: students articulate a topic "from the perspective of…" and complete "I think… because…" and "A question I have from this perspective is…" This routine is particularly powerful in humanities and social sciences for developing empathy and understanding of different stakeholder positions. It also aligns with democratic dannelse by requiring students to genuinely consider viewpoints different from their own.

    I Used to Think… Now I Think… makes conceptual change visible. Students complete both stems, reflecting on what they believed before and what they understand now. This routine is powerful for metacognition: students track their own learning and make the change in understanding explicit. It also serves as formative assessment for the teacher, revealing which misconceptions have been addressed and which persist.

    Connect-Extend-Challenge consolidates learning: "How does this connect to what you already know?", "What new ideas extend your thinking?", "What is still challenging or confusing?" This routine helps students integrate new learning into existing knowledge structures, identify what is different or novel, and flag gaps in understanding.

    3-2-1 (Creative Hunt) uses different prompts depending on context but typically captures three things that interested the learner, two questions they have, and one metaphor or analogy for what they learned. This routine is more open-ended and allows for creative response while still maintaining structure.

    Integration with TLAC and Rosenshine

    Visible Thinking routines can be embedded within lessons structured according to Rosenshine's principles. After input and modeling, a routine like Claim-Support-Question can scaffold guided practice. After independent practice, I Used to Think… Now I Think… can serve as checking for understanding. Think-Puzzle-Explore can launch a lesson's review and problem-setting. The routines provide the specific thinking scaffolds that Rosenshine's architecture allows time for.

    Similarly, Visible Thinking routines operationalise some of TLAC's emphasis on discussion quality and student cognitive work. Rather than a generic "discuss," the routine gives students specific thinking prompts and a structure that ensures all students engage (not just the most verbal), that thinking is made explicit (rather than staying implicit), and that the teacher can assess understanding (because thinking is visible).

    » In Practice

    A Year 9 history class studies the Second World War. The teacher uses Circle of Viewpoints with four perspectives: a British soldier, a German civilian, a Soviet official, and a Polish farmer. Each student takes one perspective and completes: "I am thinking of this topic from the perspective of… I think… because… A question I have from this perspective is…" Students then share their responses. The routine makes visible the diversity of experiences and the way that historical understanding shifts depending on perspective. It scaffolds empathy and complicates narrative simplification. The thinking is made visible (each student articulates reasoning), all students participate (the structure ensures it), and the teacher assesses understanding (what do their responses reveal about grasp of context and evidence?).

    Alignment: When Routines and Techniques Work Together

    The combination of TLAC techniques and Visible Thinking routines creates a powerful instructional presence. TLAC techniques ensure consistent checking for understanding and high participation in each moment. Visible Thinking routines scaffold specific thinking moves and make understanding visible over longer time scales. Together, they operationalise the macro-architecture of Rosenshine's principles through micro-practices that are reliable and transferable across contexts.

    In a single lesson, a teacher might: - Open with cold-call on yesterday's key idea (TLAC check + Rosenshine review) - Model a new concept with think-aloud (Rosenshine principle 4) - Use See-Think-Wonder to examine a related image or problem (Visible Thinking routine) - Conduct guided practice with turn-and-talk and cold-call (TLAC techniques + Rosenshine principle 5) - Close with I Used to Think… Now I Think… (Visible Thinking routine + formative assessment)

    This integration ensures that instruction is both architecturally sound (Rosenshine) and executed with consistency and clarity (TLAC) while making student thinking visible and explicit (Visible Thinking).

    Guardrails and Adaptive Implementation

    Techniques and routines require adaptation to context. A teacher in a setting with high anxiety and low prior engagement might move slowly through TLAC techniques, ensuring they feel safe before cold-calling. A teacher in a setting with high prior engagement might layer techniques more densely. A school with strong disciplinary systems can use TLAC's high-structure techniques; a school with different cultural norms might adapt the tone while preserving the function (e.g., no opt out survives cultural translation but the specific phrasing might change).

    Similarly, Visible Thinking routines work best when students are taught how to use them. The first use of a routine might be slow and supported; the tenth use should be rapid and student-initiated. Expecting students to use an unfamiliar routine without modeling is ineffective.

    TOK-Specific Resources: Where the Core Meets the Classroom

    The techniques and routines described above are domain-general — they work in any classroom. But the expanded TOK programme this textbook envisions needs resources specifically designed for TOK's distinctive epistemological focus. A growing ecosystem of TOK teacher websites offers lesson plans, activities, and instructional frameworks that move away from traditional top-down Socratic teaching and give students real autonomy and active practice of core skills. The most useful of these, reviewed below, provide a bridge between the theory in this textbook and the reality of a TOK classroom.

    TOK Resource (tokresource.org) is a non-commercial, ad-free site that offers hands-on activities and discussion formats specifically designed for student-centred learning. Its emphasis on "counterintuitive stunts" — engaging opening provocations that destabilise assumptions and launch purposeful discussion — aligns directly with Kapur's productive failure principle (see Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties). The site also provides a "Starting TOK" guide that scaffolds the transition from teacher-led to student-led discussion.

    TOK 2022 (tok2022.com) provides over 100 classroom activities aligned with the latest IB specifications, freely available. Activities are designed around inquiry rather than transmission: students investigate knowledge questions through structured experiences rather than being told what the "correct" epistemological position is. This inquiry-based approach embodies the mentor-curator model of teaching described in Ch-22-Principles-of-Effective-Instruction.

    theoryofknowledge.net offers both student-facing resources and professional development for TOK teachers, including online and in-person training. Its emphasis on helping students become "authentic critical thinkers" — rather than merely learning to perform critical thinking for assessment — connects to the autonomy research in Ch-19-Motivation-Autonomy-and-the-Adult-Learner.

    Several structural innovations from these resources are worth highlighting. Knowledge fairs, where students create exhibits demonstrating how evidence works differently across disciplines, transform passive consumers of epistemology into active investigators. Podcast projects, where students analyse media coverage through TOK frameworks, develop critical thinking and communication skills simultaneously. Digital escape rooms, requiring students to apply TOK concepts through collaborative problem-solving, embed thinking routines in genuinely engaging formats. Collaborative image webs, where students physically map interconnections among TOK concepts, make abstract relationships concrete and visible — a direct application of the "making thinking visible" principle from Project Zero (Ritchhart, Church, & Morrison, 2011).

    ◆ Design Principle — Student Ownership Over Epistemological Inquiry

    The most effective TOK activities share a common feature: they position the student as the investigator of knowledge, not the recipient of epistemological conclusions. When a student designs their own knowledge question, selects their own real-life situation, and builds their own argument about how knowledge works in that context, they are practising exactly the autonomy, critical thinking, and metacognition that the expanded core is designed to develop. The teacher's role is to provide the frameworks, pose the provocative questions, and ensure rigour — not to deliver the answers.

    Connections

    These techniques and routines operationalise the principles discussed in Ch-21-Building-Thinking-Classrooms and Ch-22-Principles-of-Effective-Instruction.
