    Chapter 29a — Dannelse: Theory and Research Foundations

    ◉ Overview

    Dannelse is far more than skills training or academic achievement. It is a philosophical tradition from Humboldt, Grundtvig, and Klafki that treats education as the formation of the whole person—intellectually, morally, socially, and civically. Understanding dannelse is essential for designing a course on core competencies in a Danish school, because it answers the deepest question: not just which skills matter, but why they matter and who learners are becoming through their education.

    What Dannelse Means

    The Danish term dannelse resists precise translation. The closest English equivalents are "formation" or "character education," but these understate its scope. The German Bildung—from which dannelse derives—means the process of self-cultivation, self-realization, and integration into culture and society (Klafki, 2001). In the Nordic tradition, dannelse encompasses five interrelated dimensions.

    Self-formation addresses the development of understanding oneself: one's strengths, values, weaknesses, and potential. A student engaged in self-formation learns not only what they can do, but who they are becoming and what they care about. This goes beyond personality assessment or the popular VIA Character Strengths framework (Peterson & Seligman, 2004), though the latter is a useful tool. Self-formation is the deeper work of identity construction through reflection, challenge, and responsible decision-making.

    Social formation involves learning to live well with others. It includes the cultivation of empathy, the ability to participate meaningfully in fællesskab (community), and the skills to navigate conflict and difference with respect. In a Danish school context, social formation is embedded in classroom structures—visibly random groups, collaborative projects, and deliberate attention to group dynamics—that require students to work across differences regularly.

    Democratic formation develops informed, active citizens. It means understanding democratic values and processes, recognizing one's rights and responsibilities, and preparing to participate in civic life. The Danish Folkeskole Act explicitly names this: schools shall prepare pupils "to participate, demonstrate mutual responsibility and understand their rights and duties in a free and democratic society."

    Cultural formation is engagement with heritage: language, history, literature, and the arts. It means developing the ability to appreciate, interpret, and contribute to cultural expression. In Denmark, this includes understanding Danish history and culture alongside respectful engagement with other cultural traditions.

    Critical formation develops the capacity for independent, critical thought—the ability to question, analyze, and form reasoned judgments. This is not critical thinking in the narrow sense of logic puzzles; it is the formation of a critical consciousness that allows learners to evaluate claims, examine assumptions, and refuse passive reception of ideas.

    ℹ Key Concept — Dannelse

    Dannelse is the holistic formation of the person across intellectual, moral, social, civic, and cultural dimensions. It is grounded in the belief that education should develop self-determination (ability to direct one's own life), participation (ability to engage with others and society), and solidarity (commitment to justice and common good) (Klafki, 2001; von Oettingen, 2018). Dannelse is not a set of skills to be checked off, but a continuous process of becoming a more thoughtful, capable, and responsible person.

    Dannelse in Danish Law and Educational Policy

    The philosophical concept of dannelse is not merely aspirational in Danish education. It is embedded in law and curriculum.

    The opening of the Folkeskole Act (Folkeskoleloven) states that the school shall, in cooperation with parents, "give the pupils knowledge and skills that prepare them for further education and give them the desire to learn more, familiarise them with Danish culture and history, give them an understanding of other countries and cultures, contribute to their understanding of man's interaction with nature, and promote the all-round development of the individual pupil" (UVM, Ministry of Children and Education). The phrase "all-round development" is a direct reference to dannelse.

    More explicitly, the Act requires that "The Folkeskole is to prepare the pupils to be able to participate, demonstrate mutual responsibility and understand their rights and duties in a free and democratic society." This is democratic formation as law.

    The Fælles Mål (Common Objectives) system, revised most recently in 2014 (Danish Ministry of Education, 2014), operationalizes dannelse by organizing learning around competency goals (kompetencemål) that embed formation within subject-specific learning. Each subject's goals include not just what students should know and be able to do, but how they should grow in judgment, values, and character. This is the bridge between philosophy and practice: dannelse becomes visible in the competencies students are expected to develop in Danish, Mathematics, Science, and every other subject.

    » In Practice

    In a Danish gymnasium classroom, a student studying Climate Literacy might develop knowledge about atmospheric science and carbon cycles. But within the dannelse framework, the same course also cultivates ethical formation (understanding responsibility to future generations), civic formation (engaging with climate policy and activism), and critical formation (evaluating competing claims about climate risk and response). The competency is not technical; it is the formation of a responsible citizen.

    Karakterdannelse: Character Strengths in Danish Context

    In recent years, the term karakterdannelse (character formation) has gained prominence in Danish schools, partly through international influence and partly through adapted Danish research. The framework gaining most traction is the VIA Character Strengths model (Peterson & Seligman, 2004), which identifies 24 strengths grouped under six virtues: wisdom, courage, humanity, justice, temperance, and transcendence.

    Danish researchers, notably Mette Marie Ledertoug at Aarhus University, have investigated strength-based learning (styrkebaseret læring) in Danish schools. Her doctoral research found that working with character strengths improved students' engagement, well-being, and academic motivation (Ledertoug, 2019). The Centre for Youth Research (CEFU) extended this work to vocational education, identifying seven key character strengths relevant to vocational success and adapted for the Danish context (CEFU, 2020).

    What makes this relevant to competency education is that strengths are not fixed traits but developable capacities. A student strong in curiosity can grow in love of learning. A student with emerging leadership can develop through deliberate practice in collaborative settings. When dannelse meets character strengths, the result is a growth-oriented, asset-based approach to human development. Instead of asking "What is wrong with this student?" the dannelse framework asks "What strengths does this student have, and how can we help them grow?"

    The institutional home for much of this work in Denmark is the Center for Karakterdannelse (https://karakterdannelse.dk), a collaborative initiative built from the ground up with ten schools, supported by ten subject experts and ten professional organisations functioning as development centres for character education. Through its development programmes, teachers and educators are both trained in character education and contribute to developing professional understanding, character-building school culture, quality materials, and concrete tools for integrating karakterdannelse into daily school life. The Centre provides practical resources — including classroom tools (værktøjer) and research summaries (viden) — that bridge the gap between philosophical tradition and classroom implementation.

    Complementing this work on character is the research programme of Ane Qvortrup, which provides the empirical foundation that connects dannelse's philosophical aspirations to measurable outcomes in Danish classrooms.

    ? For Reflection

    How does character strength language change the conversation in a school? If a teacher sees a student as "strong in bravery" or "developing persistence," rather than "disruptive" or "lazy," how does that shift the educational relationship and what becomes possible?

    Ane Qvortrup: The Empirical Foundation for Dannelse in Practice

    Ane Qvortrup — Professor of Educational Research at the University of Southern Denmark and a leading figure in Danish secondary education research — provides a body of work that bridges the gap between dannelse as a philosophical tradition and dannelse as a set of actionable strategies for schools. Her research programme spans four interconnected domains: the TrivselsLUP study of student wellbeing, a systems-theoretical framework for understanding inclusion, a didactic theory of learning as both means and aims, and ongoing work on the study environment and student engagement. Together, these contributions offer the most comprehensive empirical grounding available for the kind of holistic, student-centred education that this textbook advocates.

    TrivselsLUP: Three Dimensions of Student Wellbeing

    The TrivselsLUP study (Trivsels- og Læringsundersøgelse i Praksis — Wellbeing and Learning Survey in Practice), conducted by Qvortrup and Eva Lykkegaard at the Center for Secondary and Vocational Education Research in collaboration with the Praxis Foundation, is one of the largest and most systematic investigations of student wellbeing in Danish education. Across two annual rounds, the study surveyed more than 12,000 students at over 100 upper secondary institutions (ungdomsuddannelser) — including STX, HHX, HTX, HF, and EUX programmes — creating a dataset of remarkable breadth and depth (Qvortrup & Lykkegaard, 2023).

    The study's central contribution is its differentiation of wellbeing into three distinct dimensions. Personal wellbeing (personlig trivsel) encompasses individuals' tendency to be satisfied with everyday life, experience life as good, feel confident, and think positively. Social wellbeing (social trivsel) refers to the experience of belonging to a social group or community where the individual's participation and engagement are recognised and valued. Academic wellbeing (faglig trivsel) — and this is where the study's findings become most significant for education — refers to the experience of feeling glad, motivated, and engaged in academic work.

    ℹ Key Concept — The Trivsel Gap

    The TrivselsLUP data reveals a striking asymmetry: while more than 80 per cent of Danish upper secondary students report high personal and social wellbeing (scoring just under 4 on a five-point scale), academic wellbeing consistently lags behind at 3.24. This trivsel gap — the difference between how well students feel socially and how well they feel about their learning — is the most actionable finding of the study. It means that a student may feel perfectly comfortable in school, have friends, and enjoy the social dimension of student life, yet simultaneously feel disengaged, unmotivated, or lost in their academic work. Schools that attend only to social wellbeing are addressing only part of the picture (Qvortrup & Lykkegaard, 2023; 2024).

    The practical implications of this finding are profound. If academic wellbeing is a distinct dimension — not merely a by-product of social comfort — then it requires its own deliberate strategies. The TrivselsLUP research identifies several factors that predict higher academic wellbeing: students' self-efficacy (belief in their own ability to master academic tasks), the experience of meaningful learning (perceiving homework and classwork as connected to purposes the student values), the quality of teacher-student relationships (feeling that the teacher knows, cares about, and supports the individual student), and the student's attitude toward academic challenge (viewing difficulty as an expected part of learning rather than as evidence of inadequacy).

    These findings connect directly to the cognitive science explored in Part III of this textbook. Self-efficacy maps onto the growth mindset research in Ch-16-Growth-Mindset. Meaningful learning corresponds to the intrinsic motivation research in Ch-19-Motivation-Autonomy-and-the-Adult-Learner. Teacher-student relationships connect to the feedback research in Ch-24-Effective-Feedback. And the attitude toward academic challenge is precisely the construct explored in Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties — the understanding that difficulty is the mechanism of deep learning, not its enemy.

    » In Practice — Addressing the Trivsel Gap

    A gymnasium identifies through its annual wellbeing survey that students report high social wellbeing but low academic engagement. Rather than adding social activities, the school focuses on the academic dimension: teachers explicitly teach study strategies based on retrieval practice and spaced learning (see Ch-13-Retrieval-Practice-and-Spaced-Learning), reframe academic difficulty as productive struggle through classroom conversations about how learning works (see Ch-17-Productive-Struggle-and-Desirable-Difficulties), and restructure homework to make its purpose visible and connected to students' own questions. After one semester, students report increased feelings of competence and academic motivation — not because the work became easier, but because students understood why the difficulty mattered and felt supported in navigating it.

    Dimensions of Inclusion: A Systems-Theoretical Framework

    Qvortrup's work on inclusion provides a second major contribution to educational practice. Drawing on Niklas Luhmann's social systems theory, Qvortrup and Qvortrup (2018) argue that the concept of inclusion in education has been insufficiently differentiated — that simply saying a student is "included" or "excluded" misses the complexity of how belonging actually works in school settings.

    Their framework identifies three analytical dimensions. The first dimension distinguishes levels of inclusion: a student may be physically present in a classroom (spatial inclusion), socially connected to peers (relational inclusion), and academically engaged with the learning (didactic inclusion) — or may be present at one level while excluded at another. The second dimension identifies types of social communities from which a student may be included or excluded: the class as an instructional unit, the informal peer community of the schoolyard, bilateral friendships, teacher-student relationships, and the wider school culture. The third dimension concerns degrees — inclusion is not binary but exists on a continuum, and a student's position on that continuum may differ across communities and over time.

    ℹ Key Concept — Qvortrup's Three Dimensions of Inclusion

    Inclusion in education is not a single state but a multi-dimensional phenomenon. A student may be spatially included (physically present in the classroom) but relationally excluded (lacking meaningful connections to peers) and didactically excluded (unable to access the learning at their level). Effective inclusive practice requires attending to all three dimensions and to the multiple social communities in which inclusion plays out — not just the classroom but the playground, the peer group, and the student-teacher relationship (Qvortrup & Qvortrup, 2018).

    This framework directly supports the pedagogical approaches advocated throughout this textbook. Building Thinking Classrooms (Ch-21-Building-Thinking-Classrooms) uses visibly random grouping precisely because it disrupts the fixed social hierarchies that produce relational exclusion. Collaborative learning structures (Ch-05-Collaboration-and-Teamwork) create the conditions for didactic inclusion by ensuring that all students have access to the thinking. And the emphasis on scaffolded challenge throughout Part III ensures that productive struggle remains within the zone of proximal development — preventing didactic exclusion through tasks that are either too easy (producing disengagement) or too hard (producing helplessness).

    The connection to dannelse is explicit in Qvortrup's work: true inclusion is not merely about removing barriers to participation but about ensuring that every student can engage in the process of formation — developing intellectually, socially, ethically, and civically. A student who is spatially present but didactically excluded is not merely missing out on content; they are being denied the opportunity for dannelse.

    Learning Between Means and Aims: Qvortrup's Didactic Theory

    Qvortrup's theoretical work on the nature of learning, developed most fully in Dealing with Conceptualisations of Learning: Learning Between Means and Aims in Theory and Practice (Qvortrup & Wiberg, 2017), provides a framework for understanding the dual nature of learning in educational contexts.

    The core insight is that learning operates simultaneously as means and as aim. As a means, learning is a process — something that happens over time, with a beginning, a trajectory, and (potentially) a transformation. As an aim, learning is an outcome — a form of expertise, competence, or understanding that the process is directed toward. In educational settings, both the means and the aims have normative and instrumental dimensions: we care not only about whether students learn but about how they learn (the process matters, not just the product) and why they learn (the purpose extends beyond the immediate task to the student's formation as a person).

    This framework addresses a tension that runs through contemporary education: the pull between measurable learning outcomes (aims) and the quality of the learning process (means). When schools focus exclusively on aims — test scores, competency demonstrations, grades — they risk treating learning as an instrumental process that matters only for its end product. When they focus exclusively on means — the experience of learning, the emotional quality of the classroom, the process of exploration — they risk losing rigour and direction. Qvortrup's framework insists on holding both in view: the aims give learning direction and accountability; the means give learning depth and meaning.

    For the expanded TOK curriculum, this dual perspective is essential. The additional 50 hours are not merely aimed at producing measurable competency gains (though they should produce these). They are equally aimed at transforming the process of learning itself — developing students who approach learning differently, who understand how their own cognition works, and who experience academic work as meaningful engagement rather than compliance. Qvortrup's framework validates this approach: when means and aims are held together, learning becomes a vehicle for dannelse rather than a purely instrumental activity.

    The Study Environment and Student Engagement

    Qvortrup's ongoing research programme at the Center for Secondary and Vocational Education Research (Center for Gymnasie- og Erhvervsuddannelsesforskning) extends her wellbeing work into a broader investigation of how study environments shape student engagement, retention, and development. Her research has examined how students' behaviours in the classroom can be understood as indicators of affective, behavioural, or cognitive engagement — or disengagement — depending on the instructional context (Qvortrup & Lykkegaard, 2023; 2024).

    This work connects to a critical practical question: how do we know when students are genuinely engaged in deep learning, and how do we distinguish genuine engagement from mere compliance? A student who appears busy — taking notes, completing tasks, sitting quietly — may be behaviourally engaged but cognitively disengaged. A student who appears restless or distracted may in fact be grappling with a genuinely difficult idea. Qvortrup's research reminds educators that engagement is multi-dimensional and context-dependent — the same behaviour can signify engagement or disengagement depending on what the student is being asked to do and how they experience the task.

    Her work also addresses the problem of dropout (frafald) in Danish upper secondary education — the phenomenon of students leaving their programme before completion. Qvortrup's research frames dropout not as a single event but as a process of progressive disengagement that unfolds across the three wellbeing dimensions: first academic wellbeing declines (the student feels lost in the work), then social wellbeing erodes (the student withdraws from peer relationships), and finally personal wellbeing suffers (the student no longer sees the point of being in school). This cascade model suggests that early intervention at the academic wellbeing level — helping students feel competent, connected to the material, and supported in their learning — may prevent the downstream social and personal consequences that ultimately lead to dropout.

    ⚠ From Research to Practice — Qvortrup's Central Message

    Across all of her work, Qvortrup's central message to practitioners can be distilled to a single principle: attend to the conditions of learning, not just its content. Academic wellbeing is a condition of learning, not a luxury. Inclusion is a condition of learning, not an administrative category. The quality of the learning process is a condition of meaningful outcomes, not a separate concern. And the study environment is a condition of student engagement, not a backdrop. When schools design for these conditions deliberately — with the same rigour they bring to curriculum design — students are more likely to stay, to engage, and to develop as whole persons. This is dannelse made operational.

    Dannelse and Learning Theory: Illeris's Contribution

    The Danish learning theorist Knud Illeris provides a crucial bridge between the dannelse philosophical tradition and contemporary learning science. His comprehensive learning theory identifies three dimensions that must all be present for deep learning:

    1. Content dimension: Knowledge, skills, understanding of the subject matter
    2. Incentive dimension: Motivation, emotion, volition—the "why" that drives engagement
    3. Interaction dimension: Action, communication, cooperation with others and the environment

    Most educational approaches emphasize content alone. Good teaching often attends to motivation and interaction separately. Illeris argues that transformative learning—learning that fundamentally changes the learner's identity, values, or understanding—requires all three dimensions working together (Illeris, 2003; 2014).

    This is where Illeris's work directly supports dannelse. Transformative learning, in his framework, is the deepest form of learning and the form most aligned with the dannelse tradition. When a student's identity shifts from "I'm bad at math" to "I'm someone who can struggle well and grow," that is transformative learning. When a student's values shift from "Only grades matter" to "I care about understanding," that is transformative learning. And when a student's relationship to others shifts from isolation to genuine collaboration, that is transformative learning.

    For course design on core competencies, the implication is clear: develop content knowledge about the competencies (e.g., what does critical thinking look like in different contexts?), create genuine motivation by showing why the competency matters for who students are becoming, and embed learning in interaction—collaborative tasks, feedback, peer learning—not solitary study.

    Dannelse Versus 21st-Century Skills: A Useful Complementarity

    It is common in contemporary education policy to hear about "21st-century skills"—frameworks like the Partnership for 21st Century Learning (Battelle for Kids, 2019), the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs (WEF, 2020), and the OECD's competency models (OECD, 2019). These frameworks emphasize skills like critical thinking, collaboration, creativity, and communication. They are genuinely valuable. But they differ from dannelse in important ways.

    Aspect Dannelse 21st-Century Skills
    Origin European philosophical tradition (Humboldt, Grundtvig, Klafki) Economic/policy discourse (P21, WEF, OECD)
    Purpose Holistic human formation, democratic citizenship Workforce preparation, economic competitiveness
    Scope Identity, values, civic life, culture, character Cognitive and interpersonal skills
    Assessment Resists standardized measurement; emphasizes reflection and judgment Seeks measurable, demonstrable outcomes
    Relationship to knowledge Knowledge is transformative; learning changes who you are Knowledge is foundational; skills build on knowledge

    The key insight is that these approaches are complementary, not contradictory. The 21st-century skills frameworks provide practical, teachable, measurable competencies. Dannelse provides the why—the deeper purpose that gives those skills meaning and direction. A course on core competencies in a Danish school should do both: teach the practical competencies (problem-solving, collaboration, communication) while grounding them in the dannelse vision of what kind of person students are becoming and what their responsibility is to themselves, others, and society.

    ⚠ Important Distinction

    It is possible to teach communication skills, for example, in a purely instrumental way: "Be a clear speaker because employers will hire you." This is not dannelse, though it may develop a competency. It is also possible to teach communication through a dannelse lens: "As you develop your voice as a speaker, you are learning to participate in democracy, to be heard, and to listen to others. What kind of speaker do you want to become, and what does that mean about how you communicate?" The same competency, taught through different philosophical frameworks, produces different learners.
