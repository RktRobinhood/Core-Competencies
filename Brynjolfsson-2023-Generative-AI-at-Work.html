    Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2023) — Generative AI at Work

    Full citation: Brynjolfsson, E., Li, D., & Raymond, L. R. (2023). Generative AI at work (NBER Working Paper No. 31161). National Bureau of Economic Research. https://doi.org/10.3386/w31161

    Note on citation variant: The master bibliography lists the authors as "Li, Y." and "Raymond, B." — the above reflects the version in the Ch-32 reference list. Verify against the NBER page before citing.

    Study Design

    A natural experiment with 5,179 customer support agents at a technology company. Workers received staggered access to a generative AI-based conversational assistant, allowing causal isolation of the tool's productivity effects.

    Key Findings

    Productivity (issues resolved per hour)

    • +14% average improvement across all agents
    • +34% improvement for novice and low-skilled workers
    • Minimal impact for experienced and highly skilled workers

    Additional Effects

    • Improved customer sentiment scores
    • Increased employee retention
    • Evidence that AI disseminates best practices of top workers, accelerating skill development for newer staff

    Interpretation for This Textbook

    The study supports the "AI as knowledge-diffusion system" argument in Ch-32. AI does not uniformly boost productivity — it specifically accelerates development for those furthest from best practice, functioning as a kind of institutional mentorship at scale.

    This aligns with the competency-education argument: skilled workers benefit less because they already possess the judgment, experience, and metacognition to work effectively. The gains cluster where human expertise is still developing.

    The finding also provides empirical grounding for the "AI-first systems strategy" section in Ch-32: AI's greatest organizational value may be in leveling capability across a workforce rather than maximizing output from already-capable workers.

    Quotable (paraphrase, as paper is paywalled)

    Access to the tool increases productivity, as measured by issues resolved per hour, by 14% on average. Novice and low-skilled workers benefit most — roughly 34% improvement — while experienced workers show minimal gains. The AI model appears to disseminate the best practices of more able workers, helping newer workers move down the experience curve.
